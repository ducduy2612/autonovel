# WORLD.MD — CANTAMURA
### The Second Son of the House of Bells
*Definitive Reference Document — All Worldbuilding Questions Resolve Here*

## Cosmology & History

### The Shape of the World

Cantamura does not believe in gods. It believes in ratios. The city's founding philosophy holds that the universe is organized by frequency — that matter, law, and obligation are all, at root, vibration. This is not metaphor. The city's founders meant it literally, and two hundred years of functional tonal law have given them no reason to revise the claim.

There are other cities. Cantamura trades with them, occasionally wars with them diplomatically, and regards their legal systems the way a trained surgeon regards folk remedies: with polite contempt. The wider world uses written contracts, sworn oaths, and wax seals. These are considered quaint.

---

### Timeline of Significant Events

**~200 years ago — The Founding**
Aldric Bellwright, a bronze-caster and amateur acoustician, settles in the natural amphitheater with a small guild of craftsmen fleeing a disputed land claim. The legal system in their home city had failed them — a forged document, a corrupt magistrate, a verdict that cost them everything. Aldric's solution is radical: make forgery acoustically impossible. He spends eleven years casting forty-seven bells from a bronze alloy he refuses to document, tuning each to a specific frequency using a method he calls *the Covenant Cast*. He composes a morning sequence — the Civic Pitch — and teaches it to his guild. The city that grows around the bells adopts the system because it works. Disputes resolve cleanly. Contracts hold. Within a generation, Cantamura is the most legally stable settlement in the region.

What Aldric actually composed into the bells is not in the official record. His personal journals were sealed by his son and have not been opened. The Bellwright family maintains this is out of respect for his privacy. The city's legal scholars maintain the journals are irrelevant, since the system is self-evident. Both positions are convenient.

**~160 years ago — The Codification**
The second generation of legal scholars formalizes Tonal Law into the system currently taught at the Cantamura Academy of Civic Harmony. They publish the *Interval Codex*, which specifies binding progressions, ratification chords, and release sequences. The Codex runs to four volumes. Volume Four — covering exceptional circumstances and contested frequencies — has forty-three blank pages at the back. Scholars call this "space for future additions." They have been saying this for a hundred and sixty years.

**~90 years ago — The Expansion Wars**
Cantamura attempts to export Tonal Law to three neighboring settlements by force, arguing that a legally superior system has an obligation to spread. The wars are brief and go badly. The neighboring settlements lack the acoustic geography to make the system work — their buildings don't carry sound the right way, their climate warps bronze differently. The Bellwright family is tasked with casting portable bell-sets for the occupied territories. Most of them fail within a decade. Cantamura withdraws, officially citing "cultural incompatibility." The occupied territories remember this differently. Trade relations with Veth, the closest of the three, remain cold.

**~40 years ago — The Muteborn Question**
A child is born in the Weavers' District who cannot produce vocal sound — not mute by injury, but constitutionally, completely without voice. The Academy spends six years debating whether this child can hold legal personhood under Tonal Law, since personhood requires the capacity to enter sung contracts. The child dies of fever before the debate resolves. The Academy records the case as "pending." The Weavers' District remembers the six years. They have not forgotten.

**~10 years ago — The Perin Bellwright Contract**
Cass is four years old. His older brother Perin, sixteen, signs a service contract with the House of Corda — a merchant family that supplies the Academy with its instrument stock. The contract is sung in proper form. The Academy registrar validates it. Perin enters service. The details of what he signed and why have never been discussed in the Bellwright household. Their father Eddan was present at the signing. That night, alone in the workshop, he reads Aldric Bellwright's sealed journals for the first time — all of them, cover to cover. His hands begin shaking within the week. He has never said whether the shaking comes from what he read or what he allowed to happen. Both explanations are probably true.

**Present Day**
Cass is fourteen, enrolled at the Academy, and in constant low-grade pain.

## Magic System

### Hard Rules — Tonal Law

**The Premise**
Sound at specific frequencies and intervals creates binding acoustic structures in the air between parties. These structures are not metaphysical. They are measurable. A trained acoustician with the right equipment can detect an active binding as a standing wave between two points. When the binding is honored, the wave persists. When it is broken, the wave collapses — and so does the legal protection it conferred.

**What Intervals Do**

*Ratification:* A major third sung simultaneously by two parties confirms agreement. Both parties must sing — a contract sung by one voice is legally a declaration, not a binding. The major third must be held for a full breath cycle, minimum four seconds. Shorter holds produce a provisional agreement, which expires at sundown.

*Binding:* A descending perfect fifth from the ratification note locks the terms. The fifth must resolve downward — an ascending fifth creates the opposite effect, a formal rejection. Song-brokers spend years drilling this distinction because the intervals are close enough that a nervous client can accidentally refuse a contract they intended to sign.

*Release:* A tritone followed by resolution to the octave dissolves a binding. Both parties must be present and willing. A release sung by one party alone is legally a breach, not a termination — the breaching party absorbs the full penalty terms of the original contract.

*Codicil:* An amendment to an existing contract requires the original binding note plus a minor seventh. The minor seventh is unstable by nature and must be resolved within the same breath, which limits how complex an amendment can be. Long amendments require multiple sessions.

**What Tonal Law Cannot Do**

It cannot compel. A binding sung under physical duress is legally void — but only if the duress can be proven, which requires a witness who was present and willing to testify, which requires them to sing their testimony under oath, which creates a second binding that exposes them to cross-examination. The system makes coercion difficult to prove by design. Whether this is intentional is a matter of ongoing academic debate.

It cannot bind the dead. Inheritance disputes go through a separate, written system that everyone agrees is inferior and no one has successfully replaced.

It cannot operate at a distance. Both parties must be within earshot of the binding note. The acoustic properties of Cantamura's amphitheater mean "earshot" covers most of the city center, which is why the morning Civic Pitch carries so far. In the outer districts, where the amphitheater's effect weakens, contracts require a licensed resonance chamber — a small, purpose-built room with tuned walls. Renting one costs more than most outer-district families make in a week.

It cannot account for a note that doesn't exist in its system.

**Costs and Limitations in Practice**

Song-brokers train for seven years minimum. A poorly composed contract — wrong interval, wrong resolution, wrong key — is not just invalid; it can create a partial binding that holds one party without holding the other. These are called *lame chords* in the trade, and they generate most of the Academy's litigation caseload.

Vocal illness is a legal emergency. A party who loses their voice mid-contract negotiation can have a binding voided on medical grounds, but only if they file within three days. After three days, the provisional agreement stands. Cantamura has a thriving trade in throat remedies. The best ones come from a woman named Senna Voss who operates out of the Tanner's Quarter and has never explained her methods.

The bronze bells set the civic pitch each morning. If the bells are wrong — cracked, mistuned, struck in the wrong sequence — every contract ratified that day is potentially contestable. This has happened twice in recorded history. Both times, the Bellwright family resolved the discrepancy within hours. Both times, the official record describes the cause as "atmospheric interference." The bells do not develop atmospheric interference. The Bellwright family knows this.

### Soft Magic — Cass's Gift

**What He Perceives**

When someone sings a lie — or sings a truth they have been coerced into performing — Cass hears a frequency beneath the sung note. It sits in the gap between F and F-sharp, a quarter-tone that no instrument in Cantamura is built to produce and no scale the Academy recognizes includes. There is no name for it. Cass calls it, privately, the *under-note*.

The under-note is not loud. It is precise. It cuts through ambient sound the way a single wrong instrument cuts through an orchestra — not by volume, but by wrongness. His ear finds it the way a tongue finds a split lip.

**The Pain**

The under-note causes physical pain proportional to the stakes of the lie. A polite social evasion — *I'm well, thank you* sung at a market stall — produces a faint pressure behind his left eye, gone in seconds. A coerced oath sung in a courtroom produces a needle of heat that sits behind his eye for hours. A binding contract sung by someone who has been threatened into it produces nausea and, once, a nosebleed he couldn't explain to his mother.

He has learned to manage small lies. He cannot manage large ones. He avoids courtrooms when he can.

**What He Cannot Do**

He cannot prove what he hears. The under-note leaves no acoustic residue, produces no measurable standing wave, and appears in no instrument's range. He cannot teach it. He cannot credential it. He cannot walk into a court and say *that man is lying* and have it mean anything, because the court singer confirmed the oath as valid in G-major and that is the record.

He cannot turn it off. He has tried earplugs (they muffle the surface note without touching the under-note), humming to himself (it helps with small lies, fails completely with large ones), and pressing his tongue to the roof of his mouth (a habit he learned from Perin, which does nothing except give him something to do with his body).

He cannot always identify *what* the lie is — only that one is present and roughly how much it matters to the person telling it. A man swearing falsely about where he was last night sounds different from a man swearing falsely about a debt. He is learning to read the texture, but slowly.

**The Deeper Layer**

The bells. Specifically, the morning Civic Pitch, which Cass has heard every day of his life. He has begun to hear something in it that he cannot describe to anyone. Not the under-note — something older, lower, a harmonic that runs beneath the sequence like a current beneath still water. It is not painful. It is almost the opposite of pain: a resonance in his chest that feels like a question he doesn't have the vocabulary to answer yet.

His father's hands shake worst on mornings when the Civic Pitch runs long.

### Societal Implications

**Governance**
Cantamura's court system requires every verdict to be sung into the record by a licensed Court Singer — a specialist who composes the verdict in real time, setting it in the correct key and interval to make it legally binding. Court Singers are the highest-paid legal professionals in the city. They are also the most politically exposed: a Court Singer who composes a verdict in the wrong key, accidentally or otherwise, can nullify years of litigation. There are four Court Singers currently operating in Cantamura. Two are related by marriage to the House of Corda. A third, Dav Sorn, is independent but cautious. The fourth is Vella Ashvale, whose daughter Lenne studies at the Academy alongside Cass.

Government buildings are designed so defendants cannot hear judges deliberate. This is presented as protecting judicial impartiality. It also means defendants cannot hear if a judge is singing an under-the-breath codicil that modifies the terms of their verdict. The architecture predates the current judiciary by sixty years. Whoever designed it knew what they were doing.

**Commerce**
Song-brokers operate in every market district, composing binding contracts on commission. A good song-broker can draft a three-clause binding in the time it takes to count out the coins. A bad one produces lame chords, which is why market-goers learn early to check a broker's guild credentials before signing anything.

Money is tuned metal. Each denomination is cast to ring at a specific pitch when struck against a standard testing surface: the copper quarter rings at A, the silver half at D, the gold full-note at G. Counterfeiting is not just fraud — it is a tonal crime, because a coin that rings at the wrong pitch can corrupt a contract ratified with it. Counterfeit coins are called *flats* in the trade. The penalty for producing them is the permanent legal voiding of all contracts you currently hold — which, for anyone with a mortgage or a trade agreement, is catastrophic. The penalty for *using* a flat unknowingly is a fine. The penalty for using one knowingly is the same as producing. The line between knowing and not knowing is the most litigated question in Cantamura's commercial courts.

**Class Structure**
Tonal Law advantages the trained. A first-generation merchant who cannot afford a song-broker signs contracts by ear, which means they often miss a lame chord until it's too late. The Academy trains song-brokers, but Academy tuition requires a ratified payment contract, which requires a song-broker to compose it, which costs money. The outer districts have one licensed broker for every four hundred residents. The inner districts have one for every forty.

Children in the outer districts learn a simplified, uncodified version of tonal law from their parents — folk intervals, handed down informally, that provide rough protection in small transactions. The Academy does not recognize these folk intervals as legally valid. They work anyway, most of the time, because the acoustic principles don't care about the Academy's opinion. This is not something the Academy advertises.

**Disability and Voice**
Anyone who cannot sing cannot enter a contract as a primary party. They can be represented by a licensed Vocal Proxy — a person who sings on their behalf, legally bound to represent their interests accurately. Vocal Proxies are expensive. The Muteborn Question (see History) was never resolved, which means the legal personhood of the constitutionally voiceless remains technically unaddressed. In practice, the courts handle these cases individually, which means outcomes depend heavily on the presiding Court Singer's politics.

The deaf occupy a different legal category. They can sing contracts; they simply cannot hear whether the other party's intervals are correct. The Academy offers a deaf-accessible notation system that translates intervals into physical vibration through a resonance board. Three people in Cantamura currently use it. The waiting list for a certified resonance board is fourteen months.

**Childhood**
Children under ten are legally non-binding — they cannot enter contracts, cannot be held to oaths, and cannot testify in court. This protection expires on their tenth birthday, at which point they are considered acoustically competent. Most families mark this with a small ceremony involving the child singing their first formal declaration — usually something simple, like a statement of their name and family — in front of witnesses.

Cass sang his at ten. His father wept. He thought it was pride. He is no longer sure.

## Geography

### The Amphitheater

Cantamura sits inside a natural limestone bowl, roughly two miles across at the rim and half a mile at the floor. The bowl was formed by a collapsed cave system — the limestone is riddled with resonance chambers below street level, most of them unmapped. The city's architects have spent two centuries arguing about whether these chambers amplify or distort the acoustic effects of the tonal system. The answer is both, depending on where you stand.

**Acoustic Properties**
At the bowl's center — the Civic Square, where the Bell Tower stands — sound behaves as designed: whispers carry a quarter mile, shouts die three feet from the speaker's mouth. This is not uniform across the city. The effect weakens toward the rim. In the outer districts, normal acoustic rules apply. The boundary between the two zones is called the *Fade Line*, and it moves slightly with weather and season. Buildings near the Fade Line are cheaper to rent. They are also legally ambiguous — a contract ratified near the Fade Line may or may not be binding, depending on which side of the acoustic boundary the parties were standing. Real estate lawyers near the Fade Line are busy.

**Sensory Signature of the Bowl**
The Civic Square in morning: cold stone, the smell of bread from the bakeries on the south side, the resonant hum of the Bell Tower before the Civic Pitch begins. The sound of the city waking up carries as a low, layered murmur — individual words distinguishable if you focus, which is why people in the center speak carefully. The light comes in at a steep angle until midmorning, the bowl's rim throwing long shadows.

The Civic Square at midday: heat off the limestone, the smell of pitch from the road-sealers, the constant low-level noise of a market city that knows its conversations carry. People here speak in clipped, considered sentences. Tourists talk too loudly and then look embarrassed when strangers turn to stare.

### The Bell Tower (Interior)

Forty feet of dressed limestone, the original stonework Aldric Bellwright laid two hundred years ago. The exterior is gray-white with age; the interior is darker, the stone stained by two centuries of bronze dust and linseed oil carried up from the workshop below. Forty-seven steps to the main bell chamber, worn concave in the center from generations of Bellwright feet.

The **bell chamber** is a single vaulted room at the tower's top, open on four sides through arched windows that face the cardinal directions. The forty-seven original bells hang from iron frames that Aldric built — or so the family story goes. The frames are bolted to the limestone vault. The bells range from head-sized (the highest civic bells) to chest-height and waist-width (the major civic bells). The bronze of the originals is darker and finer-grained than standard casting bronze, with a faint reddish tinge from the old alloy's mineral trace — a compound no longer available. The chamber smells of cold stone, metal dust, and the particular stillness of a space designed to hold sound.

Standing among all forty-seven bells at once is overwhelming in a way that is more than physical weight. The bells on their frames hold a latent resonance — strike one, and the others answer in sympathetic vibration, each one speaking its own shade of the same frequency. In the right conditions, the chamber produces standing waves: frequencies that persist in fixed positions in the air, so that stepping two feet to the left changes what you hear. Cass has mapped these positions by feel.

The chamber floor is gritty with fine bronze dust that never fully clears. Afternoon light through the tower windows throws long rectangles of gold across the stone that shift perceptibly during a long inspection. The wind at this height carries differently than at street level — colder, cleaner, and it moves through the arched windows with a sound like breath across the mouth of a jar.

**Amplification mechanics:** The forty-seven bells are tuned not just to their own pitch but to the cumulative resonance of the bells that precede them in the Civic Pitch sequence. Each bell's entry finds a harmonic already waiting in the air from the earlier bells, creating a compound waveform greater than the sum of individual tones. The limestone bowl beneath acts as a natural amplifier — the concave geology reflects and focuses sound toward the Civic Square the way a parabolic dish focuses light. The sub-harmonic that Cass hears is generated by the interaction between the old alloy's undertone properties and this cumulative resonance: no single bell produces it, but all forty-seven ringing in Aldric's specific sequence create an interference pattern that generates a frequency below any individual bell's range. This is why the sub-harmonic is only audible (to those who can hear it) during the full Civic Pitch or the Founding Day concert — it requires the complete sequence. Partial sequences, individual bells, even groups of bells do not produce it.

### Districts

**The Bell Quarter** — Inner district, around the Bell Tower. The Bellwright family workshop is here: a long stone building with a forge at one end and a tuning hall at the other. The tuning hall has a vaulted ceiling that makes it the best acoustic space in the city outside the Bell Tower itself. The workshop smells of metal-dust, linseed oil, and the faint char of the forge. The floor is gritty underfoot from decades of bronze filings. The bells in progress hang from iron frames along the walls and hum faintly in sympathy when the morning Civic Pitch sounds. The Bellwright house is attached to the workshop's north wall. There are three bells in the house that Cass is not allowed to touch. They are kept in a locked room off the kitchen. He has never heard them ring.

**The Academy District** — Mid-ring, on the east slope. The Cantamura Academy of Civic Harmony occupies a cluster of buildings that have been added to for a hundred and sixty years without a coherent plan. The oldest buildings are tight-corridored stone; the newer ones are open-arcaded limestone. Sound behaves oddly in the corridors between buildings — a conversation in one arcade can be heard clearly in another forty feet away, which students learn early and exploit constantly. The Academy smells of chalk, old paper, and the particular resin used to treat the practice room floors. Students wear gray; faculty wear whatever color indicates their specialization. Song-brokers in training wear copper-colored sashes.

**The Weavers' District** — Mid-ring, west slope. Textile workers, dyers, and small-goods manufacturers. The district has a persistent smell of dye-vats — sulfur and indigo and, in summer, the sweetish rot of fermentation tanks. The Fade Line cuts through the Weavers' District's eastern edge, which means the district's legal life operates partly in normal acoustic space. The district has developed its own informal dispute-resolution system involving community witnesses and folk intervals. The Academy does not recognize it. It works anyway.

**The Tanner's Quarter** — Outer rim, north. Smells like what it is. The acoustic effects of the bowl barely reach here. People talk loudly, argue openly, and regard the inner city's careful speech patterns with open amusement. Senna Voss's throat-remedy shop is here, in a building that used to be a stable. She does not advertise. Her clients find her by word of mouth, which in the Tanner's Quarter means actual word of mouth, spoken at normal volume.

**The Merchant Tier** — Inner ring, south slope. The House of Corda's compound is here: a walled estate with its own resonance chamber, its own licensed Court Singer on retainer, and a gate that has been closed to the Bellwright family since Perin's signing. The Merchant Tier smells of money, which in Cantamura means tuned bronze and cedar storage boxes and the faint floral scent of the preservative wax used on instrument cases.

### Neighboring Places

**Veth** — Two days east by road. A city of comparable size with a written legal system, a history of resisting Cantamura's expansion, and a thriving trade in goods that Cantamura's acoustic geography makes difficult to produce — large-scale textile machinery, for instance, which generates too much noise to operate near the Civic Square. Veth regards Cantamura with the specific hostility of a place that was once occupied by people who believed they were doing it a favor. Trade happens because it must. Diplomacy is cold and formal. Veth has, in the past decade, begun experimenting with a modified tonal system of its own, using iron bells instead of bronze. Cantamura's Academy calls this "acoustically illiterate." Veth's results have been mixed but not catastrophic.

**The Chalk Settlements** — Three days north, a loose network of small farming communities in a limestone plateau. They have no tonal system and no interest in one. They trade grain and chalk to Cantamura in exchange for bronze goods and legal services — occasionally a Cantamura song-broker will travel north to ratify a land agreement, which takes a week and costs more than most Chalk Settlement families see in a year. The Chalk Settlements have a tradition of settling disputes by community assembly and public shaming, which Cantamura legal scholars find barbaric and which seems to produce roughly equivalent outcomes.

**The Flood Mouth** — Five days south, a river delta trading port. No consistent legal system — they use a rotating combination of written contract, witnessed oath, and, in disputes involving Cantamura merchants, a portable resonance chamber that the port authority rents by the hour. The Flood Mouth smells of fish, salt, and the particular mildew of buildings that flood twice yearly. It is the source of most of Cantamura's imported goods, which means it is also the source of most of Cantamura's counterfeit coins. The port authority and Cantamura's commercial courts maintain a tense extradition agreement that neither side honors consistently.

## Factions & Politics

**The Bellwright Family**
Not a political faction in the formal sense — they have no seats on the Civic Council and no licensed Court Singer. Their power is structural: they control the bells, which set the pitch, which validates every contract ratified each day. The family has maintained this position for two hundred years by being indispensable and quiet. Eddan Bellwright, the current patriarch, is the quietest of a quiet line. He knows things about the original bells that he has not told the Civic Council, the Academy, or his younger son. His hands shake. He keeps working.

**The Academy of Civic Harmony**
The institution that trains, licenses, and disciplines all tonal law practitioners. The Academy controls who can legally compose a binding contract, which means it controls who can participate in the legal system as anything other than a client. The Academy's current Chancellor, a woman named Rector Suvaine, has held the position for nineteen years and is widely regarded as the reason Volume Four of the Interval Codex still has blank pages — she has blocked three attempts to codify exceptional circumstances because codifying them would limit judicial discretion, which would limit the Academy's influence over outcomes. The Academy is not corrupt in any simple sense. It is something more durable: it is an institution that has confused its own survival with the public good.

**The House of Corda**
The largest merchant family in Cantamura, with controlling interests in the Academy's instrument supply, three of the city's five licensed resonance chambers, and the contract that binds Perin Bellwright to service. The House of Corda does not hold political office. It does not need to. It holds contracts. The current head of the family, Maret Corda, is sixty years old, sings a clean baritone, and has not lost a contract dispute in twenty years. This is not because she is always right. It is because she retains two of the four Court Singers currently operating in Cantamura — both related to her by marriage.

**The Weavers' District Compact**
An informal coalition of outer-district residents, tradespeople, and the occasional sympathetic Academy dropout who have been building a parallel legal infrastructure for twenty years. They use folk intervals, community witnesses, and a system of notched wooden records that they call *tally-songs*. The Compact does not call itself a faction. It calls itself a neighborhood association. The Academy calls it a threat to legal order. The Compact's current de facto leader is a retired dye-merchant named Torvald Hess, who is sixty-three, partially deaf, and the best negotiator in the outer districts. He has never lost a dispute before a community assembly. He has also never been permitted to argue before the Civic Court.

**The Blank Pages Society**
Not a formal organization. A loose network of Academy scholars, frustrated Court Singers, and one or two Civic Council members who believe Volume Four needs to be completed — specifically, that the blank pages represent a deliberate gap that someone put there for a reason. They meet informally, argue constantly, and have produced three competing theories about what the blank pages were meant to contain. One of those theories involves the Bellwright family's sealed journals. Eddan Bellwright has been approached twice. Both times he said he didn't know what they were talking about. His hands shook while he said it.

## Bestiary / Flora / Natural World

The limestone bowl that holds Cantamura has a specific ecology shaped by its acoustics. Animals that rely on echolocation — bats, primarily — are either absent or behave strangely. The cave systems below the city support a population of blind cave-fish in underground streams; these are occasionally caught and eaten, and have a mild, clean flavor that the outer districts prize and the inner districts regard as poverty food.

The bowl's rim supports a ring of old-growth trees, mostly oak and hornbeam, that have grown with their root systems partly in the limestone. These trees have unusually dense wood — denser than specimens of the same species grown in normal soil — and Cantamura's instrument-makers prize them for resonance boards. The Academy has a standing contract with the city's foresters for first rights on any felled rim-tree. The foresters have their own opinions about this arrangement.

Birds in the bowl's interior are quieter than their counterparts outside. Whether this is behavioral adaptation or selective pressure over two centuries of odd acoustics, no one has studied formally. Cass has noticed that the birds near the Bell Tower go silent for approximately thirty seconds after the Civic Pitch ends each morning. He has not mentioned this to anyone.

The bronze alloy Aldric Bellwright used for the original forty-seven bells has never been exactly replicated. Replacement bells cast from standard alloys hold their tune differently — not wrong, but not quite the same. The Bellwright family has a private stock of old-alloy bronze, source unknown, that they use for critical repairs. The stock is running low. Eddan knows how low. He has not told the Civic Council.

## Cultural Details

**The Morning Pitch**
Every day, forty-five minutes after sunrise, the Bellwright family strikes the Civic Pitch — a specific sequence on specific bells, lasting approximately four minutes. The city stops. Market stalls pause. Conversations end mid-sentence. The sequence sets the reference pitch for all contracts ratified that day. Missing the Civic Pitch is not illegal, but any contract ratified before it sounds is provisional. Most people wait. Old people complain that the young don't wait properly anymore.

**Coming of Age**
At ten, a child sings their first formal declaration and enters legal personhood. At fourteen, they are eligible for Academy enrollment or trade apprenticeship — the two paths that lead to full economic participation. Children who take neither path by sixteen are considered legally adult but practically marginal; they can enter contracts, but without training or a guild affiliation, they have no one to compose those contracts properly. The Weavers' District Compact runs an informal apprenticeship program for sixteen-year-olds who missed the window. The Academy does not acknowledge it exists.

**Food**
Cantamura eats simply and seasonally. Breakfast is bread and hard cheese, sometimes a boiled egg. The bread in the inner districts is white and fine; in the outer districts, it has seeds in the crust and a denser crumb that keeps longer. Lunch is the main meal — soup or a grain dish, meat twice a week for working families, more often for the merchant tier. Dinner is light. The city has a tradition of eating the main meal in silence, which outsiders find strange. The explanation given is courtesy — you don't want to accidentally sing something binding over your soup. The real reason is that after a day of careful speech, people are tired of words.

**Festivals**
The Founding Day celebration, held on the anniversary of Aldric Bellwright's first Civic Pitch, involves a public concert in the Civic Square — the only day of the year when non-binding music is played for pleasure in the square without commercial intent. Song-brokers take the day off. Court Singers take the day off. Children run through the square shouting, which they are normally discouraged from doing. The Bellwright family rings all forty-seven bells in sequence at noon. It takes twelve minutes. The sound carries to the rim and back. Cass has heard it every year of his life. It is the only hour of the year when the under-note is completely silent.

He has never told anyone this either.

**Clothing**
Functional and durable, with rank signaled by material rather than cut. Academy students wear gray wool. Licensed practitioners wear copper-colored sashes or pins. The merchant tier wears dyed linen in the summer, wool in the winter, always in solid colors — patterns are considered ostentatious. The Tanner's Quarter wears whatever doesn't show stains. Bellwrights, by tradition, wear no guild markings. They are considered above the guild system, or outside it, depending on who you ask.

**Taboos**
Humming without intent — absent-minded humming, the kind people do while working — is considered rude in the inner districts, because a hummed interval can accidentally approximate a binding note. Children are trained out of it early. In the outer districts, people hum constantly and regard the inner city's silence as repressed.

Singing in a minor key in a public space requires a license, because minor progressions include the release sequence, and an unlicensed release sung near an active binding can corrupt it. The license is cheap and easy to obtain. Most street musicians have one. This does not stop the Academy from citing unlicensed minor-key singing as a public nuisance roughly twice a year, always in the outer districts, never in the Merchant Tier.

## Internal Consistency Rules

**The physics of sound in Cantamura are not magic.** The acoustic properties of the limestone bowl are real and consistent. They do not change based on narrative need. The Fade Line moves with weather but within a predictable range. Whispers carry a quarter mile at the center; this is a fact of the geography, not a feature of the tonal system.

**Tonal Law requires both voice and interval.** A single note sung alone cannot bind. An interval requires two pitches — either two voices, or one voice and a reference tone from a tuned instrument. This is why the morning Civic Pitch matters: it provides the reference tone against which all day's contracts are calibrated.

**Coercion voids a binding, but proof of coercion is nearly impossible.** The system does not have a mechanism for detecting coercion. This is the structural flaw at the heart of the story. It is not a bug someone introduced — it is a gap that was never filled, possibly deliberately.

**The under-note cannot be heard by anyone else.** Not yet. Whether this is permanently true is a question the story will answer. For now: Cass is alone in what he hears.

**The forty-seven bells cannot be recast.** If a bell cracks beyond repair, it must be replaced with a bell from the old-alloy stock. There are currently six bells in the Bellwright workshop that have been repaired rather than replaced because the stock is too low. Repaired bells hold their tune for approximately three years before they need attention again. Eddan Bellwright is the only person who knows the stock is running low enough that this is becoming a problem.

**The Bellwright journals are sealed, not destroyed.** They exist. They are in the locked room off the kitchen, behind the three bells Cass is not allowed to touch. The journals and the bells are related. The nature of that relationship is the deepest layer of the story.

**What Aldric composed into the first bells was not a law.** The morning Civic Pitch contains it — buried in the harmonic structure, below the range of normal hearing, below even the range of most trained acousticians. It is not a command. It is not a binding. Cass is beginning to hear it. When he finally understands what it is, the question it asks will be the question the novel has been asking all along.

The answer is not in Volume Four of the Interval Codex. The blank pages are blank because no one who read the journals has been willing to write it down.
