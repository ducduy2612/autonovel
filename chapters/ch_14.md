# Chapter Fourteen: The Workshop

He went inside.

The workshop door was unlocked, the latch cold under his hand. His father looked up from beside the lathe — not from working at it, just sitting beside it, hands open on his thighs, the lamp above the workbench throwing its yellow circle down onto the floor. The bells in progress hung along the iron frames on the far wall. The forge was cold.

"You went to see her," his father said.

"Yes."

Eddan looked at him a moment longer. "Sit down."

Cass sat on the bench by the door. The stone was cold through his trousers. He could smell linseed oil and the dry chill of bronze that had been sitting in unheated air. He put his hands in his lap and looked at his father across the workshop and thought, without planning to think it: *this is still my favorite place in the city*. He sat with that for a moment. It seemed important to know it before what came next.

Neither of them spoke.

One of the half-finished bells shifted faintly on its frame — a breath of resonance, barely there, the metal remembering some vibration from hours ago. The lamp wick made a small adjustment to itself. Somewhere in the outer districts, a signal bell marked the hour, and then it was done, and the workshop was quiet again.

His father did not reach for a tool. He did not turn to the lathe. He sat with his hands open on his thighs and waited, and Cass sat on the cold bench and waited, and the space between them held everything that was about to change.





---

He organized it the way Proctor Halvane had taught them to organize a legal argument. Premise, evidence, implication, conclusion. He kept his voice level and he watched his father's face while he spoke.

He started with the journals — the first set, the ones describing the sub-harmonic, what Aldric had buried in the harmonic structure of the Civic Pitch. He talked about what Maret had confirmed and what she'd refused to confirm, and the air between them had been clean the whole time. The part that had stayed with him. He talked about the under-note, the question he'd been hearing for six months beneath the morning sequence. He talked about the void at the center of the binding — how a contract could be sung in perfect form and still be hollow, how the form was the disguise and not the thing itself.



His father sat very still through all of it. His hands were open on his thighs. He had the look of a man watching something fall that he has been waiting ten years to see fall, and who is not relieved.

When Cass got to Maret Corda — what she'd said, how she'd said it, the specific phrase *everyone the city listens to* — his father's jaw tightened. Just that. His hands stayed open.

Cass came to the end of it. The workshop held the silence after.

"I know," his father said.

"I know you know."

The lamp made a small sound. The wick settling, the oil shifting in its reservoir.

Cass looked at the cracked oil jar on the shelf. Looked at it for a moment. Then looked back at his father.

"When did you read them?"

Eddan's jaw moved. The muscle at the corner of it. "The night Perin signed."

The bell on its frame had gone entirely quiet. The forge was cold and had been cold for hours. Cass could feel the cold of the stone coming up through his boots, up through his legs, settling in.

"You read the journals the night Perin signed," he said. He kept the flat voice, the one that let him get through a sentence without the sentence breaking. "You knew the contract was void."

His father said nothing.

"You knew Perin didn't have to go."

His father's hands started shaking. It began in the fingers — a fine tremor, almost invisible, and then it moved up through the wrist and into the forearms and stayed there. The tremor Cass had watched his whole life, the one that disappeared at the lathe and returned at the dinner table, the one his father managed and managed and managed. It was worse than usual. Worse than Cass had ever seen it.

He watched it. He didn't look away.

"Why didn't you tell him?"

Eddan opened his mouth. Something moved through his face — not a single thing, several things in sequence, passing through and leaving marks. He closed his mouth. His hands shook.

"Because if I told him the contract was void," he said, "I had to tell him why." His voice was different. Quieter, but not by choice — the quietness was what was left after everything else had been taken out of it. "And if I told him why—"

He stopped. He looked at his hands.

Cass waited.

"I'd have to tell him the bells are built on a question nobody answered." Eddan's hands shook. "I'd have to tell him Aldric put something in the bronze that the city has been calling law for two hundred years, and it was never law. It was a question. And I'd have to tell him—" He stopped again. Started again, slower. "I'd have to tell him that I knew. That I had read it, that night, all of it, and I still—"

He stopped.

Cass said it. "You chose the bells over Perin."

His father looked at his hands for a long time.

"Yes." The word came out of him the way something comes out of a man who has been carrying it for ten years and has finally put it down. Not relief. Just the absence of the weight, which was its own kind of ruin. "I chose the bells over your brother." His hands shook. "I have known that every day since."

And there — in the silence after the words — Cass noticed what was absent. The under-note was gone. Not quiet, not muted, not the low background hum he'd learned to live with whenever his father spoke about Perin or the bells or the contract or any of the things that had been wrapped in careful, protective half-truths for ten years. Gone. The space behind his left eye where the needle lived was simply empty, still as a bell chamber after the last vibration has faded. His father was sitting four feet away with his hands shaking on his thighs, saying the worst thing he had ever said, and there was nothing underneath it. No evasion. No constructed version. No story told so many times it had calcified into something that almost passed for truth. Just the thing itself, bare and complete. Cass realized, with a clarity that felt like cold water, that he had never heard his father speak without the under-note before. Not once. Not in fourteen years. This — the shaking hands, the ruined voice, the word *yes* falling like a struck bell into the quiet workshop — this was what his father sounded like when every wall was down.

The lamp above the workbench made its small sound again. The oil jar with the cracked clay sat on the shelf where it had always sat. The bell frames along the far wall held their bells in their iron arms, and the bells held their bronze, and the bronze held its question, patient and cold.

Eddan looked up from his hands.

"I told myself the bells had to keep running," Eddan said. "That if I broke the system — if I told Perin the contract was void, if Perin went to the Academy, if the whole question of what Aldric composed came out — the city would—" He stopped. "I don't know. Fall. I told myself it would fall."

"And?"

His father was quiet.

"And I think I believed it," he said. "For a while. And then I think I kept saying it because I had already made the choice and the choice needed a reason." His hands shook. He looked at them as if they belonged to someone he was meeting for the first time. "I kept the bells running. I kept the records. I went to Maret Corda's compound four times a year with the maintenance reports and I sat across from her and we did not speak about your brother and I came home."

He reached for something on the bench beside him — the pitch-gauge, his hand going to it by habit — and then stopped. His fingers touched the handle and couldn't grip it. The tremor was too bad. He pulled his hand back.

"He writes to me," Eddan said. "Perin. He —" A pause. "He writes." He looked at the floor. "I read the letters in here, after you're —" He stopped. "In bed." Another silence. "He never says he's — he describes the resonance chambers. Tells me when a bell needs re-tuning." His voice was as close to nothing as a voice could get. "He sounds like me. He sounds exactly like me, and I don't know if that's because he learned it from me or because that's what ten years of keeping your mouth shut does to a person."

The workshop held that.

Cass sat on the cold bench, palms flat on his thighs. The oil jar with the cracked clay sat on the shelf. The lamp burned. His father's hands shook and shook and did not stop.

 The workshop made its small sounds — the wick, the metal, the faint settling of old stone. The bells on their frames breathed their cold breath.

He looked at his father's hands. The tremor had not eased. It would not ease tonight.



Cass had made him speak.

He didn't know yet what that cost. He would understand it later, or he'd try to. For now he sat on the cold bench in his favorite place in the city and felt the weight of what had just moved through the room — not gone, nothing like gone, but changed in shape, the way the air changes after a bell stops ringing. The sound had taken something with it. What remained was just the room.

He stood up.

"I'm going to fix this," he said.

His father looked up at him. His hands shook.

"Cass—"

"I don't know how yet." He picked up his coat from the hook by the door. "But I'm going to fix it."

He went out into the city.

---

The Civic Square at night was a different city.

The market stalls shuttered. The song-brokers gone, or most of them — one remained at the far edge of the square, a small figure behind a folding table with a lamp, finishing something late. Cass could hear the contract from twenty feet away: a short binding, a service agreement, two voices ratifying in thirds that rang clean and cold in the night air. No under-note. Whatever this was, both parties wanted it.

He watched them finish. The broker packed his table. The client walked away toward the Merchant Tier. The broker folded his lamp and was gone, and the square was empty and dark and cold, and Cass walked to the center of it.

He'd stood here before, during the morning Civic Pitch, when his father needed him to check the acoustic distribution — hold a reference tone in his head and measure how it decayed across the stone. He knew the bowl of the square the way he knew the inside of the workshop. He knew where sound pooled and where it thinned. He walked to the point where the acoustic effect was deepest, directly below the Bell Tower's longest shadow, and stopped.

The Bell Tower stood against the sky. Dark stone, old stone, the arched windows where the forty-seven bells hung in their frames — not visible from here, just implied. The massive fact of them pressing down into the square and up into the night. Cass tilted his head back.

He could feel the sub-harmonic from here.

Faint — fainter than in the mornings, when the Civic Pitch amplified it — but present. Always present. The question the bells had been asking since before he was born, since before his father was born, since Aldric Bellwright had stood in this same square and cast his alloy and tuned his sequence and buried something in the harmonic structure that no one had answered in two hundred years. Cass stood in the acoustic bowl's center and felt the question in his sternum, in the bones behind his ears, in the frequency that lived below hearing and above pain.







His father had said: *I chose the bells over your brother.*

He had said it and his hands had shaken and he had not looked away.

That was the thing Cass kept returning to, standing in the cold square with the sub-harmonic pressing against his sternum. Not the choice — the choice was its own catastrophe and he'd have the rest of his life to understand it — but as his father had finally said it. Like setting something down. Like the word *yes* was the only honest thing left.

The question in the bronze pressed and pressed.

Cass breathed. The cold came up through his boots and into his legs and he let it. The city around him was quiet in the way it was never fully quiet — the low frequency of ten thousand people at rest, obligations sealed for the night, contracts sitting in the air between parties like standing waves. He stood in the center of it and held everything he now knew: the journals, the sub-harmonic, Maret Corda's phrase — *everyone the city listens to* — and his father's hands shaking on his thighs.





*Torvald*, he thought. Not a plan. Just a name, arriving like a note struck in a quiet room, clean and precise, the first frequency of something he couldn't hear the shape of yet.

He stood in the square until the cold had gone past uncomfortable into something that was simply the temperature of the air, and then he turned and walked home through the Bell Quarter, past the shuttered stalls and the dark workshop windows and the stone buildings that had been here since before anyone now living had been born. His boots on the stone. His breath in the cold air. The sub-harmonic fading as he moved away from the square's center, and then fading further, and then quiet.

His father's lamp was off in the workshop when he passed. The window dark. He looked at it for a moment, the dark glass, the shape of the lathe just visible inside, and kept walking.

He went in the house and up the stairs and into his room and lay down on top of the blankets with his coat still on. In the drawer of his desk: his brother's letters. In the locked room off the kitchen: the journals and the three bells he'd never been allowed to touch. In the Bell Tower: forty-seven bells in their frames, holding their question in their bronze, patient as they had always been patient, as they would be patient long after Cass was gone, unless someone finally answered.



He closed his eyes.
