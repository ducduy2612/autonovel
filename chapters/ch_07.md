# Chapter Seven: The Bellwright-Corda Agreement

His father was at the lathe when Cass came in.

The workshop had gone to its night-self: lamplight making the hanging bells into shadows, linseed oil heavier without the forge's competing heat. Eddan worked with his back to the door, hands moving along a clapper shaft, testing the curve. He didn't turn when Cass set his bag on the stone bench.

Cass came further in and stopped at the edge of the lamp's reach.

"I need to ask you something."

The lathe kept turning.

"Mm."

"The Bellwright-Corda agreement. The one from twelve years ago."

His father's hands went still. The lathe slowed. Stopped.

Eddan set the clapper down with the care of a man who needed something to do with his hands, and turned.

"What about it," he said.

"I want to know what it covers."

"The maintenance arrangement. Quarterly reports. Access to the Corda resonance chambers for tuning." His father's voice was flat. "You've seen the notices."

"I've seen the notices. I haven't seen the agreement."

"It's a standard — it's..." He stopped. "Standard custodianship. The Corda family holds a — the bronze stock." A pause. "We maintain. We file reports."

"What kind of claim?"

"An old one." His father folded the cloth he'd been using. Set it down. "Aldric's son signed a document. Before the tonal system. Written, not sung. The Corda family holds it."

"Is it enforceable?"

Something crossed his father's face, quick and gone. "It's a historical document. Under current Tonal Law—"

"Is it enforceable."

The cooling lathe ticked once.

"No," his father said. "It isn't."

"But she used it anyway."

His father looked at him. The lamp threw shadows up under his eyes. "She raised the question," he said. "Twelve years ago. Whether the Academy should examine the claim. Whether the Bellwright family's custodianship was—" He stopped. Started again. "The word she used was *fitness*."

Cass's chest went tight.

"Fitness," he said.

"Whether the Bellwright family was fit to maintain sole custodianship of the forty-seven bells. It would have required a formal review. The Academy examining the journals. The bronze stock records. The casting methods." He paused. "Everything."

"And Perin signed the service contract instead."

His father said nothing.

"That's what it cost," Cass said. "To make her not raise the question."

Still nothing. His father had turned slightly, not away but angled, as if the bells on the wall needed looking at.

Cass watched the back of his head for a moment. Then he said, "I'm going to the public registry. Tonight. The original filing will be there."

"Cass—"

"I'm not asking for your copy." He picked up his bag. "I wanted you to know I was going."

He walked out before his father could find the words, if he had any.

---

The Academy's public registry occupied the ground floor of the oldest building in the district, tight-corridored stone that smelled of chalk and old paper. The registry was open until the evening bell, which gave him forty minutes.

The registrar was a thin man named Adwick who wore copper-colored sleeve guards over his coat. He looked at Cass the way he looked at all Academy students who arrived without a faculty sponsor — measuring.

"Bellwright-Corda agreement," Cass said. "Twelve years ago. Custodianship filing."

Adwick didn't write anything down. He looked at Cass. His eyes went to the shelves behind him, then back, and something in the look was off — a fraction too long, a fraction too careful.

"That's a sealed registry," he said. "Privacy clause, standard for—"

"Custodianship agreements are public record. The bells are a civic instrument."

"The privacy clause covers the codicil amendments. The primary filing is public." Adwick paused. "One moment."

He disappeared into the shelves.

Cass stood at the counter and pressed his Down the corridor, a student was running scales — descending fifth, over and over, not quite landing the resolution. He made himself stop listening.

Adwick came back with a bound folio, gray-covered, tied with the red cord that marked civic filings. He set it on the counter. Then he set it down again a second time, slightly differently, and said, "You can read it here. It doesn't leave the room," in the tone of a man reciting something he'd been told to say.

"I know," Cass said.

He untied the cord and opened it. Twelve pages, formal Academy script, dense with quarterly maintenance schedules and bronze stock inventories. He read every clause.

He found it on page seven.

He read it once. Read it again.

*Co-Custodial Review Clause. In the event that the House of Corda determines, in its sole assessment, that the Bellwright family's continued custodianship is inconsistent with the standards of fitness required by the Academy's Charter of Civic Instruments, the House of Corda reserves the right to petition the Academy for a formal fitness review. Such a petition will require the Bellwright family to submit all records, journals, and documentation pertaining to the original casting and tuning of the forty-seven bells for examination.*

And below it:

*This co-custodial review right persists for the duration of the Bellwright-Corda service agreement and all amendments thereto, and shall not expire so long as any active service obligation exists between the two parties.*

He looked up.

Adwick was watching him from the far end of the counter. Not working. Watching.

Cass looked back at the page. *So long as any active service obligation exists.* Perin's contract was a service obligation. While Perin was in service, Maret Corda could invoke the fitness review at any moment, for any reason, and the journals opened.

He turned back to page one and looked for the term date.

There wasn't one. The service obligation was referenced by a codicil number. Sealed. He couldn't see when it ended, or whether it ended, or what conditions would satisfy it.

He set his finger on the co-custodial clause and read it a third time.

*Sole assessment.* She didn't need a lame chord. She didn't need a provable breach. She needed to say one word to the Academy and the journals opened. And she could say it any time she chose, for as long as Perin's contract ran.

He closed the folio. Retied the cord.

"Thank you," he said.

Adwick said, "The codicil records. If you wanted to petition for access—"

"I know the process."

Adwick looked at him. "Someone was in yesterday," he said. "Asking about the same filing."

Cass went still.

"Who."

Adwick adjusted his sleeve guard. "I can't say who. I can tell you they weren't a student."

The under-note stirred — not the clean needle-sharp signal of a lie, but something muddier, less certain. A flicker. He'd felt it before in market disputes, when a seller quoted a price they'd already half-convinced themselves was fair. Adwick wasn't lying about someone having been here. That part sat clean in the air between them. But the way he said *I can't say* — not *I don't know*, not *I won't say*, but *I can't* — that flickered. The under-note pulsed once behind Cass's left eye, dimmed, pulsed again. As if Adwick had told himself a story about why he couldn't say, and believed the story just enough for the note to lose its edge.

The man across the counter wasn't lying — or he was, but only about which part of the truth was his to give. The important thing rang clean: someone had been here, someone who wasn't a student, someone who knew what to look for. The evasion wrapped around it was something Adwick had convinced himself was duty rather than cowardice, and the conviction was just strong enough to blur the signal.

Cass kept his voice even. "Did they take anything?"

A pause. Long enough to be an answer. "The filing is complete," Adwick said. "The primary filing."

"The codicil index," Cass said. "Is it complete?"

Adwick's eyes went to the shelves behind him. Came back. He said, "The codicil index is a separate record," in a voice that had gone careful and flat.

"Is it complete."

Adwick said nothing.

He picked up his bag and walked out.

The evening bell rang as he came through the Academy district gate. B-flat, clean and civic, rolling down from the tower. He stood on the road and let it pass and did not feel the usual thing he felt when the bell sounded right.

Someone had been there yesterday. Before him. Not a student — someone who knew the filing well enough to go straight to the codicil index, and who had enough standing in the registry for Adwick to go careful and flat when asked about it. The tally-song strip gone from Torvald's shelf. Now this. Someone moving ahead of him, taking things off shelves before he arrived.

*Sole assessment.* The clause sat in his chest where a clean chord should have been.

He turned onto the road toward the Merchant Tier.

He didn't make a decision about it. He was already walking.

---

The Corda compound sat in the inner ring of the bowl, south slope, where the Merchant Tier kept its walled estates and its cedar-scented money. The Corda compound's gate porter had hands that didn't match his position — broad, roughened, the hands of someone who had worked with materials heavier than paper. Cass filed that away without knowing what to do with it. Cass had stood outside the gate before — once, two years ago, on an errand for his father that had required him to deliver a maintenance report to the porter's window. He'd handed over the folio and walked away fast and not looked back. That time he'd had permission to be there. This time he had the co-custodial clause in his bag and his brother's name in his mouth and absolutely nothing else.

He felt the compound's acoustics before he saw the gate.

It started at the corner of Mercer Lane — a pressure change, subtle, the way your ears register a hillside before your legs do. The Merchant Tier sat close enough to the bowl's center that the amphitheater's geometry was still working, but the Corda compound had its own resonance chambers built into the east wing, and those chambers pushed back against the natural acoustic. Standing at the corner, Cass could feel the interference in his back teeth. A chord that almost resolved, held just short of resolution. The tension of it pressed against the inside of his skull like a thumb against a bruise.

He stood there for a moment. Let his jaw loosen. The interference didn't stop, but he stopped fighting it.

The gate was iron, tall, the kind designed to look permanent. A porter's window sat to the right. A boy about Cass's age was leaning against the wall beside it, eating something wrapped in paper, not paying attention to anything.

Cass walked up to the window.

The porter was a broad man with a gray coat and the specific boredom of someone who had been sitting in a small room for a long time. He looked at Cass.

"Bellwright," Cass said. "I'm here about the maintenance schedule."

The porter looked at him. "You're the Bellwright boy."

"My father sent me." The words came out steady, which surprised him. "There's a discrepancy in the quarterly report. The resonance chamber in the east wing — the tuning record doesn't match the inspection notes from last month. He needs to see the chamber logs before he files the correction."

A pause.

"He usually comes himself."

"His hands," Cass said. "He's been having trouble with the fine work. I've been running the inspections this quarter."

Another pause. The porter looked at him with the expression of a man doing arithmetic he didn't particularly want to do. Cass kept his face neutral and thought about nothing except the specific weight of a pitch-gauge in his hands, the particular angle of a bell inspection, things he actually knew.

"Chamber logs are with the house steward," the porter said. "East corridor, second door."

The gate opened.

Cass walked through.

The compound's interior hit him like walking into a different room inside his own head. The interference was worse here — not louder, but denser, layered in a way that suggested the resonance chambers had been running exercises long enough to leave a residue in the air. He could feel the standing waves from two or three different sessions overlapping, none of them fully resolved, all of them pressing. His left hand went flat against his thigh. He kept walking.

The courtyard was stone-flagged and quiet, the afternoon light cutting across it at a low angle. A woman in a dark coat crossed the far end without looking at him. Somewhere in the east wing, someone was running a minor seventh, over and over, the interval climbing and not landing.

He knew that exercise.

He stopped walking.

The minor seventh, ascending, no resolution — Perin had drilled it for weeks the summer Cass was six, complaining that it was the most useless interval in the codex because it wanted to resolve so badly and the exercise refused to let it. He'd practiced it in the workshop while their father cast, the reaching note hanging in the air, unfinished, until Cass had started hearing it in his sleep.

Someone in the east wing was running Perin's exercise.

He turned toward the sound.

The east corridor was dim, stone-floored, smelling of the cedar wax used on instrument cases and something underneath it, older — the particular dry smell of a space where sound had been made and held for a long time. The minor seventh came clearer here, closer. Second door, the porter had said. Cass passed the first door without stopping.

He stopped at the second.

Knocked.

The minor seventh cut off.

A pause that had a specific quality — not the pause of someone who hadn't heard, but the pause of someone deciding.

"Come in," said a voice Cass had not heard in four years, and the under-note was completely, perfectly silent.

---

Perin was sitting at a writing desk near the window, a manuscript open in front of him, a pen in his hand. He looked up when Cass came in and his face did several things in quick succession that he then put away, one by one, until what remained was something careful and still.

He was twenty-six. He looked older. He'd grown a beard, short and neat, the kind you grew when you'd stopped thinking about whether you had one. His hands on the desk were the same hands Cass remembered — long-fingered, ink-stained, calloused at the same points from the same work. The room around him was orderly in the way of someone who had learned to keep things orderly because it was the one thing they could control: manuscript stacks squared, pen case closed, a cup of something cold on the corner of the desk.

"How did you get in," Perin said.

"Maintenance schedule discrepancy."

His brother looked at him for a long moment. "That's father's line."

"I know."

"He actually has discrepancies. You just used it."

"Yes."

Perin set the pen down. He didn't stand up. He looked at Cass with an expression that was almost familiar — the dry, sideways look he'd had when Cass was small and had done something inadvisable and Perin was deciding whether to be impressed or alarmed. "You're going to get thrown out when they check."

"Probably." Cass came further into the room. "I need to talk to you."

"I can see that."

"The co-custodial clause."

Something shifted in Perin's face. Not surprise — something that had been waiting. He said, "Sit down, Cass," in a voice that had gone quiet.

Cass sat in the chair across from the desk. The window behind Perin showed the compound's inner courtyard, a fountain that wasn't running, the east wing's stone face. The minor seventh was gone from the air. In its place, the interference from the resonance chambers pressed on steadily, that unresolved chord sitting in Cass's back teeth like a sliver of metal.

"How long have you known," Cass said.

"About the clause? Since I signed." Perin's hands were flat on the desk. "I read the whole contract before I ratified. I knew what it was."

"You knew it had no term."

"The codicil was sealed. I didn't know the exact language. But I knew it wasn't—" He stopped. Looked at the window. "I knew it wasn't what father told me it was."

"What did he tell you?"

"That it was temporary. That the Corda family needed a composer for a specific project, two or three years, and that the fitness question would go away when the project was complete." He said it without inflection. Twelve years of saying it to himself. "I was sixteen. I wanted to believe him."

Cass looked at his brother's hands on the desk. At the ink stains at the base of his first two fingers.

"You've been composing their contracts," he said.

"Yes."

"For twelve years."

"Yes."

"And she keeps extending."

Perin didn't answer. That was its own answer.

The interference from the resonance chambers pressed on. Cass's jaw ached with it. He said, "I went to Torvald Hess. The Compact. I've been learning the tally-song system — the way they document lame chord disputes in the outer districts. Arveth Corda-Laine sang your ratification."

Perin went still.

"She's on three of Torvald's disputed agreements," Cass said. "Same structure each time. Minor seventh amendment, no tonic resolution. The codicil floats loose from the original binding because the interval never closed. Torvald's cases are small — two of them settled, one is still pending. But the pattern is the same." He heard himself speaking faster and made himself slow down. "If your standing-wave record shows the same amendment frequency—"

"Cass."

"—that's demonstrable grounds. The Academy keeps acoustic records separate from the contract terms, they don't fall under the privacy clause, and if Arveth Corda-Laine left the amendment interval unresolved across multiple—"

"Cass." Perin's voice was quiet. Not sharp, not tired. Just quiet, in a way that stopped him. "The standing-wave record from my ratification is gone. I looked for it three years ago. Someone filed a correction notice — clerical error, standard form — and the original record was pulled."

Cass felt something drop out of his chest.

"Adwick," he said. "The registrar. Someone was there yesterday, before me. Not a student."

Perin looked at him. "How do you know that?"

"He told me. He was—" He stopped. "He was trying to warn me, I think. He couldn't say it directly. But he told me the codicil index was touched."

Perin was quiet for a moment. "They've been thorough," he said. "They've had twelve years to be thorough."

"So we find another angle." Cass heard the edge in his own voice and didn't try to smooth it. "The co-custodial clause itself — the fact that it's structured so the fitness review can never expire as long as you're in service. That's not a standard clause. Torvald knows a Court Singer, Dav Sorn, who's independent of Corda. If Sorn would examine the original agreement—"

"And say what? That the clause is aggressive?" Perin's voice was still quiet. That was almost worse than if he'd sounded angry. "Aggressive isn't void, Cass. The Academy will look at the interval, confirm the ratification was in proper form, and tell you the terms are the terms."

"The ratification had a lame chord."

"That you can't prove."

"The codicil index was pulled. That's evidence of—"

"That's evidence someone filed a correction notice." Perin picked up the pen and set it down again. The same gesture their father made with the folding cloth. "I've been through this. I've been through every version of this for twelve years."

The room was very quiet. Outside, the fountain didn't run. The interference from the resonance chambers sat in Cass's teeth.

"Then show me what you have," Cass said.

Perin looked at him.

"You've been through every version for twelve years," Cass said. "Show me what you found. Show me what's left."

A long pause. Perin's eyes went to the manuscript on the desk, and then to Cass, and then to something past Cass's shoulder that wasn't in the room. Then he opened the desk drawer and took out a bundle of letters, tied with a cord, and set them on the desk between them.

Cass recognized the handwriting on the top one. His father's.

"He writes every month," Perin said. "Has since the first year."

"He told me you write. He said—" Cass stopped. *Your brother is well, he writes.* "He never said he wrote to you."

"No. He wouldn't." Perin untied the cord. The letters fanned slightly, a thick stack, twelve years of monthly correspondence. "Read the third one from the top."

Cass picked it up. Opened it.

His father's handwriting, cramped and careful. A date four months after Perin's signing. Three paragraphs about the workshop — a new clapper cast for the Seventh of Ash Street, a problem with the bronze stock, the quarterly report filed on time. Then, at the bottom, in smaller script, as if added after:

*I have read the journals. All of them. I want you to know that what I did, I did knowing what was in them, and I did it anyway, and I have not found a way to say that to you that doesn't sound like an excuse. It isn't an excuse. I am telling you so that you know I understood the cost.*

Cass read it twice. Set it down.

"He knows what's in the journals," he said.

"Yes."

"What's in them."

Perin looked at him for a moment. "Something Aldric put into the first bells. Something in the sequence — the Civic Pitch, the cumulative harmonic. It's not in any codex." He paused. "Have you been hearing something in the morning pitch? Something low. Underneath."

The question arrived like a bell struck in a quiet room.

Cass said, "Yes."

Perin nodded slowly, as if something had been confirmed that he'd been carrying for a long time. "I thought you might. You always heard more than you should." He looked at the letters. "It's not a binding. It's not a command. Aldric composed it as — Cass, I've been trying to find the right word for four years. The closest I have is *evidence*. He composed evidence into the bells. Of what the system was built to do and what it was built to hide."

His jaw was tight. He hadn't noticed when it had started.

"The fitness review," he said. "The journals. That's what Maret Corda is actually holding. Not just the bronze stock claim. She knows what's in them."

"She's known for thirty years, from what I can piece together. She built the whole Corda empire partly around the fact that the Bellwright family would do anything to keep those journals closed."

"Including give her you."

Perin said nothing. He reached out and retied the cord around the letters, neat and precise.

"Read the intervals," he said.

"What?"

"In the letters. The technical observations — the chamber tunings, the maintenance notes, the things I describe. I've been writing to you since you were old enough to read them. Not the letters you've seen." He looked at Cass. "The ones I've been putting inside the letters you've seen."

Cass stared at him.

"The specific intervals I mention. The exact frequencies. The sequence they appear in." Perin's voice was still quiet, but something had shifted underneath it — not hope exactly, but the thing that lives in the same house as hope. "I've been building a record. Every lame chord I've composed for them, every amendment that didn't resolve, every codicil that floated loose — I've been encoding the pattern in the maintenance notes. The observations are real. The sequence they appear in is a message."

"To me."

"I didn't know when you'd be old enough to read it. I didn't know if you'd hear what I put in it." He looked at Cass steadily. "But you went to Torvald Hess. You learned the tally-song system. You found Arveth Corda-Laine across three disputed agreements." He paused. "Read them again."

Cass looked at the bundle of letters on the desk between them.

Twelve years of monthly correspondence. His father's cramped handwriting, the small script at the bottom of the third one from the top. *I have read the journals. I want you to know I understood the cost.*

And inside those, something else. A record Perin had been building in the gaps, in the technical observations, in the specific intervals he described — a message written in the only language Maret Corda's people wouldn't think to read, because it was too small and too patient and it had been waiting for someone who could hear what the intervals said.

Outside, the fountain didn't run. The resonance chambers pressed on with their unresolved chord, reaching and reaching and not landing.

Cass picked up the bundle.

"Read them again," Perin said.
