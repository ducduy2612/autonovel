# Chapter Five: Founding Day

The city slept an extra hour on Founding Day.

But the morning Civic Pitch came forty-five minutes after sunrise like any other day, and then the bells went quiet, and the market stalls stayed shuttered, and the song-brokers locked their offices, and even the most punctual of the inner-district merchants found reasons to linger over their bread. The city breathed out. It had been holding its breath for three hundred and sixty-four days, and now it let go.

Cass had loved this since he was small.

He stood at the workshop window while his father finished the last pre-holiday check on the bells — a ritual that took two hours and involved a tuning mallet, a pitch-gauge, and that focused silence his father wore like a second skin in the workshop. Outside, the Bell Quarter was doing its slow morning stretch. A woman from two doors down was hanging washing. A boy Cass didn't recognize was kicking a stone along the center of the lane, which he would never have done on a regular day — the acoustic properties of the Bell Quarter meant sounds off the stone pavement carried a surprising distance, and kicking things was considered a minor social offense. Today, nobody cared. The boy kicked his stone. The woman hung her washing. Somewhere past the rim, someone was singing — not a contract, not a ratification, just a song, rising and falling with the careless pleasure of a voice let off its leash.

Cass pressed his fingers against the cold glass and listened.

No under-note. Not yet. It was there in the background most mornings — faint, persistent — but this morning the Bell Quarter was quiet enough that even the faint version had nothing to attach to. The woman hanging washing wasn't singing. His father wasn't speaking. The boy with the stone was just a boy with a stone.

He could hear his own pulse.

"You'll be ready by the eleventh hour," his father said from behind him. Not a question.

"Yes," Cass said. Then, because it was Founding Day and he was in a reasonable mood: "Do you want me to carry anything? The mallet, or —"

"I'll carry the mallet."

"Right."

His father struck a test note on the Fourteenth — the bell that covered the lower Merchant Tier, the one that needed the most attention in autumn, its bronze contracting in the cold and pulling the pitch fractionally sharp. The note rang clean through the workshop, bounced off the vaulted ceiling, and settled into the floor.

Cass's chest hummed. The frequency lived in his ribs, right of center, as it always did with the Fourteenth. He had mapped the bells in his body years ago without meaning to — the Seventh of Ash Street in his left shoulder, the Twenty-Second in the base of his skull, the Fourteenth in his ribs, right of center. On good days it felt like a hand pressed flat against his chest.

Today was a good day.

"Clean," he said.

His father set down the mallet.

---

The Civic Square filled from the edges inward.

By the eleventh hour it was dense with people, standing in loose groups, the good-natured press of a city that had given itself permission to simply be present somewhere. Children ran between the adults' legs. A vendor near the south entrance was selling hot drinks from a cart — not a licensed stall, no contract required, just a woman with a copper pot and a ladle, and a line of people willing to pay a copper quarter for the warmth of it. Cass could hear the coins ringing as they changed hands. Each one a clean A, as they should. No flats in the square today.

His father stood beside him, hands in his coat pockets. They had walked from the Bell Quarter together without speaking much, which was the normal texture of their walking together. The cold had come in overnight — it sat in the limestone and stayed there — and the square smelled of woodsmoke and that clean sharpness of cold stone in morning light.

Around them, the city talked.

"—said the Forty-Third rang flat last year, but I didn't hear it—"

"You never hear anything, Da."

"I heard *that.*"

A group of Academy students had clustered near the fountain, still in their gray wool even on a holiday, which Cass found faintly depressing. One of them — a third-year he vaguely recognized, tall with a copper-colored scarf that probably wasn't technically a sash but was doing its best to imply one — was holding forth about the original composition.

"Aldric's sequence is a D-major resolution at the end, which is why it feels complete. The major seventh before the final chord is what gives it the emotional weight. It's quite sophisticated for the period."

"It's bells," said the girl next to him. "It's forty-seven bells. It doesn't have emotional weight, it has *mass.*"

Cass almost said something. He stopped himself. He didn't know them and it wasn't worth it and also the girl was at least partly right.

Closer to the tower, two small children were conducting an argument at a volume that suggested they'd been raised in the outer districts. Not a criticism.

"I want to stand at the front."

"You *were* at the front. You moved."

"I didn't *move*, I went to see the vendor and then came back and you were *in my spot.*"

"There's no spots. It's a square."

"There's *my* spot."

Their mother, a broad woman in dark wool with the stained hands of someone who worked near the dye-vats, put one hand on each of their heads without looking away from the Bell Tower. "Both of you," she said, "will be quiet in thirty seconds or we're going home and you can hear the bells from the lane."

Silence, immediate and complete, from both children.

Cass was a little impressed.

"The Seventh's been running sharp," his father said, beside him. Not to anyone in particular. Just noting it.

"Since the last week of summer," Cass said. "I heard it from the kitchen."

His father glanced at him. "You didn't say."

"I thought you'd already caught it."

"I had." A pause. "It'll need attention before the quarter-feast."

"I know." Cass hesitated. "Do you want me to —"

"After the holiday."

Right. After the holiday. He filed it away.

The Bell Tower stood at the square's center: forty feet of dressed limestone, the original stonework Aldric Bellwright had laid two hundred years ago, the bells hanging in their chambers above, invisible from here but present as large things always are, even unseen. Cass had climbed the tower twice, both times with his father, both times for maintenance work. Up close the bells were bronze and metal-dust and the faint char of old flux, and their size made you aware of your own smallness in a way that was not unpleasant.

The crowd quieted.

Not all at once. In waves, from the center outward, as sound moves. Someone near the tower stopped talking, and then the people around them stopped, and the silence spread through the square like a stone dropped in still water. Even the two children from the outer districts went still, the argument about spots apparently suspended by mutual agreement.

His father took his hands out of his pockets.

The first bell rang.

The sequence Aldric had composed ran forty-seven bells over twelve minutes, and Cass had heard it every year of his life, and it was still the thing he could not prepare for.

It started with the deepest bell — the Forty-Seventh, the one that covered the outer districts near the northern rim, the lowest note in the system — a sound that was less heard than felt, a pressure in the sternum that arrived before the ears had processed anything. The Forty-Seventh rang once, twice, three times, and then held its resonance while the crowd stood in it.

Then the Forty-Sixth entered, a minor third above.

Then the Forty-Fifth, a perfect fourth.

The sequence built slowly, each bell entering on its interval, the overtones of the earlier bells still ringing when the new ones joined. This was the craft of it — the bells were tuned not just to their own pitch but to the cumulative resonance of the bells that preceded them, so that each new entry found a harmonic already waiting for it in the air. Aldric had spent eleven years on this. His son had spent his whole life maintaining it.

"There," his father said, very quietly. Not to Cass. To himself, or to no one, or to the bells.

Cass looked at him.

"The Twenty-Ninth," his father said. He was watching the tower with the focused attention he gave the lathe. "Hear how it sits against the Thirtieth."

Cass listened. The Twenty-Ninth had just entered, a major second above the Thirtieth's lingering resonance, and the two together produced a shimmer in the overtone — not wrong, but particular, a texture you could spend years learning to notice.

"It's always done that," Cass said.

"Yes."

"Is it a problem?"

His father was quiet for a moment. The Twenty-Eighth entered above them. "No," he said. "It's what Aldric wanted. The shimmer is intentional. I spent six years thinking it was an error before I understood that."

Cass looked back at the tower. He stored that away — not the fact of it, but the shape of the thing, that his father had spent six years wrong about something and then understood it. That this was a thing that happened.

The air changed. By the time the middle bells were ringing, the sound had a thickness that was different from the sum of its parts, something that pressed gently against the inside of Cass's skull in a way that was not pain.

He had never had the words for this. He had tried, once, in a journal he kept at the back of his wardrobe. *The bells make the air heavier,* he had written, and then crossed it out. *The bells make the air present.* Closer. Still not right.

The twenty-third bell entered. The twenty-second. The twenty-first.

The under-note was still silent.

He knew it before he checked for it — as you know a headache is gone, the absence of a thing that had been so constant you'd stopped noticing it until it wasn't there. Even in the workshop, even on the best days, there was a thread of it somewhere, thin as a wire, running under everything.

Not now.

The fifteenth bell entered. The fourteenth — his ribs rang with it, right of center. The thirteenth.

Cass closed his eyes.

The silence where the under-note lived was not empty. It was full of something else, something the under-note's constant presence had been sitting on top of. Without it, he could hear the thing beneath the sequence more clearly: older and lower than any of the forty-seven bells, a resonance that ran through the ground under his feet and up through the bones of his legs and settled in his chest like a question he didn't have the vocabulary to answer.

 He had not told anyone.

The tenth bell. The ninth. The eighth.

He breathed.

The cold air came into his lungs and the sound came with it, and for twelve minutes on one day a year, Cass Bellwright stood in the Civic Square and was not in pain. The needle behind his left eye was gone, and with it the faint nausea that lived at the back of his throat on bad days. The greasy, curdled wrongness that attached itself to lies and coerced oaths and the small daily violations of a city that had built coercion into its foundations — gone.

The last three bells entered together: a chord that resolved the whole sequence into a major seventh, held, and then released.

Silence.

Real silence, the kind the square hadn't heard since last year.

Cass stood in it with his eyes closed.

He let the seconds pass uncounted. He had counted them when he was younger — had wanted to know exactly how long it lasted, had thought that knowing the number would help him hold onto it. It didn't. The silence lasted as long as it lasted, and then the crowd began to breathe again, and one of the children near the tower said *there,* very softly, to her sister, and the day resumed.

He opened his eyes.

His father was watching him.

Not watching a stranger, not even watching something interesting. The way you watch something you already understand and cannot speak about. His father's hands were in his coat pockets again, but Cass could see the shape of them through the wool — the left one was still, and the right one was moving, the small rhythmic tremor that lived in his father's right hand everywhere except the workshop.

His father looked at him for three seconds.

Cass looked back.

For a moment he thought his father was going to say something. The man's jaw moved, almost — a small shift, the preparation for speech.

Then he looked away, toward the Bell Tower, and the crowd began to thin around them, and the vendor with the copper pot was ladling out drinks again, and Founding Day continued.

"Soup?" his father said.

Cass blinked. "What?"

"It's almost noon."

"Right. Yes. Soup."

They walked, without further discussion, toward the south side where the bread sellers were.

---

They ate at the kitchen table.

His father had cooked the grain soup himself, as he always did on Founding Day: thick with the last of the autumn root vegetables, the recipe written in his mother's hand on a card that lived in the drawer under the bread board. The bread was the outer-district kind, with seeds in the crust, the kind his mother had preferred. His father always bought that kind on Founding Day. Cass had never asked why.

The silence at the main meal was city custom. You didn't talk over your food because a city where words had legal weight needed at least one daily pause from the labor of careful speech. Outsiders found it strange. Cass had grown up in it and found the alternative — the tourist families who ate in the square on Founding Day and talked through the whole meal without seeming to notice — somehow exhausting to watch.

He lasted about four minutes.

"The Academy student near the fountain," he said. "The one with the scarf. He said the sequence ends on a D-major resolution."

His father looked up.

"He's wrong, isn't he. The final chord isn't a resolution. It's a suspension."

His father set his spoon down. He looked at Cass with the expression he used when a student at the workshop said something almost correct. "The seventh resolves to the octave," he said. "That's a resolution."

"But the bass note doesn't move. The Forty-Seventh is still ringing when the final chord lands. So it's not a clean resolution, it's —"

"It's both," his father said. "The upper voices resolve. The bass holds." He picked up his spoon again. "Aldric was doing something specific with that."

"What?"

A pause. His father looked at his soup.

"What was he doing with it?" Cass said.

"Eat your bread."

Cass ate his bread. He ate it with more force than was strictly necessary. The soup was good — it was always good on Founding Day, something about the recipe and the cold and the fact that his father only made it once a year — but the bread required his full attention for a moment.

He tried again. "The thing beneath the sequence," he said, keeping his voice even. "The harmonic. The low one, below the Forty-Seventh's range. Can you —"

"Cass."

"I'm just asking if you can hear it."

His father's right hand was resting on the table beside his bowl. The tremor in it was visible, a small rhythmic motion like something ticking down.

"I hear what the bells are tuned to produce," his father said. "That's all I hear."

Which was not an answer to the question Cass had asked, and they both knew it.

The kitchen smelled of the soup and old wood and, faintly, from the wall to his left, bronze.

The locked room was through that wall.

Cass looked at the wall. He looked at his father. His father was looking at his soup with the focused attention of a man who had decided the soup required it.

"The locked room," Cass said.

The spoon stilled.

"I'm not asking to go in," Cass said, before his father could say whatever he was going to say. "I'm asking what the bells in there are tuned to. Because I can feel them from here and they're tuned to something, and it's the same interval as the harmonic in the Civic Pitch, and I've been hearing that harmonic for six months and I'd like to know what it is."

His father looked at him.

"I know what I heard," Cass said.

The silence that followed was not the comfortable Founding Day silence they'd had during the first four minutes. It was the other kind — the kind with weight and edges. His father's right hand was very still now, which was, Cass had learned, worse than the tremor.

"Eat your dinner," his father said.

"It's lunch."

"Eat it."

Cass picked up his spoon. He ate. His father ate. Outside, the city was celebrating — children in the lane, the distant sound of someone playing a stringed instrument in one of the squares, the general loosened quality of a city that had given itself an afternoon.

His father refilled his bowl from the pot on the stove without asking if he wanted more, which was how the man expressed care, and Cass held the bowl in both hands for the warmth of it and said nothing else.

The under-note was still quiet. He had maybe another hour of this.

He ate his soup and did not look at the wall.

---

The afternoon came in gray and cold, the light going early as it did in the deep part of autumn, the bowl's rim cutting off the low sun before the fourth hour past noon. His father went to the workshop — not to work, just to be there, the place the man went as other people went to gardens or to the window seat. Cass heard the door close. Heard the silence of the workshop when no one was working in it. A different silence from the workshop in use, fuller somehow, the bells on their iron frames holding their potential energy.

He went and sat outside the locked room.

He didn't plan to. He had told himself he was going to the kitchen for water, and then he was standing in the kitchen, and then he was sitting with his back against the wall beside the door, his knees up, his hands in his lap.

The door was old oak, the same age as the house, with a lock that was newer — the lock his father had added the winter after Perin left, a heavy iron thing with no key Cass had ever seen. He had tried the door once, when he was nine. He had not tried it since.

He sat with his back against the wall and listened.

Through the door: nothing. The absence of the ordinary sounds of a room in use — no breath, no movement, no creak of wood settling. Just the bells.

He could feel them. Three of them, hung in the dark on whatever frame his father had built, unrung for as long as Cass had been alive. Their resonance was faint enough that he might have imagined it on any other day, but the under-note was still quiet, and in the quiet he could feel the three bells the way you feel a tooth that's been loosened — not quite pain, a constant awareness of something that was there and shouldn't be touched.

They were tuned to the harmonic.

He had always known this in the abstract — everything in the Bellwright house was tuned to something, that was the nature of the family, the nature of the work. But sitting here with his back against the wall, in the quiet hour of Founding Day, he could feel it with a precision he hadn't had before. Not a note he could name. Not an interval in the Codex. A frequency his body recognized with that recognition of a word you've forgotten sitting just behind your teeth.

The under-note was still silent.

The harmonic from the locked room pressed gently against the wall between them.

Cass sat with it.

The Academy library. The blank pages at the back of Volume Four. The Voss paper that stopped mid-sentence. The interval that didn't exist in the Codex, sitting below F, the one that someone had been asking about in the Bellwright journals. Suvaine's voice, the previous afternoon, moving away from him down the Academy arcade.

Perin's letters. *Tell him the Seventh of Ash Street always ran a quarter-flat in autumn. He'll know what to do.* Perin writing about bells the way a man writes about home when he can't write about home.

He sat there until the cold from the stone floor came through his trousers and into his legs.

Then his father's voice came from the workshop doorway, through the house: "Dinner in half an hour."

Cass stood up. His knees ached. He pressed his once, briefly, then let it go. He looked at the locked door — the old oak, the new iron lock, the dark gap at the threshold where no light came through.

He went to help with dinner.

The under-note came back as he was setting the table, a thin thread of it from somewhere in the street outside — someone ratifying a small transaction at the end of the holiday, the system resuming its business. Faint and familiar.

He set the bowls out. His father came in from the workshop, hands washed, the tremor back in the right one.

"The shimmer in the Twenty-Ninth," Cass said. He was looking at the bowls, not at his father. "You said Aldric wanted it. The specific overtone when it sits against the Thirtieth."

His father stopped in the doorway.

"What does it do?" Cass said. "The shimmer. What's it for."

A long pause. Long enough that Cass looked up.

His father was looking at the wall between the kitchen and the locked room. Not at Cass. His hand rested against the doorframe — not gripping it, just touching it, the way he touched the workshop walls, a press that checked something was still there.

"It keeps the sequence honest," his father said.

Then he came in and sat down, and they ate dinner in the ordinary silence, careful and full — the silence that held things rather than rested from them.

Cass turned the answer over while he ate. *Keeps the sequence honest.* It was the most his father had said about the bells' design in years, and it was also, characteristically, an answer that opened into a larger question his father was not going to address. Keeps it honest how. Against what. Why would a sequence need to be kept honest. What would it mean if it weren't.

He thought about the three bells in the dark behind the wall, and what they were tuned to, and the way his father had looked at him in the square this morning — not like a stranger, not like something interesting, but like something he already understood and could not speak about.

His father knew something.

He had known it for ten years.

He was going to keep not saying it.

Cass put his spoon down. Picked it up again. Finished his dinner.

Outside, the city sang.
