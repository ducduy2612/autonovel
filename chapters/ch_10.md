# Chapter Ten: What Torvald Knows

Torvald listened the way the city's bowl caught sound — without effort, without interrupting, letting everything settle to the floor.

Cass talked for a long time. He laid out the inspection report, the language about *fitness*, Section Twelve and what it triggered, the old-alloy stock his father knew was insufficient and had known for years. Then the co-custodial clause, which he had to explain twice because his voice went flat the first time and he wasn't sure he'd said it in the right order. Then the lame chord — the way Perin's ratification note had sat a quarter-tone low, the specific texture of it, greasy and unresolved, the kind of thing you'd hear if you were listening for it and miss entirely if you weren't.

Torvald's expression didn't change. He cupped his left ear slightly and kept his eyes on Cass.

The sub-harmonic in the bells took the longest to explain. Cass had not said it aloud to anyone before — not the full version, not the part about how it felt like a question rather than a sound, how it was lowest on mornings when his father's hands shook worst at breakfast and then settled by the time they reached the workshop. Saying it to Torvald's face, in the Compact's back room with its smell of old dye and pine resin, felt like striking a test note against a practice bell he'd never tuned. He didn't know what the resonance would be.

He stopped talking.

Torvald was quiet. On the Dyer's Road, a cart horse shifted its weight and one of the wheel-spokes knocked against the cobble. Past the Fade Line, that sound was just a sound.

"The inspection was today," Cass said, because the silence had started to feel like something he'd broken.

"Yes," Torvald said.

"And."

"I heard you."

He stood up, crossed to the window, looked out at nothing in particular. His hands, blue-green from thirty years of dye work, hung at his sides. He turned back after a moment.

"You're describing a system that — look, it's like a dye that doesn't stain. You dip the cloth and it comes out changed but there's nothing on your hands, nothing in the vat, no trace of the color anywhere except in the fabric itself. Coercion without fingerprints." He said it the way Cass said *that fifth is flat* — as an observation, a thing the ear simply caught. "I've been saying that for twenty years, in one way or another, to anyone who'd sit still long enough. Nobody listened because I don't have credentials and because a retired dye-merchant talking about contract law sounds about as serious as a bell-maker giving advice on indigo."

The under-note had been with him since the inspection; it hadn't gone anywhere.

"The report uses the word *fitness*," Cass said.

"Section Four of the custodianship agreement. Yes. She's had this ready for a while." Torvald came back to the table and sat down heavily. "The question is what you do with it. Not what she's done. What *you* do."

"I've been trying to find a mechanism inside the Codex. Something that would void the clause, or contest the inspection findings —"

"I know what you've been trying." Torvald's voice was not unkind. "The system just moved against you. That's the part you have to sit with for a minute."

Cass sat with it for approximately three seconds. "So what else is there."

Torvald almost smiled. He reached under the table and produced a flat wooden box, the kind used for keeping tally-songs — notched records, strips of scored wood that the Academy didn't recognize and the Compact relied on completely. He didn't open it. He set it in front of him and pressed both palms on the lid.

"There's a group," he said. "Informal. No name they use publicly. I call them the Blank Pages Society because that's what they care about — the back of Volume Four."

Cass had heard of the blank pages. Every Academy student had. Forty-three of them, sitting at the end of the volume that was supposed to cover exceptional circumstances and contested frequencies. The official explanation was that they were reserved for future additions. The official explanation had been the official explanation for a hundred and sixty years.

"Scholars," Torvald said. "A few frustrated Court Singers. People who think the blank pages are deliberate — that someone left them empty on purpose and the reason for it matters. I know two of them personally." He paused. "One is a retired lecturer. Taught acoustical theory at the Academy for thirty years before they put her out. Her name is Mira Fen."

Cass heard the name and something in his chest did a strange, short movement. "Proctor Fen's mother."

"Yes."

He almost laughed. He stopped himself, but the shape of it was there — the specific, wrong-key humor of finding out that the proctor who had docked his marks on a contract theory exercise last spring was the son of the woman who thought the whole system had a gap in it. , but it gave the laugh somewhere to go.

"Who's the other one," he said.

Torvald looked at him steadily. "Dav Sorn."

The name landed wrong. Cass turned it over, checked the pitch of it, checked again. "The Court Singer."

"He's been attending meetings for three years. Hasn't acted on anything they've discussed." Torvald spread his hands briefly — not a shrug, just an acknowledgment of the shape of the thing. "He's there. That's not nothing."

Dav Sorn's voice in a courtroom — the precise, hedged sentences, the way he touched his throat when he was working through something. *In my assessment. Based on available evidence.* A man who had composed forty-three verdicts and been satisfied with eleven of them. Sitting in a room with scholars who believed the system had a deliberate hole in it. For three years. Doing nothing.

He didn't say anything. The thought sat in his chest.

"The Corda woman," Torvald said. "She knows something about the bells that she's been using as pressure. Not the inspection — that's new, that's the mechanism she chose to use the pressure with. The pressure itself is older. She's had it for thirty years."

"The journals," Cass said.

"Whatever's in them. Yes."

"They're in the locked room."

"Yes."

Cass looked at the tally-box under Torvald's hands. "My father has the key."

"Yes." Torvald's voice was level. He let the silence run for a moment. "I'm not telling you to steal from your father, boy. I'm telling you the answer is in that room."

The difference between those two statements was very small. Cass could hear it — the gap between them, a quarter-tone at most, the kind of interval that looked clean on paper and sat wrong in the air.

He nodded once.

The door opened.

Lenne came in carrying a fold of paper in one hand and her coat in the other, the coat still half-on because she'd been moving quickly and hadn't stopped to sort herself out. She looked at Torvald, then at Cass, then at Torvald again.

"You started without me," she said.

"You were late."

"I was gathering information." She dropped into the chair across from Cass and got her second arm into the coat sleeve with the focused efficiency of someone who has already moved on from the coat problem. She put the paper on the table. "My mother was approached."

Cass went still.

"The House of Corda," Lenne said. "They sent a representative to the Ashvale house four days ago. Asked her to ratify the co-custodial transfer — the bells moving from Bellwright custody to joint Corda-Bellwright management." She said it the way she said everything: precisely, with the Academy's careful diction, but underneath it something that wasn't quite level. "She refused."

"Good," Cass said.

"She's now under pressure." Lenne's finger moved on the table — a small, unconscious gesture, fingering an interval she wasn't playing. "The Corda-retained Court Singers filed a complaint with the Academy two days ago. About a verdict my mother sang in the Merchant Quarter last spring. They're claiming the interval resolution was irregular."

The spring verdict. Cass remembered Lenne saying something about it months ago — *I watched. The form was perfect. I've been thinking about it since.* He looked at her face. Her jaw was set.

"Was it?" he said.

"No." Short. Certain. "The form was correct. I was there. I watched her tune the ratification note against the reference bell before she sang a single phrase." She picked up the paper and put it down again. "The complaint is — it's a message. That's all it is."

"Does your mother know you're here?" Torvald said.

A pause. "She told me to stay out of it."

"And."

Lenne looked at him. "I'm here."

Torvald made a sound that was not quite approval and not quite anything else. He leaned back in his chair and the old wood creaked under him. "The pressure is spreading," he said, to both of them and neither of them specifically. "That's what this is. The Corda woman has the inspection mechanism ready, she's moving on the bells, and anyone who might complicate that process is getting a message. The Ashvale complaint is the message."

"What do we do about it," Cass said.

"Nothing yet. Vella Ashvale is the best Court Singer in this city and she knows how to handle a complaint. That's not your problem right now." He looked at Cass. "Your problem is the locked room."

Lenne looked at Cass.

He didn't explain. She didn't ask. That was one of the things about Lenne — she gathered information and waited to see if it was offered, and if it wasn't, she filed it and moved on. He'd noticed it early and found it useful in a way he hadn't expected.

They stayed another half-hour. Torvald talked through what Mira Fen had published — three papers, none of them through the Academy, all of them circulated in the Compact's network — about the blank pages. The argument, roughly, was that Volume Four's exceptional-circumstances section had been left incomplete because completing it would require acknowledging that the system couldn't account for coercion. That the forty-three blank pages were a gap that someone had decided was better left unfilled than filled honestly.

"She can't prove it," Torvald said. "She has the structural argument. She needs the source material."

"The journals," Cass said.

"The journals."

The under-note had settled to a dull, persistent weight by the time they left. Outside, the Dyer's Road had gone to evening light — amber off the limestone, long shadows from the vat-buildings, the smell of indigo and something chemical underneath it that caught at the back of the throat. Lenne walked with him as far as the Fade Line.

"Mira Fen," she said, when they were nearly there.

"Yes."

"She's been to our house. My mother knows her." She paused. "I didn't know about the Society."

"Torvald said your mother knows things."

Lenne was quiet for a moment. "She knows more than she says. That's not — I mean, I've known that for a while. She watches my father's verdicts and doesn't say anything." She stopped walking. They were at the Fade Line now, the invisible boundary where the city's careful acoustics began to take hold. On this side, the cart horse and the dye-vat sounds and the normal noise of a district that talked out loud. On the other side, the lower, considered murmur of the inner city, where you kept your voice down because voices carried. "I've been thinking about the spring verdict," she said. "The Merchant Quarter one."

"You said the form was perfect."

"It was. That's the part I can't stop thinking about." She looked at him — not asking him to solve it, just saying it to someone who would hear it. "The form was correct and the outcome was wrong. I watched the whole thing. I know the intervals. And the outcome was wrong."

He didn't say anything. She wasn't asking him to.

"Good luck," she said. Not what she meant, and they both knew it, and she turned back toward the Weavers' District while he crossed the Fade Line and walked home through a city that had gone quiet for the evening.

The house was lit when he got there. His father's shadow moved behind the workshop's high window — still working, the Civic Pitch long done, the bells for the day all struck and settled. Cass stood in the narrow passage between the workshop wall and the kitchen door and looked up at the shadow for a moment. Then he went inside.

He ate the bread and cold soup his mother had left covered on the kitchen table. The kitchen was quiet. The wall to the locked room was two feet from where he sat, plain plaster, a door in it that had been locked since he was four years old and that he had never once tried to open.

He had always told himself there was a reason he'd never tried. That his father would tell him when he was ready, when the time was right, when Cass had learned enough to understand what was in there.

He was fourteen years old.

His brother had been in service for ten years, composing contract music for the House of Corda in a resonance chamber with acoustics better than the Academy practice rooms, writing letters about the weather and the quality of the bells and what he'd had for lunch. His father's hands shook at the breakfast table and settled at the lathe and had been doing both for ten years. The bells were going to be taken, or shared, or transferred, or whatever word the co-custodial clause used to describe the thing that was happening. The Corda woman had a document and a mechanism and thirty years of pressure built from something she'd read in journals that sat twenty feet from where Cass was eating cold soup.

His father had not told him anything.

He was ready.

He set the bowl down. The sound of it on the table was ordinary and specific — ceramic on wood, a small resonance that died in less than a second. He pressed his palm flat on the table beside the bowl and felt the grain of the wood under his hand, the slight roughness of it, the temperature.

The bells in the locked room were old — three of them, small, hung from iron brackets, cast from the same alloy as the forty-seven originals. He had never heard them ring. He had stood close to the door sometimes, in the kitchen, and heard nothing from the other side, which meant either the room was acoustically sealed or the bells were silent. He had always assumed silent.

He was no longer sure he'd assumed correctly.

There was a pitch the body knew before the ear did. He had felt it his whole life, in the mornings when the Civic Pitch sounded, in the specific harmonic that sat beneath the sequence like a current under still water — not painful, not quite pleasure, something older than either.

He was still afraid. He was going to open the room anyway.

His father kept spares of everything. Organized by type, on a board by the lathe — workshop keys on the left side, house keys on the right, each one on a labeled hook in Eddan's careful hand. Cass had grown up watching him hang them there after use, take them down before work, replace them exactly. It was the most orderly thing about him. The rest of the workshop was controlled chaos, bronze filings in the floor grit, bells in progress crowding the iron frames, half-finished clappers on every horizontal surface. But the key board was precise.

He had seen the locked room's key on that board. He had seen it for years, a small brass thing on a hook labeled *K. rm.* in his father's shorthand, and he had never taken it down because he had told himself there was a reason he was waiting.

He was done waiting.

He got up from the table. He rinsed the bowl at the basin and set it to dry. He went to bed, because his father was still in the workshop and there was no point going for the key with his father twenty feet away and the night still early. He lay in the dark and listened to the workshop sounds — the low rasp of the lathe, a test strike against a practice bell, the clean note of it ringing through the wall and then fading.

He didn't sleep for a long time.

Sometime past midnight, when the workshop had been silent for hours, he heard movement below. Not the workshop — the kitchen. The particular creak of the floorboard near the basin, then the heavier one by the locked room door. His father's tread, unmistakable even through the ceiling, the weight of him shifting from one foot to the other.

Then the sound of a key in a lock.

Cass held still. The door opened — he couldn't hear it open, but he heard the change in the house's acoustics, the way the air moved differently when that room was unsealed. Then nothing for a long time. Long enough that Cass thought his father might have gone in and closed the door behind him.

But then: a page turning. Not one page — several, in quick succession, the sound of someone flipping through something they'd read before, looking for a specific passage. The rustle stopped. A pause. Then his father's breathing, audible through the ceiling in the dead-quiet house, the long uneven exhale of a man reading something he already knew by heart and reading it again anyway.

It went on for ten minutes. Maybe fifteen. Then the pages again, slower this time. The door. The lock. The creak of the kitchen floorboard, and then the stairs, and then his father passing his door without stopping, the tremor in his footsteps that the lathe couldn't steady.

Cass lay in the dark and breathed.

When he finally did, he dreamed of nothing. Or if he dreamed of something, he didn't remember it. What he remembered, waking in the gray early morning before the Civic Pitch, was the specific feeling of having made a decision — the clean, settled fact of it, sitting in his chest like a struck bell that had finally stopped vibrating.

He was going to open the room.

He lay still and listened to the city coming awake outside his window, and waited for the morning bells.
