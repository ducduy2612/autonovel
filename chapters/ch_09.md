# Chapter Nine: The Acoustician's Report

Cass had slept badly. The Sorn meeting sat in his chest — the shared frequency, the cautious hope — but when he came downstairs at dawn, his father was already in the workshop with a stranger.

The acoustician had arrived at seven, early enough that the morning Civic Pitch still hung in the air above the Bell Quarter, its last harmonics bleeding out of the limestone. His name was Ferren, around forty, with the careful posture of a man trained in breath work. He carried his reference instrument in a leather case strapped across his back and introduced himself with a card rather than a handshake — heavy stock, copper-inked, the Corda house mark in the corner.

Eddan took the card without reading it. "The tower's open. I'll show you the access sequence."

He did not introduce Cass. Cass followed anyway.

There was a woman already at the tower door.

Cass nearly walked into her. She was small and upright, dressed in dark navy, standing with her hands at her sides in the stillness of someone who had been waiting long enough to stop looking like they were waiting. Gray hair, close-cropped. A bronze ring on her left hand. She was looking at the Bell Tower's upper fittings, where the morning light caught the bronze bright, and she did not turn when they came up the path.

Then she did.

"Eddan," she said. One word. A statement, not a greeting.

Eddan stopped. "Maret."

Cass knew the name before his father said it. He had known it for ten years, the way you knew the name of a thing you'd been circling without being allowed to look at directly. He made himself stay still. He made himself breathe.

Maret Corda looked at him — not the way adults looked at children, deciding how much to explain. The way she'd looked at the tower fittings: assessing something that fell within her field of concern.

"The co-custodial clause grants oversight," she said. To Eddan, not to Cass. "I have the right to be present."

"I know the clause," Eddan said.

She nodded once and turned back to the tower door. The right existed. She was here. These were the same fact.

No under-note.

He had expected something — the greasy quarter-tone, the needle behind his eye. She was the reason Perin was gone. She was the reason his father's hands shook. She was standing six feet from him in the morning light and the air between them was acoustically clean, and that was somehow worse than anything he'd prepared for.

The four of them went up the stairs.

---

Ferren set his case on the floor in the center of the bell chamber and opened it with the care of a man who had done this many hundreds of times and had not stopped being careful. Inside, nested in fitted leather: a reference mallet, a resonance meter in a brass housing, a ledger already open to a fresh page.

"I'll work sequentially," he said. "Registered pitch first, then resonance decay. I'll note any deviation above a quarter-tone." He picked up the mallet. "The decay curve tells me more than the strike. A repaired bell will often ring true on impact and then drift. That's what I'm listening for."

He struck the first bell.

The sound filled the chamber completely, immediately — a high, clear D-sharp that rang off the limestone vault and came back enriched, the other bells answering in sympathy. Cass felt it in his sternum before he heard it with his ears. Ferren watched the needle. He wrote. He made no other movement for nearly a minute.

He moved to the second bell.

The morning light came through the east window in a rectangle across the floor, pale gold. The second bell was the Ash Street sequence bell, a B-natural, and when Ferren struck it the sound had a quality Cass had never managed to describe adequately — something in the overtone series sitting exactly right against the limestone.

"Good sustain on this one," Ferren said, not quite to himself. "The chamber geometry helps. You get a natural extension on the B-natural because the east wall's at the right angle." He moved on.

"What's he writing?" Cass said, low, to his father.

Eddan didn't look at him. "Pitch and condition."

"Both columns?"

"Don't."

Cass looked at his father's profile. The set of his jaw. He looked at the floor.

Maret stood on the far side of the chamber, near the east wall. She had not moved since they'd come up the stairs. She watched Ferren's meter needle with the attention she would have given a Court Singer mid-ratification, and she did not look at Eddan, and Eddan did not look at her.

Eddan's hands were at his sides. In the workshop, they had a life of their own — moving to the next tool before the last motion had finished, the tremor settling into purposeful work. Here they were simply still. A different stillness. Something held.

Cass knew which bells were coming. He had grown up with them the way you grew up with the layout of your own house — not by memorizing it but by living in it until the knowledge was in your feet. He knew the sequence, the positions, the register of each bell's voice. He knew which six had been repaired. The first repaired bell was the twenty-fourth.

Ferren was at the eleventh. Cass did the arithmetic without wanting to.

The bells came in their sequence. The silence between the four of them had its own texture by now — not the comfortable silence of people with nothing to say, but something denser, a silence that knew what it was waiting for.

"Does the decay curve on the repaired bells look different," Cass said.

Eddan's head turned slightly. A warning in the angle of it.

Ferren glanced at him. "Depends on the repair. A good repair on a cracked bell — if the craftsman knows what he's doing — the decay profile can be nearly identical to an unrepaired bell. The difference is usually in the overtone series, not the fundamental." He looked at the meter. "You have to know what you're listening for."

"What are you listening for?"

"Drift," Ferren said. "A repaired bell sometimes shows a slight slope in the decay — the repaired section vibrates at a fractionally different rate than the original material. It's small. You'd miss it in a busy market." He tapped the meter housing. "You don't miss it in here."

Cass looked at his father.

Eddan was looking at the bell.

The twenty-fourth hung between two originals, and you would not know it by looking. Three years of Eddan's work had made the repair invisible: the new metal seated into the old casting, the clapper rebalanced, the pitch-collar adjusted by fractions until the registered frequency was exact. Cass had watched him do it. He had stood in the workshop on a winter afternoon and watched his father's hands — steady, absolutely steady, the tremor gone — work the alloy into the bell's damaged shoulder with a patience that had nothing of grief in it. Only craft.

Ferren raised the mallet.

He struck.

The bell rang true. Completely, cleanly true — the registered D-flat blooming into the chamber air with no hesitation, no thinness. The meter needle swung to the correct position and held there, steady as a plumb line, and Cass's jaw was tight.

Ferren stood with his head tilted, watching the needle. "Clean strike. Good pitch." He watched the decay. "The sustain is —" He paused. Wrote something. "Slightly shorter than the adjacent originals. Not significant on its own." He wrote again. "I'll note it."

"What does that mean for the report," Cass said.

"Cass." Eddan's voice was flat.

"I'm asking a technical question."

Ferren looked at him with the equanimity of a man who had been asked technical questions by anxious families many times before. "It means the repair is documented. The bell rings to its registered pitch. The repair is noted." A pause. "The report reflects what is, not what was."

He wrote the asterisk. Cass could see the motion of the pen, the two small strokes. *Repair confirmed, original alloy components unverified.*

The bell rang true. His father had made it ring true. Three years ago, with hands that shook at the dinner table and settled at the lathe, Eddan Bellwright had repaired this bell until it was indistinguishable from the original by any measurement any instrument could take.

The bell rang true. That was not the point.

Cass pressed his Maret's eyes moved to Eddan's face. Eddan was looking at the bell.

Then Cass was watching through the gap between two iron frames, Maret thinking herself unobserved, and her hand went to the twenty-fourth bell. Not the way Ferren touched it, clinical, testing. The way Eddan touched a bell when he was listening for its history. Her fingertips found the join where the old alloy met the new, the seam Eddan had made invisible, and she traced it. Slowly. The way you'd trace a scar on someone you used to know.

Her lips moved. No sound came out, nothing Cass could hear, nothing the under-note could register. But her mouth shaped something, and from the angle he was watching it looked like a note. A single interval. Something learned from a father who tuned by ear.

Then her hand dropped. Her face settled back. She clasped her hands behind her back and turned to watch Ferren, and the moment was over.

Cass stood among the bells and thought: *she can hear them too.*

Not the way he could. Not the under-note, not the sub-harmonic. But the bronze. The alloy. The difference between a bell that was whole and a bell that had been broken and mended. She had known the six repaired bells before Ferren tested a single one. She had known because she had touched them, and her hands knew bronze the way her father's hands had known it, and the Academy had told her that knowledge didn't count.

He looked at her and looked at his father's back and held that for a moment — the two of them, Eddan and Maret, both with hands that knew bronze, both in this room, both watching a man with a meter do what their hands could have done in thirty seconds. What the system required instead of what would have been true.

Ferren worked through the remaining bells. Each repaired one rang true. Each received the asterisk. At the fortieth bell — an original, ringing with the warmth of two-hundred-year-old bronze — he paused.

"The repair work is exceptional," he said. "Whoever did it understood the alloy." He glanced at Eddan. "The decay slope is minimal. Most repair work I see, the drift is obvious within the first two seconds. This held for nearly four."

Eddan said, "Thank you."

Flat. Ferren nodded and moved on.

The sixth repaired bell was the forty-first. When Ferren struck it, Cass was watching his father's hands. At his sides. Still. The stillness of a man who had decided this was the place where his hands didn't shake, and he was going to hold to that, and it was costing him something.

The forty-first bell rang true.

Ferren made the asterisk.

He closed the ledger. He capped the meter. He fitted the mallet back into its case and pressed the catches down with both thumbs, and the soft click of them was the only sound in the chamber.

"Six bells have been repaired rather than recast," he said. "The repairs are technically sound. The bells ring true to their registered pitch." He paused. "The custodianship agreement specifies maintenance of the original forty-seven bells. A repaired bell is not the same instrument as the original."

He said it without apology and without particular emphasis. A measurement.

"I'll have the report to the House of Corda by this afternoon."

Eddan said, "I see."

Maret said nothing. She moved to the stairwell and went down without waiting for Ferren. Her footsteps were unhurried on the left-winding stairs. They faded.

Ferren nodded once and followed.

The tower was quiet. Forty-seven bells hung in their sequence, and the light through the high vents had gone flat and white with midday, and Cass's father stood in the middle of it with his hands at his sides.

"What does fitness mean," Cass said.

Eddan turned toward the stairwell.

"Dad." Cass took a step after him. "What does fitness give her."

Eddan stopped. He didn't turn around. His hands — Cass could see them from here — had started shaking. The fine tremor. The one that didn't show at the lathe.

"The right to determine whether the current arrangement is —" Eddan stopped. Tried again. "Whether we're —" He didn't finish that either. "That's what fitness means."

"And if she determines it isn't."

"Co-custodial oversight. Section Twelve."

"Her people in the tower. Every morning. For the Civic Pitch." Cass heard his own voice going flat, the way it went when he was trying to hold something very still. "So she'd have people with their hands on the bells that set the reference pitch for every contract ratified in the city. Every day. Until the old-alloy stock is sufficient to recast six bells." He stopped. "Which it isn't. And hasn't been."

Eddan turned then. His face was composed, his hands were not, and he looked at Cass with the specific exhaustion of a man who had known this conversation was coming and had spent ten years hoping he'd die before it arrived.

"How long has the stock been too low," Cass said.

"Seven years."

"She's known for seven years that she had this."

"Maret has known the stock was low since before I did."

The quiet in the chamber was complete. Forty-seven bells, the light flat, the wind through the arched windows with its breath-across-a-jar sound.

"And Perin's been there for ten." His voice went somewhere he hadn't meant it to go. "So you've spent seven of those ten years knowing she had this and — "

"Don't." Eddan's voice cracked on the word. One syllable. It landed in the room like a struck bell, resonating in the silence, and neither of them moved.

Cass looked at his father's shaking hands. He looked at his father's face. *He knows. He has always known. He has been knowing it every morning at the lathe and every evening at the dinner table and every night alone with Perin's letters, and knowing it has not moved him.*

"I'm going to Torvald," Cass said.

Eddan closed his eyes.

"He knows about the co-custodial clauses. The Compact has records — "

"I know what Torvald Hess knows."

"Then you know he might be able to — "

"Cass." His father opened his eyes. Something in his face that was not quite a plea and not quite a warning. The face of a man asking his son to stop before they both went somewhere they couldn't come back from. "Not today."

Cass looked at him for a long moment.

"The report comes this afternoon," he said.

"I know."

"So when."

Eddan turned back to the stairwell. He started down. His footsteps were careful on the worn stone, one hand on the wall.

Cass stood at the top of the stairs and listened to his father descend and did not follow.

---

The report arrived at half-past three. Eddan brought it inside, set it on the kitchen table without opening it, stood there a moment, then went to the workshop.

Cass picked it up.

Four pages, Ferren's neat hand on heavy Corda stock. The first two pages were technical — each bell by number, pitch, condition. The six repaired bells marked with asterisks: *repair confirmed, original alloy components unverified.* The third page was the analysis. The fourth was the conclusion.

He read the conclusion twice.

*Given the above findings, the House of Corda respectfully notes that the current state of six bells in the forty-seven-bell civic sequence constitutes a discrepancy between the present condition of the instrument set and the terms of the Bellwright-Corda custodianship agreement, which specifies the maintenance of the original forty-seven bells in their original composition. This discrepancy may be resolved by one of two means: first, by recasting the six affected bells from original-alloy bronze to restore compliance; or second, by the invocation of co-custodial oversight under Section Twelve of the custodianship agreement, to remain in effect until the discrepancy is resolved to the satisfaction of both parties. The House of Corda defers to the Bellwright family as to the preferred resolution, while noting that the fitness of the current arrangement to fulfill the terms of the agreement is a matter that warrants prompt attention.*

*Fitness.* Sitting in the middle of that last sentence like a stone dropped in still water.

He set the report down. He sat with it in front of him and the kitchen going dim around him. Seven years. She had known the stock was insufficient for seven years and she had waited, and the waiting was its own kind of craft — the patience of someone who understood that the system moved slowly and had planned accordingly. She had stood in the tower this morning and watched a man with a meter confirm what her hands had already told her, and her face had given nothing, and she had gone down the stairs without waiting.

He thought about Perin's ratification line. The small handwriting. The quarter-tone flat.

He thought about eleven satisfactions out of forty-three.

He stood up. He walked to the door of the locked room.

He had passed this door every day of his life. Solid oak, old enough that the grain had gone dark with years of hand-oil at the latch, the lock a newer addition — the new metal slightly brighter than the surrounding wood. He pressed his ear to it.

The three bells inside were humming.

Not the way the workshop bells hummed in sympathy with the Civic Pitch. This was something else. A low vibration he felt in his cheekbone and jaw before he heard it with his ear. Sub-harmonic. Below the range of the morning Pitch, below the range of anything he could name.

It pressed against him like a question.

He stood with his ear against the door and the question pressed against his jaw and the word *fitness* sat in the report on the kitchen table behind him, and the lathe ran in the workshop, and his father worked in the only place his guilt couldn't reach him, and somewhere across the city in the Merchant Tier compound Maret Corda had a copy of the same four pages.

He thought about her fingertips on the seam of the twenty-fourth bell. Her mouth shaping a note that made no sound.

The humming did not change. It had no urgency. It had no answer. It was just there, the way it had always been there, waiting for him to have the vocabulary.

He lifted his ear from the door.

He went to find Torvald.
