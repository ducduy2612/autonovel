# Chapter Four: The Codex and the Summons

The Academy library smelled of binding glue and old paper and the close, dry air of a room where windows were kept shut to protect the manuscripts. The silence in here was different from the workshop's silence. The workshop held the memory of the lathe, the cooling tick of bronze. The library's silence was enforced — the shelves absorbed what little noise there was and gave nothing back.

He had forty minutes before afternoon theory.

The official founding record took up two pages in the Civic Index. Aldric Bellwright, bronze-caster and amateur acoustician, settled in the limestone bowl with a guild of craftsmen. Spent eleven years casting forty-seven bells from a proprietary alloy. Composed the Civic Pitch sequence. Taught it to his guild. The system spread because it worked. Clean and thin, the record told him almost nothing he didn't already know.

He moved to the academic papers.

Three dealt with the harmonic structure of the original bells — not the sequence itself but the underlying mathematics of how Aldric had tuned them. A fourth line in the catalog index read: *Fen, M. — Structural Analysis of Foundational Pitch Variance — WITHDRAWN.* No shelf location. He filed it away. Two required a request from the restricted shelf, which meant signing his name in a ledger and waiting while the librarian's assistant climbed a ladder. He used the time to read the third, which turned out to be inconclusive in the specific way of scholarship that had run out of evidence before it ran out of confidence. A man named Priel, forty years dead, had measured the bells' overtone series and found that the intervals exceeded standard temperament by a factor he couldn't account for. He had concluded, without apparent embarrassment, that this was probably a casting error incorporated into the system by accident, and that the system worked despite it.

Cass set the paper aside.

The restricted papers arrived. The librarian's assistant — a boy two years younger than Cass, collar buttoned too high — set them on the table with the careful neutrality of someone who wanted to ask what they were for and had been trained not to. Cass thanked him. The boy retreated.

The second paper was better. A woman named Aldeth Sour, writing sixty years ago, had approached the problem from the receiving end: not the bells' construction but their effect on listeners in the Civic Square. She had documented that listeners standing within forty feet of the Bell Tower during the Civic Pitch experienced a measurable increase in what she called *sympathetic resonance* — a vibration in the chest cavity that persisted for several minutes after the sequence ended. She had concluded this was a property of the limestone bowl's natural amplification and had nothing to do with the bells' specific tuning.

She had been wrong, Cass suspected. But usefully wrong. She had measured the right thing and drawn the wrong conclusion from it.

He wrote *chest resonance — limestone or bell structure?* in the margin and turned to the third paper.

The name registered slowly. *Korven Voss, Lecturer in Acoustic Theory, Cantamura Academy of Civic Harmony.* He thought of Senna Voss, the throat-remedy woman in the Tanner's Quarter. He filed the question and kept reading.

For ten pages, Korven Voss had been doing something extraordinary. He had started with the overtone series Priel had dismissed as a casting error and traced it back — not to the bells' physical dimensions, the standard approach, but to the alloy itself. He had gotten a sample of the old bronze from somewhere and had it analyzed, and what he found was that the alloy contained a trace mineral compound that altered how the metal vibrated at the fundamental frequency. Not just at the fundamental. At the sub-fundamental. Below the range of the note itself, below the range of the instrument, below anything a trained ear should be able to detect, the alloy produced a second vibration. Not an overtone. An undertone. Something that ran beneath the bell's voice the way a river ran beneath ice — present, moving, invisible from above.

Voss had been building toward something. Page ten ended mid-sentence: *—which would suggest that Aldric's original composition was not simply a reference sequence but a structured harmonic that, when heard in its complete form, would produce in any listener with sufficient sensitivity a—*

Page eleven was blank.

Pages twelve through thirty were blank.

Cass turned them one at a time. The paper was old, edges slightly foxed, but the blankness was not decay. The pages had never been written on. He held one up to the light from the high window. No impression from a pen. No ghost of text. Nothing.

He set it down.

He was about to close the folder when his eye caught the catalog slip tucked inside the back cover — a standard Academy record, listing the paper's acquisition date, its classification, its restrictions. At the bottom, under a column marked *cross-references*, two entries. The first was the Priel paper he'd already read. The second was a single line: *M. Fen, unpublished, withdrawn.*

No title. No date. No subject notation. Just the name and those two words.

He stared at it for a moment. *Withdrawn* was different from *restricted*. Restricted meant someone had decided access should be limited. Withdrawn meant someone had pulled it from the record entirely — and then, for some reason, left the cross-reference behind.

He wrote the name in his notes. Then he gathered his papers, returned the restricted documents to the desk, and went to find a practice room.

---

The practice rooms in the east wing were small and close-walled, each one lined with acoustic panels meant to prevent sound from bleeding between rooms. They mostly worked. From the next room, faint but clear, someone was running scales — the same ascending figure, over and over, with a slight drag on the sixth.

Cass sat at the small writing table and spread his notes.

He tried to write down the harmonic from the Bell Tower. Standard notation gave him five ledger lines and four spaces. He wrote the F below the bass clef staff — the lowest note the Codex recognized as usable in a binding sequence. The harmonic sat below that. Not slightly below. Not a quarter-tone, the way the under-note did. This was different. Below the staff, below the ledger lines, below the range of the lowest bell in the civic sequence, below the range of anything he had been taught to name.

He wrote the F. He drew a downward arrow beneath it. He wrote: *below F.*

Two words. Accurate and useless, like a map that only showed you something existed past the edge.

"What is that."

He turned.

Lenne Ashvale stood in the doorway with her composition folder under one arm and her copper practice sash over her shoulder. She was looking at his notation with her head tilted slightly, the way she did when something didn't resolve as expected.

"I've been looking for you," she said. "The contract exercise — we were supposed to work through the third-clause structures together. I asked Devin but he composes like he's afraid of the interval."

"Sorry. I was in the library."

She came in and set her folder on the table. She looked at the note on the page. In the next room, the student dragged the sixth again.

"Your father asked you to work on this?"

"I'm working on something for him." Which was true in the sense that the Voss paper's blank pages were true — accurate as far as it went.

She looked at the notation another moment. Then she sat down across from him, pulled the page toward her, and studied it with the focused quiet she brought to anything that didn't resolve immediately.

"That's below the bass staff," she said.

"Yes."

"The Codex bottoms out at the F below the staff. Aldric's second bell." She pushed the page back. "So this is either a transcription error or something that doesn't exist."

"It exists. I heard it."

She looked at him. He looked back. Her eyes moved slightly — tracking intervals in her head, fingering through progressions against her thumb.

"Where?"

"Bell Tower. Three days ago."

"The Bell Tower has forty-seven bells. The lowest is the Second Bell of the Founding sequence, tuned to—"

"E-flat, yes, I know. This was below that."

"By how much?"

Cass thought about it. "I don't have the vocabulary. That's the problem."

Lenne was quiet for a moment. The sixth dragged in the next room.

"Volume Four," she said. "Has blank pages at the back." She said it the way she might say *the binding requires two voices* — a piece of information being set on the table.

"I know."

"Forty-three of them. I counted once. In the third year, when we study the exceptional circumstances chapter. I counted because the chapter ends mid-argument and I wanted to know how much space was left before the back cover." She paused. "Mid-argument. Which argument?"

"The one about frequencies that fall outside codified intervals. The chapter says" — she opened her composition folder, turned past the exercises, and found a page of her own notes — "*any frequency not codified in Volumes One through Three exists in a state of acoustic ambiguity, pending formal recognition by the Academy's Committee on Interval Expansion.* Then a footnote. Then the chapter ends."

"When was the Committee on Interval Expansion last convened?"

She looked up. "I don't know that it was."

"It wasn't. Not once in a hundred and sixty years."

She absorbed this. In the next room, the ascending scale stopped.

"So the Committee exists on paper," she said.

"The Committee exists on paper, Volume Four has forty-three blank pages, and the Voss paper stops mid-sentence on page ten." He spread his notes. "Korven Voss. Forty years ago. Lecturer in Acoustic Theory. He found an undertone in the old bronze alloy — not an overtone, an undertone, below the bell's fundamental — and he was building an argument about what it meant when the paper just ends. Thirty blank pages."

Lenne looked at the notes. She reached across and turned the page with the downward arrow toward her.

"*Below F,*" she read.

"I know."

"That's your argument? *Below F?*"

"That's all I have."

She sat back. She was doing the composing-face again — not quite a frown, more like the expression of someone listening to a chord that hadn't resolved yet.

"Voss is in the restricted collection?"

"The last twenty pages are blank. Someone restricted a paper with thirty blank pages."

"That's not nothing," she said. "That's someone deciding the blank pages needed restricting."

Cass looked at her.

"What," she said.

"I didn't think of it that way."

"The content isn't dangerous. The content is nothing. So the restriction is about the question, not the answer." She picked up her pen and wrote something in the margin of her notes — small, precise. "Which means whoever restricted it knew there was an answer and didn't want people looking for it."

"Or knew there was an answer and was afraid of it."

"Those are the same thing."

"No they're not," Cass said.

She looked up. "Explain."

"Afraid of it means they know what it is. Didn't want people looking means they might not. One of those is a cover-up and one is just — institutional caution."

"Rector Suvaine has blocked three attempts to complete Volume Four," Lenne said. "Is that afraid of it or institutional caution?"

"I don't know yet."

She made a small sound. Not quite skeptical. Something more like *fair enough.*

"There was a catalog cross-reference," Cass said. "In the Voss paper's folder. *M. Fen, unpublished, withdrawn.* No title. No date. Just the name."

Lenne was still. "Withdrawn is different from restricted."

"Someone pulled it from the record and then left the reference behind. Either they forgot or they wanted someone to find it."

"Or they were interrupted."

He hadn't thought of that. He wrote *M. Fen* at the top of a fresh page and put it aside.

"The blank pages in Volume Four," she said. "You think they're connected to the blank pages in the Voss paper."

"I think Voss was writing toward something that would have filled some of them."

"And the Bell Tower harmonic is the thing he was writing toward."

"I think it's related. I don't know how."

She turned her pen over in her fingers, once. "Does your father know you're looking at this?"

"He knows I heard something in the Bell Tower." Cass paused. "He didn't seem surprised."

She was quiet for a moment. Then she said: "My mother sang the Merchant Quarter verdict last spring. I watched from the gallery." She turned a page in the folder without reading it. "The form was perfect. Every interval correct. I've been thinking about it since."

"What was the verdict?"

"A debt dispute. The creditor won. The debtor was a man from the Weavers' District who couldn't afford a song-broker to review the original contract, so he'd ratified it himself, and there was a lame chord in the third clause that he didn't catch. It held him without holding the creditor." She paused. "The verdict was correctly sung. That's not what I've been thinking about."

Cass waited.

"The form being correct doesn't tell you anything about whether the original contract was just. The verdict was correctly sung. The original contract had a lame chord. The man lost his trade license." She set her pen down. "The system worked exactly as designed. I don't know what to do with that."

"Neither do I. But I think it's the same question."

She looked at him.

"The blank pages," he said. "The Voss paper. The Bell Tower harmonic. They're all the same question. The Codex has edges, and something exists past them, and nobody has written it down, and the people who know that have decided not to."

"That's a very large claim."

"I know."

"You heard one sound in the Bell Tower."

"I've been hearing sounds that aren't in the Codex for four years," Cass said. "The under-note." He watched her face. "You know what that is."

"I've heard students use the term," she said carefully.

"It's not a student term. It's what I call the frequency I hear when someone sings a lie. Or sings something they've been forced to sing. It sits between F and F-sharp, a quarter-tone that doesn't exist in any scale the Academy teaches, and it hurts." He said it flatly. "It's been hurting since I was ten. The Bell Tower harmonic is different — it doesn't hurt, it's lower, it's older — but it's the same kind of thing. Something that exists outside the Codex."

Lenne was looking at him with an expression he couldn't read.

"You hear it every time someone lies," she said.

"Every time someone sings a lie. Or a coerced truth. There's a difference in texture."

"And you've never told anyone."

"My brother knew. Knows." He picked up his pen. "It's not useful information. I can't prove it. The Court Singer validates the oath as clean and that's the record."

"But you know."

"I know."

She was quiet for a long moment. He could see her working through something — not with the composing-face, something more careful.

"The man from the Weavers' District," she said finally. "If you had been in that courtroom. When my mother sang the verdict."

"I would have heard whether the original contract had a lame chord, yes. If anyone sang it aloud."

"And if they had?"

"I still couldn't prove it."

She picked up her pen. She looked at the exercise page. Then she put the pen down again.

"The third-clause exercises," she said. "Can we actually do them now, or are you still thinking about the blank pages?"

"I'm always thinking about the blank pages. I can do both."

She made a dry sound — barely a breath. "Right. Show me the problem with Devin's approach."

"He resolves to the tonic when the clause calls for a suspended fourth."

"The tonic is correct per the Codex."

"The tonic closes the binding before the third clause has been stated. The whole thing becomes provisional." He pulled the exercise sheet toward him. "It's technically correct and functionally wrong."

"The Codex says tonic resolution after the second clause."

"The Codex assumes the second clause is complete. Look at the exercise — the second clause has a conditional embedded in it. If the condition hasn't been met, the clause is still open. You can't resolve to the tonic off an open clause."

She leaned over to look at the exercise. He could see her reading it properly this time.

"The conditional is in the subordinate phrase," she said slowly.

"Second measure. After the ratification."

"The Codex notation doesn't mark it as a conditional."

"It's marked with a minor seventh — the unstable interval, the one that has to resolve within the same breath. When you see an unresolved minor seventh in a subordinate phrase, the clause is open."

She took the exercise sheet and read the second measure again. "I didn't catch that."

"It's in the third line."

"Devin definitely didn't catch it."

"Devin doesn't read subordinate phrases. He goes straight to the resolution."

She wrote *unresolved m7 = open clause* in her margin with two underlines. "How did you know to look for it?"

"The header says the parties are in a ten-foot resonance chamber. A fifth at that ceiling height creates a standing wave that interferes with the third clause. You'd need a fourth instead. The fifth tells you something's wrong with the standard approach, which means you look harder at the clauses."

"The Codex says fifth."

"The Codex footnotes say fourteen feet minimum for a standard chamber. Chapter Seven, footnote thirty-one."

She wrote *Ch. 7, fn. 31* in the margin. "I didn't read the footnotes."

"The footnotes are where they put the things they want you to miss."

"That's a very cynical way to read a legal textbook."

"It's worked so far."

She looked at him for a moment. Then she looked back at the exercise. "So the correct approach is — fourth instead of fifth at the second-clause resolution, which keeps the third clause open, which means the tonic can't come until after the third clause completes."

"Right."

"Which means Devin's contracts, if anyone ever actually used them, would be provisional from the second clause forward."

"If the ceiling was ten feet, yes."

She turned the exercise sheet over in her hands. "Did you catch it the first time?"

"No. I caught the standing-wave problem and worked backward."

"I would have caught it if I'd read the footnotes."

"Probably."

"I always read the footnotes," she said, with a slight edge that was almost offense.

"You didn't read them this time."

"I was looking for you." She said it flatly, without embarrassment, and moved on. "Show me the fourth-instead-of-fifth substitution. I want to see how it changes the third clause structure."

They worked through the remaining exercises. His shoulders loosened without him noticing. The room settled into the quiet of two people working in parallel, close enough to consult, far enough not to disturb. The student in the next room finally corrected the sixth, ran the scale clean twice, and stopped.

She caught an error in the fifth exercise that he had missed — a codicil amendment that extended past the breath limit, which made it legally void under standard form.

"How long is a trained singer's breath?" he asked.

She looked up. "Four seconds minimum, per the Codex."

"Has anyone measured it? Actually measured it?"

"That's what the Codex says."

He looked at the exercise. "Korven Voss and Senna Voss. The throat-remedy woman in the Tanner's Quarter. Her remedies work — my brother used them. If she's related to Korven, she might know something about the paper."

Lenne was quiet for a moment. "Her mother uses them before high-stakes hearings. The Academy ones don't do anything." She paused. "If the answer to the blank pages isn't in the Academy, that would make sense. Someone decided not to put it there."

"The Compact uses folk intervals," Cass said. "Torvald Hess — the dye-merchant, the one who runs the outer-district dispute system — he's been settling contracts with uncodified intervals for twenty years. The Academy says they don't count. They work anyway."

"My mother's cases. He comes up." She didn't look up from the exercise. "The folk intervals work because the acoustic principles don't care about the Codex. The Codex is a record of what people have named. It's not a record of what exists."

"You should write that down."

"I did write it down. In a different notebook." She looked up. "The blank pages aren't the end of the Codex. They're the beginning of the part no one has been willing to write."

The afternoon light had shifted, going amber through the high window. He had not thought about the Voss paper for forty minutes.

He didn't name that. He gathered his papers and she gathered hers, and they went into the corridor without deciding to leave at the same time.

---

The corridor between the east wing and the main building carried sound oddly — a conversation at the south arcade came through clearly at the north end, something about the angle of the archways and how the limestone bounced. Cass had learned to walk it quietly.

He was halfway down it when he heard Rector Suvaine's voice.

She was in the north arcade, speaking to someone — one of the senior faculty, a man whose voice didn't carry as well. Cass slowed. Not because he meant to listen. Because the Rector's voice had a quality in formal settings that made his ear sharpen automatically, the way it did near anything that might produce an under-note.

"—entirely inappropriate use of the restricted collection. The Bellwright family journals have been sealed since the second generation. The request was flagged because the phrasing was specific enough to suggest prior knowledge of the contents."

The faculty member said something low and indistinct.

"The student is not the concern," Suvaine said. "The concern is where the prior knowledge originated. Someone has been talking about the journals to someone who should not have heard about them."

Cass stopped walking.

He stood in the corridor and waited. He listened to Rector Suvaine talk about a student who had been asking questions about the Bellwright family's sealed journals, and he held his breath, and he waited.

It didn't come.

He waited longer. His jaw was tight enough to ache. He listened to her voice — the cadence of it, the specific weight she put on *sealed* and *prior knowledge*, how her sentences resolved fully, no trailing edge, no hesitation. He waited for the quarter-tone between F and F-sharp, the greasy wrongness, the frequency that announced itself like a split lip.

Nothing.

A lie would have meant she was hiding something she knew. This meant she had been told something and had no reason to question it. She was looking for a student who had been asking about the journals. She was not lying. She was not afraid. She was a woman who had been handed a piece of information by someone who understood exactly what she would do with it, and she was doing it.

He was almost certain she was talking about him.

The faculty member said something. Suvaine's voice moved — she was turning, heading toward the main building. Cass walked. Not fast. The pace of a student going from one room to another, which was what he was.

He went through the main building and out the front entrance and down the steps into the late afternoon. The Civic Square was settling toward its end-of-day register — market stalls closing, the last contracts of the afternoon being ratified, the sound of the city going quieter as the light went.

He walked home through the Bell Quarter.

The workshop light was already on, amber behind the high window. His father had come down early. Cass stopped outside the front door and heard, faintly, the lathe running from the back of the building — the low, regular vibration of it coming through the stone wall, the sound that meant his father was somewhere the tremor couldn't reach him.

He went inside.

The kitchen was dim. The fire had been banked but not stoked, which meant his father had been in the workshop since before supper and hadn't come up. The bread from this morning was still on the board, half a loaf, and the cheese beside it had dried at the cut edge.

On the table, between the bread board and the cold lamp, was a letter.

Not a letter Cass had left there. Not a bill from the Academy, not a supply order from the bronze merchants. The seal on the back was a circle with a tuning fork inside it, rendered in dark green wax, and Cass had seen that seal precisely once before — on the contract his father had brought home ten years ago and locked in the room off the kitchen without ever explaining what it said.

The House of Corda.

He stood in the kitchen doorway for a moment. The lathe ran on through the wall. The bread sat on the board. The letter sat on the table.

He crossed the kitchen and picked it up.

The seal was already broken. His father had read it.

He read it.

*To the Bellwright Family, Bell Quarter, Cantamura — Greetings from the House of Corda.*

*This letter serves as formal notice of a scheduled Maintenance Assessment of the resonance chambers located within the Corda compound, as required under Section Four of the Bellwright Service Agreement. The Assessment is scheduled for the fourteenth day of the coming month. The family is requested to provide one qualified bell-wright for the duration of the Assessment, estimated at two days.*

*Per the terms of the Agreement, this request constitutes a standard obligation under the maintenance provisions and requires no additional ratification.*

*The House of Corda further notes, with appreciation, the continued fitness for service of the current Bellwright composer, whose work has been of exceptional quality throughout the term of engagement. We anticipate his continued contribution.*

*Signed in proper form, Maret Corda, House of Corda.*

Cass read it twice.

*Fitness for service.*

He set the letter down on the table and looked at it. The lathe ran on through the wall. He listened to it for a moment — the low, patient vibration of his father working through something he didn't want to name.

*Fitness for service.* Like Perin was a bell. Like Perin was something you assessed for cracks and tuned and put back on the frame.

He picked the letter up again and read the third paragraph for the third time. *Continued fitness.* Present tense. Not *has been fit* or *was fit* but *is fit*, the kind of language that assumed an ongoing state, an arrangement that had no expected end.

Perin's contract was twelve years. Everyone in the family knew that. Twelve years, and Perin had been gone for ten.

He set the letter down again. He stood at the kitchen table and counted back from now, and the arithmetic was simple, and the arithmetic was wrong.

Twelve years minus ten years was two years. In two years, Perin came home.

So why did the letter read like there was no end in sight?

He was still standing there when the lathe stopped.

He heard his father's footsteps in the workshop — the particular gait of a man who had been standing at the lathe for three hours, careful and slightly stiff, coming toward the kitchen door. He heard the workshop door open and his father's boots on the stone passage, and then the kitchen door, and then his father was standing in the kitchen with bronze dust on his hands and his apron and the particular flatness in his face that meant he had been expecting this moment and had been hoping it wouldn't arrive.

He looked at Cass. He looked at the letter on the table. He looked at Cass again.

"You read it," he said.

"It was open."

"I know." His father went to the basin and began washing his hands. His back was to the room. The water ran.

Cass waited.

"The assessment is —" His father paused. "Routine. The chambers need —" He stopped. "Someone has to go."

"You're going to go."

"Yes."

"You go every quarter," Cass said. "The maintenance reports. I know."

His father turned off the water. He dried his hands on the hanging cloth by the basin. He turned around, and his face was the face of a man who had been deciding how much to say for ten years and had still not finished deciding.

"Dad."

"Cass."

"The contract is twelve years." He kept his voice level. Flat. The same pitch he used when he was reading an interval and wanted to be accurate. "Perin signed ten years ago."

His father said nothing.

"The letter says *continued fitness for service.* That's present tense. That's not language for something that's ending in two years."

"Contract language is formal. It doesn't mean—"

"Which key was the original ratification sung in?"

His father stopped.

Cass watched him. The kitchen was very quiet. The banked fire made no sound. Outside, somewhere in the Bell Quarter, a cart went past on the stone lane, and then it was quiet again.

"I'm not asking to argue," Cass said. "I'm asking because it matters. Which key."

His father pulled out the chair at the head of the table and sat down. He put his hands flat on the table, and Cass could see them — the tremor was there, faint but present, the slight movement in the fingers that was always worse away from the lathe.

"G-major," he said. "Standard form."

"And the agreement was twelve years."

"Yes."

"So in two years—"

"The agreement was twelve years," his father said. "The contract is ten."

Cass heard it. He stood at the table and heard the sentence, and his , and he waited for the under-note, for the quarter-tone between F and F-sharp, for the proof that his father was lying.

It didn't come.

His father was not lying. His father had just said something true.

"The agreement," Cass said carefully, "and the contract are different things."

"Yes."

"Perin signed a contract for ten years. But there was an agreement — a different document — for twelve."

"The agreement predates the contract. It was — it was the terms Maret Corda set before the contract was composed. The broader terms. The contract was the ratified version, the one that was sung. The agreement was the — the framework."

"The framework that says twelve years."

"Yes."

"And the contract says ten."

"Yes."

Cass looked at his father's hands on the table. The tremor in the fingers. The bronze dust still caught in the creases of his knuckles where the water hadn't reached.

"So which one governs?" he said. "If the contract says ten years and the agreement says twelve — which one is binding?"

His father said nothing.

"The sung contract governs," Cass said. "That's Tonal Law. The ratified version supersedes any prior written framework. A sung contract in proper form in G-major, held for a full breath cycle, witnessed and registered — that's the binding document. That's what the Codex says."

"Yes," his father said.

"So the contract says ten years. In two years, Perin comes home."

His father looked at his hands.

"Unless," Cass said, "the contract has codicils."

The word sat in the kitchen air. His father's hands didn't move.

"Codicil amendments," Cass said. "A minor seventh added to the original binding note, resolved within the same breath. You can extend a contract's term with a codicil. You can extend it multiple times." He pulled out the chair across from his father and sat down. "How many codicils does the contract have?"

"Cass."

"How many."

His father looked up. His eyes were tired in a way that had nothing to do with three hours at the lathe.

"I don't know," he said.

"You don't know."

"I haven't seen the contract in ten years. It's held by the House of Corda. I have the — I have a copy of the original ratification. I don't have the amendments."

"You don't have the amendments," Cass said. The words came out very flat. "Perin signed a contract ten years ago and you don't know how long it actually runs."

"The agreement was twelve years. Maret assured me—"

"Maret Corda assured you." Cass heard his own voice. He didn't raise it. He didn't need to. "In writing? In a sung ratification? Or did she just tell you, and you believed her, and you've been counting down to twelve years ever since?"

His father's hands pressed flat against the table.

"She assured you," Cass said. "And in the meantime, the contract has codicils you haven't read, and the letter uses present-tense language about Perin's *continued fitness for service*, and you go to the Corda compound every quarter to file maintenance reports and you haven't asked to see the contract." He paused. "Have you."

The fire made a small sound. The coal settling.

"No," his father said.

Cass sat with that.

His father's hands were shaking. Not the faint tremor from before — more than that, a visible thing, the fingers pressing harder against the table as if the pressure could stop it. He was looking at the letter. Not at Cass. At the letter on the table between them, as if it were something he'd known was coming and had been hoping wouldn't arrive in this particular form.

"Why didn't you tell me," Cass said.

"You were four years old."

"I'm fourteen."

His father said nothing.

"I'm fourteen," Cass said again. "Perin's been gone ten years. I've been asking questions about the contract since I was old enough to understand what a contract was, and you've told me nothing except that he's well and he writes and the contract runs twelve years." He picked up the letter from the table. "*Continued fitness for service.* That's what they wrote. He's been gone ten years and they're writing letters about his fitness like he's a piece of equipment." He set it back down. "I needed to know this."

"I know," his father said.

"Then why—"

"Because I didn't want you to—" He stopped. He pressed his hands flat again. "I didn't want you to know what I did."

The kitchen was very quiet.

Cass looked at his father. The bronze dust in the knuckle-creases. The shaking hands. The tired eyes that had been tired for ten years, tired in the specific way of someone who has been carrying a thing alone and has stopped expecting it to get lighter.

"What did you do," Cass said.

His father looked at the letter.

"What did you do," Cass said again, and this time it was a question with a shape — not *tell me something I don't know* but *say the thing you've been not saying* — and he waited, and the fire settled, and the Bell Quarter was quiet outside, and his father's hands shook on the table.

"I let her take him," his father said. "I could have — there were things I could have done. Things I should have looked at more carefully before I let it go to ratification. I was afraid of what she held over us and I was afraid of what was in the journals and I told myself the twelve years was real and that Perin would be home and everything would—" He stopped. "I chose the bells. I told myself I was protecting the family. I was protecting the bells."

The sentence hung in the kitchen air.

Cass sat with it. He didn't say anything. He looked at his father's face — the specific exhaustion of a man who has just said the true version of something he's been saying the false version of for ten years — and he felt something he didn't have a clean name for. Not forgiveness. Not anger. Something that sat between them like an interval that hadn't resolved yet.

"The contract," he said finally. "The copy you have. The original ratification."

"Yes."

"I want to see it."

His father looked at him.

"I'm not asking to argue," Cass said. "I'm asking because I need to know what it actually says. The key it was ratified in. The terms as written. Whether there's a mechanism for challenging the codicils." He paused. "Perin has been in there ten years. If the codicils are void — if any of them were composed wrong, if there's a lame chord, if the breath limit was exceeded on an amendment — then they don't hold. And if they don't hold, the contract runs ten years, and in two years he comes home."

His father was very still.

"And if the codicils are valid?" he said.

"Then I need to know that too."

His father looked at the table. At the letter. At his hands, which had stopped shaking — the stillness that came over him sometimes, the same stillness as the lathe, a body that had found the one thing it could do when everything else was too much.

He pushed back his chair and stood. He went to the kitchen wall — the one that adjoined the locked room — and Cass watched him press his palm flat against the stone for a moment, the gesture he made when he passed it, brief and almost unconscious. Then he took his hand away and went to the hook by the door where his keys hung.

He took the smallest one off the ring.

He set it on the table in front of Cass.

"The contract is with the journals," he said. "The locked room. Third shelf, the brown folder." He paused. "The bells in there — don't touch them."

Cass looked at the key on the table.

"I'll make supper," his father said. He went to the fire and began building it up, his back to the room, his hands steady now for the first time since he'd walked in from the workshop.

Cass picked up the key. It was small and old, the bow worn smooth from years of handling. He turned it over once.

He got up from the table and went to the locked room.
