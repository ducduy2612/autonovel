# Chapter Eight: Dav Sorn

He started with the oldest letter and Lenne sat across from him on the floor, her back against the bed frame, her notation paper on her knees.

They had been at it for two hours. The lamp was burning low and the letters were arranged between them in rows — oldest near the door, newest near Cass's feet. Forty-three letters. Not all of them had intervals. More than expected did.

"This one," Lenne said. She held up a letter from year three. Her index finger was ink-stained from copying out intervals as Cass read them aloud. "He says the lower register wants to be *closer to a minor sixth.* Not a minor sixth. Closer to one."

"Quarter-tone flat," Cass said. He wrote it down.

"That's a technical observation if you're not looking for a message."

"That's the point."

She set the letter in the done pile and picked up the next one. Her lips moved slightly as she read — working through it internally, not translating, just understanding. He'd noticed she did this. Most Academy students read notation by naming intervals out loud, like spelling a word letter by letter. Lenne heard it.

They worked through the rest methodically. When Perin mentioned an interval — a tuning problem, an acoustic adjustment, a frequency that wasn't sitting right in a particular room — Lenne called it and Cass wrote it: the interval name, the direction, the modification if there was one. Quarter-flat. Minor-adjacent. Closer to a sixth than a fifth.

By the fifth hour they had a sequence.

Lenne looked at it. "Fourteen intervals across ten years."

"One per letter. Sometimes two. He was careful."

"He was slow on purpose." She touched the page with one finger. "One interval out of place and it reads as a tuning note."

"Not a message."

"He trusted you to hear the difference." She looked up. "How old were you when he sent the first one?"

"Four."

She was quiet for a moment. "He waited until you were old enough that someone would save the letters. He calculated that."

Cass had understood this already, sitting alone at his desk the night before, but hearing it said was different. It made the calculation real — Perin working it out, deciding: *Cass will be old enough when he's about four. I'll start then.* Ten years of patience. Ten years of writing careful letters that said *I hope you're well* and *tell Father the Ninth of Coppergate needs its clapper re-set* and also, underneath, in the gaps between the technical observations: *here is the thing I cannot say directly.*

His throat felt strange.

"What does it look like to you?" he said. "The sequence."

Lenne studied the paper. Her right index finger moved against her thigh, tracing intervals — a fourth, a fifth, back to the root. She was hearing it internally. After a moment she said, "It's not in any key. It's not avoiding a key either — it's not like chromatic passages where you're moving through keys. It's outside. Like it was composed before keys existed."

"I think it was."

She looked at him.

"The original bells," Cass said. "Aldric's bells. They predate the Codex. They predate the codified scales."

A silence. Lenne set her notation paper on the floor. "Your family's bells."

"My family's bells."

"The ones in the locked room."

He looked at her.

"You mentioned it last year," she said. "The corridor between the theory rooms. You said there were three bells in your house you weren't allowed to touch. You said it like it was nothing." Her finger stilled against her thigh. "It wasn't nothing."

He hadn't remembered saying it. He felt a brief flash of irritation at himself, and then something else — a relief he didn't quite want to name. He'd been carrying this alone for six months and she'd been holding a small piece of it for a year without knowing what it was.

"The bells in the locked room," he said. "I think they're tuned to this." He held up the paper. "To this phrase."

Lenne looked at it. "Have you tried? Singing it near the door?"

"Not yet."

She waited.

"I was working up to it," he said.

She nodded slowly, as if this made complete sense, and did not push. He was grateful for that. He picked up the lute from the wall peg and tuned it by ear — faster than the peg-and-mallet method, less precise. He got the lower strings close enough.

He played the phrase.

It wasn't right on the lute. The instrument wanted to pull the notes into the nearest recognized pitch, the way a cart wheel finds a rut. He had to press against that pull, hold the strings at slightly wrong positions. The sound that came out was close.

Lenne listened with her head turned a few degrees left. "There," she said, when he reached the third interval. "Stop. Play that one again."

He played it again.

"That's the one that makes my teeth feel wrong." She worked her jaw, then stopped. "What is that?"

"It doesn't have a name in the Codex."

She thought about it. "You know when you've been holding something heavy for a long time and you put it down, and your arms still feel the weight for a few seconds after? Like the weight is still there even though it isn't?"

"Yes."

"That. That interval feels like that."

He played the full phrase through. Lenne didn't speak. Her finger traced something against her thigh — not the phrase, something adjacent to it, a response. He watched her work through it.

"It's the second interval," she said when he stopped. "The almost-minor-sixth. A minor sixth is two notes pulling away from each other — unresolved. You know when a conversation ends wrong, when something's been said that needs answering and the person's already walked away? That." She tapped her own notation paper. "But this is flatter. This is a minor sixth that doesn't know it's a minor sixth yet. Still deciding."

Cass stared at her. "I've been trying to describe that for six months."

"My mother makes me do interval identification exercises. She plays something and I have to name what it makes me feel before I name what it is." Lenne's expression shifted into something not quite comfortable. "She says the Academy teaches it backwards. Name the interval, then describe the feeling. She says that's like learning the word for red before you've seen a color."

"She's right."

"Don't tell her I agreed. She'll assign more exercises." Lenne stood, picking up her coat from the floor. "We should go."

"We should go now?"

She looked at him. "You've had this for ten years. You've had the sequence for one night. You were going to sit on it for how much longer?"

He couldn't argue with that. He put the lute back on its peg, folded the paper into his coat pocket, and followed her down the stairs.

---

They went to the locked room first.

The kitchen was quiet. His father's boots were gone from the door, the workshop sounds faint through the wall — slow finishing work, the kind that needed patience. Cass stood in front of the door and Lenne stood a few feet back, giving him room he hadn't asked for but appreciated.

The phrase was still sitting just behind his teeth.

He sang it, quietly, in the kitchen.

The response came through the door.

His cheek was a foot from the wood and the sound reached him through the air first, just barely, then the vibration found the door itself and traveled through it — into his jaw, down into his chest, settling there in a way that was less like hearing and more like confirmation. Nothing had struck the bells. This was what bells did when a nearby pitch matched their own: they answered without being touched, the bronze waking up, each one a slightly different shade of the same phrase.

Three notes. Three resonances, lower than his voice, fuller. Bronze cast for this purpose and tuned to hold this frequency.

He stopped singing. The resonance faded slowly.

Behind him, Lenne said nothing. He turned and she had one hand flat against the wall, her expression careful and strange.

"You heard that," he said.

"I felt it. In my sternum." She took her hand off the wall. "Those bells have been in there the whole time."

"My whole life."

"Waiting for someone to give them the phrase." She looked at the door, then at him. "Cass. Your father knows this is in there."

"Yes."

"He's known your whole life."

"Yes."

A silence. Then Lenne picked up her bag from the chair. "We need to see Dav Sorn."

---

She wrote the letter of introduction at the Academy desk while Cass gathered the papers — the tally-songs from Torvald, the lame chord pattern, the co-custodial clause, the sealed record. The case he had been building in the language the system understood, because he had believed the system was the tool he needed. He still believed it. He needed to believe it a little longer.

But underneath the case, underneath the argument about intervals and ratification keys and contract form, there was the sequence on the folded paper in his coat pocket. Fourteen intervals. Ten years. The thing beneath the Civic Pitch.

The mid-ring smelled of rendered tallow and old paper. Dav Sorn's building had no sign — just a brass knocker shaped like a treble clef, worn smooth at the grip.

Lenne knocked. Cass stood half a step behind her and listened to the building settle.

A clerk took the letter of introduction, left them in a narrow hallway with a bench and a coat-hook and a single window that looked onto the alley wall. Lenne stood with her back against the plaster. Her right index finger traced intervals against her thigh — a fourth, a fifth, back to the root, then again. Cass sat on the bench. The stone was cold through his coat. He put his papers on his knees and looked at the window and said nothing.

The building had a resonance of its own — not a lie, nothing like that, just the quality of old limestone that had been absorbing sound for a hundred years. It pressed against the back of his jaw in a way that wasn't painful. More like pressure. Like the air before a storm.

He counted intervals under his breath. Not words. Just the numbers.

The clerk returned. *Follow me.*

---

Sorn was already reading Vella Ashvale's letter when they entered. He stood at his desk, two fingers resting against his throat just below the jaw, and he did not look up until he'd finished. Then he set the letter down with one flat motion and looked at Cass.

"Vella Ashvale does not write letters of introduction lightly."

"No," Lenne said. "She doesn't."

He gestured at the two chairs across the desk. The office was small and very orderly — not the orderliness of someone who didn't work, but of someone who needed to be able to find things quickly. Stacks of paper with weighted stones on top. A shelf of Codex volumes with slips of paper marking pages. On the desk, a tuning fork and a small bronze disc, the kind used for testing coin pitch.

Sorn sat. He was slight, with the upright posture of someone trained in breath control. He touched his throat again briefly — two fingers, just below the jaw — and then moved his hand to the desk.

"You've brought documentation," he said, looking at the papers on Cass's knees.

"Yes."

"Then start with the documentation. We can move to the other matter afterward." He picked up a pen. "The Bellwright contract with the House of Corda."

Cass laid out the papers in order. The original contract form, as he'd reconstructed it from the registrar's record. The tally-songs Torvald had given him, showing the pattern of cases where Corda-affiliated Court Singers had been unavailable. The co-custodial clause, flagged. The penalty terms.

Sorn read without speaking. His pen moved occasionally, marking something in the margin of his own notes. His face stayed neutral — not the performed neutrality of someone hiding a reaction, but the working neutrality of a man who had learned not to commit to a position before he'd finished reading.

When he reached the end, he set the papers down and looked at the tuning fork on his desk.

"The co-custodial clause," he said. "You believe it creates a conflict of interest in the original ratification."

"The ratification was witnessed by a Court Singer retained by the House of Corda," Cass said. "The contract binds a member of the family that holds co-custodial responsibility for the bells. The witnessing Singer had a financial relationship with the contracting party."

"That argument has been made before," Sorn said. "Twice, in my recollection. Both times the Academy ruled that the Court Singer's role was procedural — witnessing form, not adjudicating terms. The conflict-of-interest doctrine doesn't extend to procedural witnesses."

"I know. But those cases didn't have the lame chord."

Sorn's pen stopped.

"The ratification," Cass said. "The fifth resolves up, not down. I've checked the registrar's notation three times. An ascending fifth is a formal rejection. The contract was ratified with the wrong interval."

Sorn looked at him. Two fingers went to his throat.

"That would make it," he said, carefully, "not a contract."

"A gesture," Cass said. "Legally a declaration. Not a binding."

A silence. Lenne had gone very still in her chair.

Sorn picked up the papers again and went to the page with the ratification notation. He looked at it for a long time. His fingers moved against his desk in a small, contained way — not the bronze ring striking, not Maret Corda's gesture, but something similar. Working through a problem.

"Where is the standing-wave record?" he said.

Cass looked at him.

"The registrar keeps a standing-wave record for every ratified contract," Sorn said. "An acoustician measures the binding wave at the point of ratification and records its frequency and amplitude. If the fifth resolved upward, the wave would be — absent, or malformed. The record would show it." He set the papers down. "Where is it?"

"I couldn't find it," Cass said. "I requested it from the registrar's office three days ago. They said the record was in the archive. When I went to the archive, the clerk said it had been transferred to the Academy for review. When I went to the Academy, they said they had no record of receiving it."

Sorn looked at the tuning fork.

"The standing-wave record for the Bellwright contract ratification is missing," he said.

"Yes."

"That is —" He stopped. Started again. "In my experience, records of that kind do not go missing through administrative error. They are not the kind of document that gets misfiled." He picked up his pen and set it down again. "How long ago was the ratification?"

"Ten years."

"Ten years." He touched his throat. "And the record has been missing for —"

"I don't know. I'm the first person who's tried to find it."

Another silence. Sorn stood and went to the window. It looked onto the same alley as the hallway window — a narrow strip of gray sky between two limestone walls. He stood with his hands clasped behind his back.

"I have sung forty-three verdicts in the last two years," he said. He was looking at the alley. "I have been satisfied with eleven of them."

Cass said nothing. Lenne's finger had stopped moving against her thigh.

"The remaining thirty-two," Sorn said, "were — not wrong, exactly. The form was correct. The intervals were correct. But the outcomes —" He stopped. "The outcomes reflected the resources of the parties more than the merits of the case. In each instance, the merits were genuine. The form was genuine. The outcome was still predictable from the start." He turned from the window. "I am telling you this because you should understand what you're asking me to do."

"What are we asking you to do?" Lenne said.

He looked at her. "You're asking me to file a formal challenge to a contract ratification on the grounds of a procedural defect that the Academy has already ruled irrelevant in analogous cases, using a standing-wave record that no longer exists, in a case where the contracting party is the House of Corda." He sat down. "The House of Corda retains two of the four Court Singers in this city. The other two are myself and your mother. If I file this challenge, the Academy will need to convene a review panel. The panel will require a Court Singer to sing the standing-wave reconstruction — to recreate, from existing records, what the binding wave would have looked like if the ratification was valid." He touched his throat again. "That Singer will be either me or your mother. And the House of Corda will know it."

"Will you do it?" Cass said.

Sorn looked at him. Something shifted in his expression — not softening, exactly. More like a man recognizing a question he had been waiting for.

"The sub-harmonic," he said.

Cass went still.

"Vella's letter mentioned a frequency beneath the Civic Pitch," Sorn said. "She described it as — and I'm quoting — *an interval outside the codified scales that her son has been hearing for some time, and which she believes may be relevant to the Bellwright case.* She was characteristically uninformative about why." He looked at Cass. "She wasn't writing about you, was she. She was writing about herself."

"She's been hearing it too," Cass said. "For years."

"As have I," Sorn said.

The room was very quiet.

"I've heard it since my third year of practice," he said. "The morning Civic Pitch, at the end of the sequence. Below the bells. I reported it once, to a senior Court Singer, when I was newly licensed. She told me it was acoustic fatigue — a perceptual artifact from too many hours in resonance chambers. I accepted this explanation because it was convenient." He looked at the tuning fork on his desk. "I have not accepted it since."

"What do you think it is?" Lenne said.

"I think it is a frequency that Aldric Bellwright put into the bells two hundred years ago, that the Codex has no language for, and that the Academy would prefer not to have to explain." He picked up the tuning fork. Set it down. "I think it is why the Bellwright family journals are sealed. And I think it is connected to why a standing-wave record from ten years ago no longer exists in any archive in this city."

Cass reached into his coat pocket and took out the folded paper. He unfolded it and set it on the desk between them.

Sorn looked at it.

"These are from my brother's letters," Cass said. "One interval per letter, over ten years. He's been encoding a phrase."

Sorn read the sequence. His expression didn't change. But his breathing changed — a small, controlled adjustment, the kind a trained singer made when something hit them and they didn't want it to show.

"This is the sub-harmonic," he said.

"Yes."

"Your brother has been sending you the sub-harmonic. Piece by piece. From inside the House of Corda." Sorn set the paper down. He looked at the wall for a moment. Then: "He knows what it is."

"I think so. I think he found out when he was sixteen and it's why Maret Corda wanted him."

"Not just a good composer," Sorn said. "Someone who already knew what was in the bells." He touched his throat. "Someone who couldn't be allowed to tell anyone else."

The afternoon light was moving across the desk. Somewhere in the alley, a cart went past, the wheel-noise muffled by the limestone.

"The case I can make," Sorn said, "is the lame chord. The ascending fifth. Even without the standing-wave record, I can argue that the record's absence is itself evidence of irregularity — a missing record in a case involving the House of Corda, where the standing-wave record for every other contract from that period exists intact. That is not nothing. It is not sufficient on its own, but it is not nothing." He straightened the papers on his desk. "The sub-harmonic is a different matter. I cannot bring that before the Academy. I cannot walk into a review panel and say *there is a frequency in the Civic Pitch that the Codex doesn't recognize, and a sixteen-year-old boy was bound into service because he knew about it.* They will rule it inadmissible. They will question my fitness to practice."

"But you believe it," Cass said.

"What I believe is not the point." Sorn's voice was careful. Precise. "What I can argue before a panel is the point. And what I can argue before a panel is the lame chord, the missing record, and the conflict-of-interest doctrine, which — as I said — has been ruled irrelevant in analogous cases. That is a difficult case. It is not an impossible one."

"Will you take it?"

Sorn was quiet for a long moment. He picked up the tuning fork and held it, not striking it. Just held it.

"I need to think," he said. "There are — implications I need to work through. If I file this challenge, I am filing it against the House of Corda, in a city where the House of Corda retains half the Court Singers. The consequences for my practice if I lose are significant." He set the tuning fork down. "I'm not saying no. I'm saying come back tomorrow."

"Tomorrow," Cass said.

"I know that's not what you wanted to hear."

It wasn't. But he also heard what was underneath it — a man who had been satisfied with eleven verdicts out of forty-three, sitting with a tuning fork in his hand, not saying no. That was different from no. That was a man deciding something.

"Tomorrow," Cass said.

Sorn nodded. He picked up the papers — the tally-songs, the contract form, the co-custodial clause — and squared them into a neat stack. He did not hand them back.

"Leave the documentation," he said. "I'll want to read it again."

Cass left the folded paper too, the sequence of fourteen intervals, the phrase his brother had been building for ten years. He almost picked it up again at the door. He didn't.

---

Outside, the mid-ring afternoon was cold and bright. Lenne walked beside him and neither of them spoke for a block.

"Eleven out of forty-three," she said.

"I know."

"That's a man who's been keeping count." She pulled her coat tighter. "He's already decided. He just hasn't said it yet."

Cass thought about the tuning fork in Sorn's hand. The careful, qualified language of a person who had learned what words cost and was measuring out exactly how much he was willing to spend.

"Maybe," he said.

"The sub-harmonic," Lenne said. "He's heard it for years and never told anyone."

"Neither had your mother."

She was quiet for a moment. "She's going to ask me how it went."

"What will you tell her?"

"The truth." Lenne glanced at him. "That there's a Court Singer who's heard the same thing she has, and he thinks it matters, and we're going back tomorrow." She paused. "She's going to want to come."

Cass thought about that. Vella Ashvale, who had been hearing the sub-harmonic for years and had kept it from the Academy, who had written a letter of introduction that was characteristically uninformative about why. A Court Singer who already knew there was a frequency the Codex couldn't name.

"Let her," he said.

They turned into the Bell Quarter. The workshop sounds reached him through the wall — still the slow finishing work, steady. His father at the lathe, hands quiet, giving them something to do.

The locked room was still in there. The three bells still tuned to the phrase. And somewhere in the House of Corda's compound, Perin was at a composing table, doing work that was genuinely good, in a room that was comfortable enough, writing careful letters with technical observations that weren't only technical observations.

*A minor sixth that doesn't know it's a minor sixth yet. Still deciding.*

Tomorrow Sorn would either say yes or he wouldn't. The case would move or it wouldn't. But the sequence was on the paper on Sorn's desk now, out of Cass's coat pocket, out of the box under the floorboard, no longer only his. Fourteen intervals. Ten years. His brother's handwriting in forty-three letters, composing a question piece by piece and trusting that someone would eventually hear the answer in it.

Cass went inside. The kitchen was quiet. He stood in front of the locked door for a moment, not singing, just standing there.

Then he went upstairs to wait for tomorrow.
