# Chapter Nineteen: The Morning Pitch

The workshop was cold when Cass came downstairs.

He'd woken before the sky lightened and lain listening to the dark, the distant sound of the night-watch making its rounds. Then he'd gotten up, pulled on his stockings and his outer shirt, and come down.

The lamp in the workshop was already burning.

He stopped in the doorway.

His father was at the lathe, turning a small clapper, the lamplight catching metal dust drifting in the air around his hands. And at the composing table in the corner — the spot where their mother used to sit on household accounts and which had stood empty for as long as Cass could remember — Perin was bent over notation paper with a pencil, writing in the careful, cramped hand he'd always had, the letters getting smaller when he was concentrating.

They were not talking. The lathe turned. The pencil moved.

Cass came in and sat on the cold stone bench by the door and watched them. His father's hands were steady on the lathe in the way they always settled here, among the bells — the tremor present if you knew where to look, a slight irregularity in the rotation, but quieter than the dinner table, quieter than anywhere else. Perin's pencil moved and stopped. The notation paper was half-covered already.

The Civic Pitch would sound in forty minutes.

He sat and listened.

---

Perin had come home four days ago.

He'd walked out of the Corda compound at midmorning carrying one bag — a canvas satchel with a broken buckle that Cass recognized from when he was six. He'd knocked on the door. Eddan had opened it.

Cass had been on the stairs, halfway down, where he'd stopped when he heard the knock. He could see his father's back, the set of his shoulders. Eddan's hands were both visible, hanging at his sides, shaking — not the fine tremor of the dinner table but a deeper thing, starting in the wrists and moving the whole hand, both hands, as if his body had been waiting ten years and had finally been given permission.

Perin stood on the doorstep. Older — the jaw sharper, the hair shorter, a quality of stillness that hadn't been there at sixteen. One hand on the strap of his satchel. The other not steady either.

Neither of them spoke.

The doorway held them. The threshold between them was three feet of stone that the morning light fell across, and for a long moment neither could cross it — the weight of ten years pressing down on the space between a father and a son who had last stood this close on the day one of them signed the other away.

Eddan's right hand came up. Not reaching. Just rising, involuntary, the way his hand rose to check a bell's surface. It stopped halfway. Hung in the air.

Perin looked at the hand. Then at his father's face. Something passed between them that Cass could feel but not hear — below the under-note, below any frequency he had a name for.

Eddan's hand dropped. He stepped back.

Perin came in. His first step across the threshold was careful, as you step onto ice you're not sure will hold. By the third he was inside and his father had closed the door, and the hallway was too small for three people and none of them moved to fix that.

Perin's eyes found Cass on the stairs.

His satchel hit the floor by the door. He looked around the front room, which had not changed because Eddan didn't change things he didn't have to. The same hooks. The same table with the ring-marks from hot pots. The same crack in the plaster above the window that Cass had been meaning to ask about since he was seven.

"The Seventh of Ash Street," Perin said, to no one in particular. "Does it still run flat in autumn?"

Cass's throat was raw enough that the laugh came out wrong. It didn't matter.

Perin moved through the house like a man reading a map that had been redrawn while he was away. He went to the cabinet above the basin for a glass and opened the wrong door — the one that held oil and salt now, had held glasses ten years ago. He stood looking at the oil for a moment, then tried the next cabinet. The glasses had moved six inches to the left. At the kitchen table he pulled out the chair nearest the window — his chair, once — and when he sat down his knees came up against the underside. He shifted. The chair was the same chair. He reached for the bread without looking and his hand landed where it had been kept when he was a child. It was at the other end now. Cass pushed it toward him without comment.

Perin took it and ate slowly, in his old chair that didn't fit, drinking from a glass he'd found in the wrong cabinet.

---

The first time Cass and Perin were alone together was that evening.

Their father had gone to bed early — not tired, just unwilling to be present for whatever was about to happen in the kitchen. The lamp was low. The bronze ring Maret Corda had sent sat on the table where Cass had left it, and Perin had been looking at it since he sat down, not touching it, the way you look at a word you're not sure how to pronounce.

Cass's voice was still wrong. He'd been managing it all day — short sentences, no sustained notes, the throat remedy Senna Voss had sold him good for conversation but nothing more. He sat across from his brother and tried to remember how to begin.

Perin saved him the trouble.

"You're shorter than I expected," he said. "In the letters. You seemed taller."

Cass stared at him.

"That's not a complaint. I'm just noting it."

"I'm fourteen."

"I know how old you are."

"I'm still growing."

"Obviously." Perin glanced up from the ring. His eyes were the same — gray, level, with that quality of noticing things slightly ahead of when he commented on them. "Your voice is wrecked."

"Yes."

"How long?"

"Senna Voss says two weeks. Maybe three for the upper intervals."

Perin nodded. He looked back at the ring. "The letters couldn't say certain things. You know that."

"I know."

"I want you to know that I know you know." He paused. "That sentence got away from me."

Cass exhaled. "I read the gaps."

"I know you did. That's why I left them." Perin turned the ring over in his mind, not touching it. "There's a difference between leaving a gap and having someone read it. I kept thinking — he's twelve, he's fourteen, he's reading what I'm not saying and building something out of it that might not be what I meant."

"The east wing," Cass said. "The one that needed re-tuning."

"You got that."

"Third letter. You'd mentioned it twice before. The third time you said *I've told them twice* — past tense. Then you described the specific interval it was drifting toward." He stopped. His voice was fraying at the edge. "It was the same interval as the sub-harmonic."

"That took you three letters."

"I was twelve."

"Fair." Something shifted in his expression. "I started that when you were nine. I didn't know how long it would take. I was composing for a listener I couldn't hear."

That landed somewhere specific in Cass's chest. He pressed his tongue to the roof of his mouth. Not because there was an under-note — there wasn't, or it was too small to register. Just the habit.

Perin saw it. His expression went still in a different way. "You still do that."

"You taught me."

"I didn't think it would stick."

"It stuck."

"Does it help?"

"No."

"It doesn't help me either." Perin's mouth curved slightly. "I've been doing it for ten years in the Corda compound and it has never once helped. It's completely useless."

"I know."

"And yet."

"And yet," Cass agreed.

The lamp guttered. Neither moved to adjust it.

"In the letters," Cass said, "I couldn't tell if you were angry."

Perin looked at him steadily. "At who?"

"Father."

"I was angry at Father."

"And now?"

"Now I'm home and he went to bed early." Perin picked up the bronze ring at last, turned it once between his fingers, set it back down. It rang a faint G against the table — too soft to bind anything, just a note in a quiet kitchen. "I don't know what to do with him yet."

"He didn't tell me," Cass said. "What the contract said. Why you signed. I had to work it out from nothing."

"I know he didn't."

"I needed him to tell me and he didn't."

"No." Perin's voice was level, but there was something underneath it Cass could hear even without the under-note telling him what to call it. "He wouldn't."

Cass looked at his brother across the table. There were questions stacking in his chest — about the compound, about the work, about the years. But the kitchen was late and the lamp was low and some things needed a different room.

"I spent six months looking for the clean mechanism," Cass said. His voice cracked at the upper register and he waited for it to settle. "The flaw in the form. The Codex provision that would void it. I kept thinking there had to be a version of this where nobody was wrong in a way that couldn't be fixed. And there wasn't."

"So you put the question to the square."

"I put the question to the square."

Perin looked at him. "A preliminary ruling in writing. The registrar hasn't updated the record. If Maret Corda files a counter-petition in the right key with the right Court Singer—"

"I know."

"Then why aren't you angry about that?"

"I am angry about it." Cass's voice was flat in the way it got when the anger had been load-bearing long enough to become structural. "I asked the question because it was the only thing the system could hear. Not because I thought it would be enough."

Perin picked up the ring again and held it in his palm.

"You know what the compound taught me?" he said. "That the system works exactly as designed. No flaw in the form. No lame chord in the ratification. The coercion is invisible because the system was built to make it invisible — not as a mistake but as a feature, because the people who built it needed it to work that way." He set the ring down. "And then my fourteen-year-old brother asks a question in the Civic Square and the city answers it, and I'm sitting here thinking — that should have been enough to void the whole mechanism. Not a question. Not a ruling. A void. And it wasn't."

"No," Cass said. "It wasn't."

"You did the right thing."

"I know."

"And it wasn't enough."

"I know that too."

Silence. The lamp threw shadows across the lines of Perin's face.

"You had fourteen years in this house with a father who felt guilty every morning," Perin said. His voice had changed — something had come off it, some layer of care. "You had that. I had ten years in a compound with people who didn't."

Cass went still.

It was true. He knew it was true. He sat in the truth of it and felt it land in a place that had no good response — not because it was unfair but because it was completely fair, and the fairness of it meant there was nothing to argue and nothing to fix and nothing to offer except the fact of having heard it.

The kitchen held the silence.

Perin was looking at the table. His jaw was set. He wasn't taking it back and he wasn't softening it and Cass didn't want him to.

The lamp guttered again and this time the flame dipped low enough that the room went amber and close. Cass reached over and adjusted the wick without thinking about it, the way you do a thing you've done a thousand times in a room you've lived in your whole life. The flame steadied.

Perin watched him do it.

Neither of them said anything for a while.

Then Perin said, "Does he still keep the bread at the wrong end of the table?"

"He moved it when I was eight. I think he forgot where it was originally."

"He always forgot where things were. Mum used to say he had a workshop mind. Perfect recall for bell frequencies, no idea where he'd left his coat."

"He leaves his coat on the lathe," Cass said. "Every day. It's been there so long there's a shape worn into the leather from the handle."

Something in Perin's face shifted. Not quite a smile. Something older and quieter than a smile.

"That's very him," he said.

"Yes."

The bread was between them on the table. Perin reached for it — this time to the right end, where Cass had pushed it earlier — and broke off a piece and ate it slowly, and the kitchen was still not resolved and the twelve years were still twelve years and neither of them had said the things that still needed saying, but the ordinary act of eating bread in a kitchen at night was also in the room, and it was doing some small work that neither of them could have managed directly.

---

Upstairs that night, Cass lay listening through the floorboards.

The workshop door opened and closed. Two sets of footsteps on the stone. The lathe didn't start. For a long time there was nothing.

Then his father's voice — too low to make out words, just the shape of them. Short phrases with pauses between. The cadence of a man who had been composing this conversation for ten years and was finding that the words he'd practiced didn't fit the room they were in.

Then Perin's voice. Higher, steadier. Deciding in real time how much to let through.

A longer silence.

Then one sentence came through the gap in the floorboards where the wood had shrunk, carried up with the precision of a bell struck in an empty hall:

*I practiced what I'd say to you every night for ten years and none of it was I'm sorry I knew.*

Then silence. Then a sound Cass had never heard before — his father, crying. A small, private sound, muffled, lasting about thirty seconds. Then it stopped, and after a while the lathe started.

Two people at the lathe. He could tell from the rhythm — one pair of hands guiding the work, another passing tools. The specific syncopation of two craftsmen who had once worked together and were remembering how.

He pressed his face into his pillow. His eyes were wet. He let them be wet.

The lathe ran for two hours. He fell asleep to the sound of it.

---

Maret Corda sent no messenger. She filed no breach claim.

The note arrived the following morning, slipped under the workshop door before the Civic Pitch, written on plain paper in a hand that was small and precise and utterly without ceremony.

*The question has been asked. I will not be the one to suppress the answer.*

Below the signature, in a hand less controlled — written faster, or later, after a pause long enough that the ink had dried and had to be re-dipped:

*My father would have heard it.*

Cass read both lines standing in the workshop doorway in his stockings. He thought about Maret Corda in her receiving room, the dark green wool and the stillness. A girl who had walked into the Academy at seventeen with folk intervals in her ears and been told they didn't count, and who had spent forty years building an empire from the mechanism that had excluded her, and who had last night written two lines on plain paper instead of filing a counter-petition. He thought about what it cost to hear something true and decide not to be the one who buried it.

He didn't forgive her. He wasn't sure forgiveness was his to give. But he folded the note and set it on the composing table next to the notation paper Perin had been working on, and he went to put the kettle on.

---

The morning Cass came downstairs to the lit workshop, Perin was writing his own release.

He'd been at the composing table for an hour already — Cass could tell from the crossed-out lines, the pencil worn down. The release sequence was not simple. A tritone followed by resolution to the octave, both parties present and willing. Perin was composing for a situation the Codex had no form for: a binding contested at the root rather than dissolved at the surface. He was building the form himself.

Cass sat on the cold stone bench and watched him work.

After a while, Perin set down his pencil and looked at what he'd written. He tilted his head the way he always had when he was listening to something — the same angle Cass used, the same slight lean to the left — and Cass had never noticed until this moment that they did the same thing.

"It's not right yet," Perin said.

"The resolution interval?" Cass's voice came out rough but audible.

"The opening. The tritone has to acknowledge the original binding before it can dissolve it. Otherwise it's just noise." He tapped the pencil against the paper. "You can't release something you haven't named."

Eddan's hands slowed on the lathe. He didn't look up.

"Does it need to name the binding or the coercion?" Cass asked.

Perin looked at him. "What's the difference?"

"The binding was valid in form. The coercion was the problem. If you dissolve the binding, you're treating it like a normal release. If you name the coercion first, you're saying the binding was void at the root — which means there's nothing to release. Just something to acknowledge."

A silence.

Perin looked at the notation paper. "That's not in the Codex."

"No."

"That's a new interval."

"Lenne thinks Volume Four was left blank on purpose. Space for things the founders knew would come up eventually." He stopped. "She might be right."

Perin picked up the pencil and crossed out the opening line and wrote something new, the letters getting tighter as he concentrated. He wrote for about two minutes without stopping. Then he set the pencil down.

"Better," he said.

Eddan turned off the lathe.

The workshop went quiet. The bells hummed on their frames, picking up vibration from the city waking beyond the walls — early carts on stone roads, the first voices of the market setting up. Forty-seven minutes until the Civic Pitch.

Eddan turned on his stool and looked at the notation paper from across the workshop. He didn't cross the room. Just looked, the way he looked at a bell from a distance when he was trying to hear something he couldn't quite name.

"The opening," he said. "You'll want the B-flat reference from the Seventh of Ash Street. It's the note that was present at the original ratification. If you're naming the coercion, you need the original witness pitch." He paused. "I have the maintenance records."

Perin looked at his father for a long moment. "You kept the maintenance records from twelve years ago."

"I keep all the maintenance records."

"For every bell."

"Yes."

Perin's face was still. The anger was there — Cass could hear it in the quality of the stillness, a man holding something carefully because he hadn't yet decided whether to set it down or throw it.

"Get me the records," Perin said.

Eddan stood. He went to the shelf where the bound ledgers stood in their row — thirty years of bell maintenance, each labeled in his precise hand — and pulled out the one from twelve years ago. He carried it across the workshop and set it on the composing table, open to the correct page. The Seventh of Ash Street's entry from the autumn of the ratification. The exact pitch, the maintenance notes, the specific reading from the morning of the signing.

He went back to the lathe and sat down without saying anything else.

Perin looked at the ledger. His hand was not quite steady when he picked up the pencil. He wrote the opening interval.

---

Eddan went to check the Tower bracket, the way he did every morning. The door closed behind him. The workshop held its quiet.

Perin set the pencil down and looked at the clapper their father had been turning. It was nearly finished — a small piece for one of the mid-tier bells, the curve of the head not quite right, the contact angle off by a degree. Maybe two. He picked it up and turned it in his hands.

"This wants reshaping at the strike point," he said.

Cass crossed the room and took the clapper when Perin held it out. He could feel it — the slight asymmetry in the head, the way the weight sat wrong. He set it in the vise and picked up the fine file from the rack without looking.

They worked. Perin held the clapper steady while Cass filed, small precise strokes, the bronze dust collecting on the bench below. It was the kind of work that required attention from the hands and freed something in the rest of you — the same principle as the lathe, the same reason their father's tremor settled here. Cass could feel the moment the contact angle came true, a rightness in the resistance that traveled up the file and into his fingers.

"That's it," Perin said. "Hold there."

Cass held. Perin leaned in and sighted along the strike face, one eye closed, the way he'd done since he was twelve. Something about the angle of his head, the concentration, made him look both older and younger than he was.

"Good," he said. He took the clapper and weighed it in his palm.

He didn't look up.

"I got good at it," he said. "The contract work. The composition. I got genuinely good and there were days when that felt clean." His thumb moved along the clapper's freshly filed edge. "I composed something that hurt someone. Not accidentally — I knew what the terms would do when they were sung. A binding for a textile merchant in the Weavers' District. Corda wanted the storage contracts and the merchant was in debt and I composed the codicil that locked him in." He turned the clapper over. "His name was Farren Gault. I don't know what happened to him after."

The workshop was quiet. The bells hummed on their frames.

Cass didn't say anything. He stood with the file in his hand and let the silence have the space it needed.

"I did it because they asked me to," Perin said. He was still looking at the clapper — his eyes on the work instead of his brother. "And because I was good at it. And because the work was the only thing in the compound that felt like mine." He set the clapper down on the bench, gently, the way you set down something you've made. "I don't know what to do with that."

"You were coerced," Cass said.

"I was. And I still composed it. Both things." He looked up then — finally — and his expression was steady and unguarded, and Cass understood that this was why Perin had waited. Not the kitchen. Not the late lamp and the bread between them. Here, with his hands still warm from the work, with the evidence of what he could make sitting on the bench between them. "I'm not asking you to tell me it wasn't my fault. I know it wasn't entirely my fault. I also know I did it."

Cass set the file down. He wanted to offer something clean and couldn't find it. "Did you try to leave it out? The codicil."

"Once. Early on. They gave the work to someone else and the result was worse and the next time they asked me I did it." Perin's voice was even. "That's the part I can't make tidy."

"No," Cass said. "I don't think it gets tidy."

Perin picked up the clapper again. Turned it once. Set it back. The strike point was true now — they'd made it true together, and that didn't fix anything, but the bell it belonged to would ring cleaner because of it.

"All right," he said. And then, quieter: "All right."

---

The song-broker arrived at the workshop door an hour after the Civic Pitch.

She was one of the outer-district brokers — Cass had seen her twice before, a woman with gray at her temples and a guild pin old enough to have the original copper-bright finish worn off. Torvald Hess had sent her with a note that said, in its entirety: *When your brother is ready, send for Mira Fen. She's seen lame chords before.*

She came in, looked at the three of them, looked at the notation paper.

"That the release sequence?" she said.

"Working draft," Perin said.

She crossed to the table and read without touching the page, her lips barely moving. She read for a long time.

"The opening is unusual."

"Yes."

"It names the coercion before the binding."

"Yes."

She looked up at Perin. "I've not seen that form before."

"It's not in the Codex."

"No." She looked back at the page. "But the acoustic logic is sound. If the binding was void at the root, you're not releasing it — you're declaring the void. The tritone still resolves correctly." She paused, longer than the first pause. "Just making sure I'm asking right." Another beat. "Are you filing this as a release, or as a declaration of void?"

Perin looked at Cass.

Cass looked back at him. The question underneath the question: declaring void meant the original agreement had never been legally real, which meant the twelve years were not a contract term served but twelve years taken. Which was true. Which was also something the system had no clean mechanism for addressing. You could declare the void. The void didn't give the years back.

He sat with the discomfort of it and didn't try to resolve it.

"Declaration of void," Perin said. "On grounds of coercion at the original ratification."

The broker nodded slowly. "That will require a co-signing Court Singer."

"Dav Sorn has agreed," Cass said.

She looked at him. "Has he."

"He filed the response to the Corda objection. He knows the case."

"Dav Sorn," she said again, with the tone of someone recalibrating an estimate. "All right." She looked back at the notation paper. "I'll need to review the full draft. And I'll want to hear the Seventh of Ash Street's reference pitch from the morning of the original ratification, if you have it."

"We have it," Eddan said from the lathe.

She looked at him. He was already turned back to his work, the clapper taking shape under his hands. She looked at the ledger open on the composing table — the maintenance record from twelve years ago — and seemed to understand something about the room she was in without needing to have it explained.

"Give me an hour with the draft," she said.

She sat down at the composing table, across from Perin, and they worked.

Cass sat on the cold stone bench by the door. His voice was still wrong — still a half-step flat from where it should be, still cracking at the upper intervals, still not ready for anything that needed to be held. Senna Voss had said two weeks, maybe three. He was on day four.

He found he was not impatient about this.

Outside, the city was doing its morning. The Civic Pitch had sounded and the contracts were moving through the markets — the bread sellers and the cloth merchants and the small disputes about fence lines and shared walls, all of it moving through the system the same way it always had. The system had not changed.

Through the workshop's open window, he caught a glimpse of the Academy gate. A fresh notice had been pinned to the iron rail — white paper, the Rector's seal. He couldn't read it from here, but Perin, glancing up, could. "All ratifications suspended pending review of foundational pitch variance," he said quietly, and went back to his notation.

But the broker across the room was pausing before each interval. He could hear it from here — the slightly longer breath, the moment of consideration before she committed to the note. Not hesitation. Something more deliberate.

The under-note was quiet. The smallest he'd heard it in months. He read the texture of it: not coercion, not a lie. Just the ordinary uncertainty of people trying to agree on something, composing as carefully as they knew how.

Perin looked up from the notation paper. He looked at Cass on the bench, and for a moment his expression was the one from the kitchen — the one that sat between recognition and grief. Then it settled into something else, something without a name that was steadier than either.

"The opening," he said. "Is it right?"

Cass listened to the interval in his head. The B-flat from the Seventh of Ash Street. The naming of the thing before the dissolution.

"Yes," he said.

His voice barely cleared his teeth. But it was audible, and it was honest, and it was the right pitch.

The broker looked up from the page.

"Yes," she said quietly.

The lathe turned. The pencil moved. The bells hummed on their iron frames, and outside the city went on with its ordinary work, and Cass sat with his back against the cold stone wall and his voice slowly coming back, and the silence in the room was not holding anything.

It was just silence. The kind that rested.
