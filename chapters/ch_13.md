# Chapter Thirteen: Maret Corda

The gate was iron and old, with a bell set into the pillar beside it — a house bell, a signal bell, the kind a coppersmith could make in an afternoon. Cass looked at it for a moment before pulling the cord. It rang inside the wall: a clean, unremarkable E-flat. No under-note. Nothing there except the sound itself.

A porter opened a slot in the gate and looked at him.

"Cass Bellwright. I'd like to see Maret Corda."

The slot closed. He waited on the cobbles of the Merchant Tier with his hands in his coat pockets, the document folded against his ribs. His hands were cold. His stomach was empty — he'd left the house before breakfast, which had seemed like a reasonable idea at the time and now seemed like the kind of thing only an idiot did on the day he went to confront the woman who held his brother's life. The tier smelled of cedar and the faint floral wax they used on instrument cases, and underneath it the metallic sharpness of tuned bronze — not his father's bronze, which smelled of the forge and the floor grit of the workshop, but a cleaner, more deliberate version of it. The smell of bronze that belonged to people who thought of it as currency.

His left hand was shaking. He pressed it flat against his thigh.

The gate opened.

"She'll see you," the porter said, with the flat voice of a man who had expected this, or something like it, and found it neither interesting nor surprising.

The compound was larger inside than the walls suggested. There was a garden — not decorative, practical, with herbs growing in raised beds and a limestone path running through the center. The resonance chamber occupied the building on the left, its walls half a foot thicker than the others, the roof slightly lower. He felt the standing waves from its interior the moment he passed within twenty feet of it: a gentle pressure against his eardrums, like a room that had been holding its breath for a very long time.

The porter brought him to a receiving room in the main house. Stone floor, not wood — resonance management, he recognized that now. A table of dark wood with four chairs. The walls were bare except for a single iron bracket holding a small bell he'd never seen before. Bronze, old, with a patina that had gone past green into something close to black. He stood in the doorway and looked at it. It was tuned — he could tell that much from the shape of it. He couldn't tell to what.

Maret Corda was already seated at the table.

She didn't look at the door when he came in. She was looking at the window, or at something past it, her hands folded on the table with a bronze ring resting against the wood. He had spent weeks imagining this moment, and in the imagining she'd been larger somehow, more theatrical, the kind of person whose face would tell him something. She was none of those things. She was a small woman in dark green wool, gray-haired, upright in her chair with the posture of someone who had spent forty years in rooms where posture mattered.

She raised her left hand and struck the ring against the table edge. Once. A clean G, brief and precise.

Then she looked at him.

"Sit down," she said.

He sat. He put his hands on the table so she could see them and so he could watch them and so they would stop doing what they were doing, which was shaking badly enough that he was embarrassed by it. They did not stop.

"You know why I'm here," he said.

"I have a reasonable hypothesis. Tell me."

So he told her.

He told her about the sub-harmonic in the bells — the frequency Aldric Bellwright had composed into the original forty-seven, the one that ran beneath the Civic Pitch like a current beneath still water, too low for most ears. He told her what it meant: that the morning sequence the city used to calibrate every contract, every oath, every binding ratified for two hundred years had been built on a pitch that wasn't what it claimed to be. He told her he knew she had a copy of the journals, that Aldric's son had made it before the originals were sealed, and that she had been building the Corda family's position around the knowledge in those journals for thirty years. He told her the co-custodial clause in the thirty-day notice was pressure, not law — that the written document she held predated the tonal system and was unenforceable under any interval codex the Academy recognized.

He kept his voice even. He watched his hands.

She listened. Her hands stayed folded on the table. The ring rested against the wood.

He had expected, somewhere in the back of his mind, to hear the under-note — some performance, some polite evasion he would feel as a needle behind his left eye. There was nothing. The air between them was clean. She was not lying. She was not even shaping what she said to avoid lying. She was simply listening to him the way you listened to a contract being read aloud, taking in the terms.

When he finished, she was quiet for a moment.

"You're fourteen years old," she said, "and you've read the journals."

"Yes."

"Your father read them ten years ago." No inflection. Just the fact. "He chose the bells over your brother. I read them thirty years ago and made a different choice." She looked at him steadily. "The system is built on a lie. I know it. Your father knows it. Three scholars at the Academy know it. We have all made the same decision: keep it running, because the alternative is a city that wakes up one morning and has no legal ground to stand on. Every agreement anyone ever made — void. Every debt, every property claim, every marriage contract, every trade deal going back two hundred years." She paused. "What are you going to do that's different?"

The question sat in the room like a struck note.

"Free my brother," Cass said.

Maret looked at him. Something in her face shifted — not much, not in a way he could name. "Your brother's contract is void," she said. "Every contract is void. You know that now." She waited. "So why do you need me to release him?"

The air in the receiving room was very still. Outside the window, something moved in the herb garden — a bird, maybe, or just the wind off the bowl's rim.

He heard it the moment she said it, the way you hear a flat note in a chord you've been playing for months and suddenly cannot unhear. If the sub-harmonic in the bells had corrupted the foundational pitch — if every contract ratified against that pitch was built on a frequency that had no legitimate standing in any interval codex — then Perin's contract was void. Not voidable. Not contestable. Void, in the absolute sense, the way a binding sung by one voice alone was void, because the underlying condition for its existence had never been met.

Perin didn't know that. Perin was sitting in a room in this compound right now, composing contract music for a family that held a void claim over him, because no one had told him, because the knowledge was buried in sealed journals and thirty years of deliberate silence.

"If I tell him," Cass said, and stopped.

"Go on."

"If I tell him, and he acts on it — the Bellwright family's breach record becomes irrelevant. There's no valid contract to breach." He made himself say the rest. "But a fourteen-year-old boy walking in here and saying all contracts are void. Nobody's going to believe that. The Academy won't. The Civic Council won't."

"No," Maret said. "They won't."

"They'll say I need a physician."

"At best," she said. "At worst, you become a problem to be managed." She said it without cruelty, the way you said *the alloy's wrong* about a bell that couldn't be salvaged. "You need someone the city will listen to. And everyone the city listens to has already read the journals and chosen silence."

She let that sit for a moment.

"Including your father."

Something happened in Cass's chest. Not a collapse — more like a bell being struck in a closed room, the sound with nowhere to go.

He had known it already. He had known it since he'd read the blank pages, since he'd understood what his father's shaking hands meant, since he'd sat in Torvald's back room and seen the thirty-day notice and understood that his father had known about the sub-harmonic for ten years and kept setting the Civic Pitch every morning without a word. But hearing Maret say it, in that clean, undefended voice — two words, no under-note, no performance — had made it different. It had made it a fact in the world rather than a thing he was carrying alone.

He looked at her. At her hands on the table, the ring, the careful stillness of a woman who had spent forty years making herself unmovable.

"Why are you telling me any of this?" he said.

"Because you already knew it."

"That's not an answer."

She looked at him for a moment. "No," she agreed. "It isn't."

The room was quiet except for the distant sound of the city — a cart somewhere on the tier, the faint harmonic hum of the resonance chamber next door, the herb garden moving in the wind.

He had come here with the full picture. Every piece of it. And she had confirmed all of it inside five minutes and handed it back to him with the piece he'd been missing: the full picture was useless. He had the argument. He had the evidence. He had the truth, which in Cantamura was the one thing that couldn't get you anywhere without someone willing to sing it into the record.

His left hand was still shaking. He pressed it harder against the table.

"You've been using a lie you know is a lie," he said. The words came out louder than he intended. "To trap people into contracts you know are void. My brother has been in this house for ten years. He was sixteen." His voice was doing something he didn't like, a thinness at the edges of it, and he couldn't stop it. "You took him because you could. Because my father was too much of a coward to say no and you knew it. You knew it before you walked through our door." He stopped. His hands were shaking on the table and he could see them shaking and he couldn't make them stop. "That's not pragmatism. That's just cruelty with better vocabulary."

The last sentence surprised him. He hadn't planned it. He wasn't proud of it, exactly, but he wasn't sorry for it either.

Maret looked at him for a long moment. The room was very still.

"Yes," she said. "I know."

Her hand went to the ring on the table, but she didn't strike it. She turned it once between her fingers — a small movement, something involuntary in it, a gesture that belonged to a different person than the one who had been sitting here.

"My father sang folk intervals," she said. "Outer-district bindings. You know what that means — two notes a quarter-tone off the Academy standard, the kind of tuning you get when your family has been setting pitch by ear for three generations with no notation and no reference bells. The Academy called it acoustically uncodified." She set the ring down on the table. "When I was seventeen I walked into the Academy entrance examination and sang the binding sequence my father had taught me. The examiner stopped me on the third interval." She was not looking at Cass. She was looking at the ring. "He said it was wrong. My father had tuned by ear his whole life, as his father had, as his father's father had, and that ear had settled real disputes and held real oaths and kept real people to real promises, and the examiner said it was wrong, and I stood in that room and couldn't say otherwise because I didn't have their language yet."

Cass said nothing.

"I learned their language. I learned it better than most of them knew it. I built every piece of this —" a small gesture, taking in the room, the compound — "and my father died during the second year of my apprenticeship and I did not go home for the funeral because I had a contract examination the following morning that I could not afford to fail."

The room was very quiet.

Her left hand moved to her sternum — a brief press of her palm against her chest, as if checking for something, and then her hand came back to the table and she was still again. The movement was so quick he almost missed it. But he didn't miss it.

"You're right that it's cruelty," she said. "I don't have a better word for it either." She struck the ring against the table edge. Once. The G rang out — pure, standard-pitched, nothing like the quarter-tone her father had sung — and held in the air for a moment, and was gone.

Cass stood up.

His hands were still shaking. He was hungry, and tired, and the dull ache behind his left eye from the morning had not gone anywhere, and he was standing in Maret Corda's receiving room having just called her cruel to her face, and she had said *yes, I know*, and somehow that was the worst possible answer she could have given him. There was nothing to push against in it. Nothing to argue with. Just a woman who had made her choice thirty years ago and was still sitting with it, and knew exactly what it had cost, and had paid it anyway.

He walked to the door.

At the threshold, he turned back.

She was watching him. The ring rested against the table. She had not moved.

"My brother's intervals are probably a quarter-tone off your standard too," Cass said. "He learned from the same person I did."

He didn't wait to see what that did to her face. He walked out.

---

The porter let him out the gate without a word.

The cobbles of the Merchant Tier were cold under his boots, and the cedar-and-wax smell of the tier was already behind him, replaced by the open air of the city — bread from a bakery somewhere on the south slope, the metallic sharpness of the afternoon, the low constant murmur of Cantamura going about its business with its careful, measured speech.

He walked.

Up toward the mid-ring and then back down toward the Bell Quarter, through streets that carried their sounds the way the bowl always carried them — conversations he caught the edges of, a child counting out a transaction at a market stall, the distant harmonic hum of the Bell Tower above the rooftops. He kept his hands in his pockets and his head down.

The under-note was quiet. It was usually quieter in the open air, away from courts and contract rooms, and today it was almost entirely silent — just the clean sounds of the city, no lies worth hearing in the ambient murmur of the afternoon. He was grateful for that. His left eye had a dull ache behind it from the morning, from the runner and the document and the thirty-day notice, and he didn't need more.

She had no self-deception left. That was the part he kept coming back to. He had expected someone who believed in what they were doing, or who had convinced themselves they believed in it, or who at minimum performed belief well enough that the performance had become its own kind of truth. He had expected the under-note.

Instead she had said *yes, I know*, and struck the ring.

No performance. No evasion. Just a woman who had built an empire on a void and knew it and had made her peace with knowing it, and who had looked at a fourteen-year-old boy telling her the truth she already had and seen, accurately, that the truth couldn't touch her.

He stopped walking.

He was at the edge of the Bell Quarter, at the corner where the tier dropped down toward the Civic Square, and he could see the Bell Tower from here — the upper third of it above the roofline, the bronze of the great bells just visible in the late-afternoon light. He stood there and looked at it. Every day of his life from roughly this angle, from the workshop window, from the street on his way to the Academy, from his bedroom in the small hours when the pain was bad and sleep wouldn't come.

*Everyone the city listens to has already read the journals and chosen silence.*

He turned the phrase over. Not *everyone who knows* — she had said *everyone the city listens to*. As if those were the same category. As if the only people worth counting were the people the city had already decided to hear.

He stood at the corner for a while longer, long enough for the light to shift, for the afternoon murmur of the city to thin toward the quieter sounds of early evening. Then he put his hands deeper in his pockets and started toward home.

The document folded against his ribs pressed against him with each step. Steady as a heartbeat. Steady as a bell that had been rung and hadn't finished ringing yet.

Thirty days before Maret Corda put her hand on the bells.

He had the knowledge that every Bellwright before him had chosen to bury. He had thirty days. He had a choice that none of the blank pages had been willing to make.

The workshop light was on when he got home. He could see his father's shadow through the glass. He didn't go in. He went upstairs, lay on his bed in the dark, and stared at the ceiling until morning.
