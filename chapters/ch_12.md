# Chapter Twelve: Volume Four

He was back in the locked room before the Civic Pitch finished its fourth bar.

The bells were still ringing their sequence across the city when he came down the stairs, past the kitchen where his father's teacup sat untouched on the table, and let himself in with the key he'd taken from the hook the previous afternoon and not returned. The three bells hung in their corners. The journals waited in their row.

He didn't touch the bells.

He pulled Volume Two from the shelf and sat on the floor with his back against the wall, because there was no chair, and opened it.

---

Volume Two was shorter than Volume One. The second-generation Bellwright — a man named Corvan, who had apparently been more interested in the practical work of bell maintenance than in his grandfather's theoretical obsessions — had recorded the births of his children, the replacement of two bells in the outer ring, a dispute with the Weavers' District over the pitch of the morning sequence, and almost nothing else. The handwriting was large and slightly careless. A practical man, Corvan. The kind of man who ate his breakfast and got on with things.

The entry about the question was on the last page before the binding changed to Volume Three.

*Father's device. We do not speak of it.*

Cass read it twice. Turned the page.

Volume Three was forty pages of bell maintenance, two pages of a dispute with the Academy over the licensing of a new resonance chamber in the Merchant Tier, and nothing else. No mention of the question at all. The third-generation Bellwright had apparently decided that even eight words was too many.

He set Volume Three aside and picked up Volume Four.

The handwriting changed immediately. Smaller, more careful, the letters formed with the precision of someone who had been trained to write for an audience. An Academy man, this Bellwright — Cass could tell from the first paragraph, where the author introduced himself as Aldric's great-great-grandson and a lecturer in acoustic theory at the Cantamura Academy of Civic Harmony. His name was Tomas. He had strong opinions about the relationship between interval and obligation, and he was not afraid to express them at length.

Cass read slowly. The argument was dense and he had to follow it carefully — Tomas had built it the way a good contract was built, each clause depending on the one before, the whole thing moving toward a conclusion that felt inevitable once you saw where it was going. The acoustic properties of the original bells. The specific alloy Aldric had used. The harmonic structure of the Civic Pitch, which Tomas had apparently spent three years measuring with equipment he'd built himself. The sub-harmonic. The question it asked.

*Do you consent to be bound?*

Tomas had reached it by page thirty-one. He'd known exactly what it was. He'd laid out the implications in four careful paragraphs that Cass read with his jaw tight and his If the question required consent, and the question was built into every binding ratified against the morning Civic Pitch, then every binding ratified without genuine consent was technically —

Page forty ended mid-sentence. The next page was blank. The forty-two after it were blank. The binding was still tight, the pages still cream-colored and flat, as if they'd been waiting this whole time.

Cass sat on the floor of the locked room and looked at the blank pages.

 Every binding ratified without consent. Every contract, every verdict, every obligation the city ran on — all of it technically void, technically always-void, going back two hundred years to the morning Aldric Bellwright struck the first Civic Pitch and asked a question the city had never answered.

Cass closed the journal. Set it on the floor beside him.

The three bells hung in their corners. The one on the left was slightly out of true, its mount tilted a fraction of a degree — he'd noticed it yesterday and noticed it again now,. The room was very quiet. The Civic Pitch had finished while he was reading.

He stayed on the floor for a while.

---

The Academy library opened at the second bell of morning and closed at the sixth bell of afternoon. Cass arrived at the third bell, paid his reader's fee with coins that rang at the right pitch against the testing plate, and went directly to the periodicals shelf.

The Voss paper was where it had always been: *On the Persistence of Sub-harmonic Frequencies in Cast Bronze Instruments*, by one A. Voss, published in the Academy's journal eleven years ago and apparently read by almost no one since, judging by the condition of the binding. Cass pulled it out and carried it to a table in the back corner, away from the two Academy students arguing over a contract theory text near the window.

He'd read this paper before. Twice, in fact — once in the early weeks of his investigation, when he'd been looking for any mention of the sub-harmonic, and once after he'd found the journals, when he understood better what to look for. He'd noted then that the argument stopped on page eleven.

He read it again now, with what he knew.

A. Voss — Senna Voss, he was almost certain, though the paper used only the initial — had approached the problem from the other direction. Not from the journals, which she wouldn't have had access to, but from the acoustic measurements. She'd spent what must have been years taking careful readings of the morning Civic Pitch, using instruments she described in a footnote as "of non-standard design, calibrated to extend the measurable frequency range below the conventionally recognized lower threshold." She'd found the sub-harmonic. She'd characterized it precisely — frequency, interval relationship to the surface sequence, the specific harmonic structure that made it distinct from simple resonance artifacts.

Page ten was the measurement data. Page eleven was the beginning of the interpretation.

It stopped six lines down.

*The implications of this frequency, if its origin is intentional rather than incidental to the casting process, would require a systematic re-examination of —*

And then nothing. The sentence ended without a period. The rest of the page was blank. The paper had been submitted and published in this state, with an editorial note at the front that described it as "a preliminary report, with further analysis forthcoming." The further analysis had never appeared.

Voss had gotten there. Had understood what she was looking at. Had stopped writing.

Cass sat with the paper in his hands and felt the specific exhaustion of understanding something that didn't help him.

He put the paper back on the shelf.

The two Academy students were still arguing by the window. One of them was wrong about the resolution requirements for a minor-seventh codicil, and Cass could hear it in the precision of the other student's responses — too careful, too patient, the patience of someone who has decided not to correct directly. He walked past them and out into the corridor.

The Academy's east arcade was quiet at this hour. His footsteps carried further than they should have, bouncing off the limestone columns and coming back slightly delayed, slightly changed, the way sound always behaved in the spaces between buildings here. He counted the delay automatically. Roughly forty feet to the nearest reflective surface. He was doing it before he'd decided to.

He needed to talk to Torvald.

Torvald Hess lived above a tally-song registry on the west edge of the Weavers' District, a fifteen-minute walk from the Academy. Cass had been there four times now and still got turned around on the last street, which looked like the second-to-last street until you noticed the blue-green stain on the doorstep of the corner building — Torvald's hands had left it there years ago, he'd explained once, when he used to carry the dye samples himself. The stain had never come out. He seemed to find this satisfying.

Cass found the stain and turned left.

Torvald answered the door before Cass finished knocking, which meant he'd heard the footsteps on the stairs. He looked at Cass's face and stood aside.

"Sit down," he said. "You look like you've been reading."

"I have been reading."

"I mean the other kind." Torvald went to the small stove in the corner and lifted the kettle. "You want tea?"

"No." Cass sat in the wooden chair that had become, over four visits, his chair by default. The room smelled of old paper and the faint chemical sweetness of the district — dye-vats a few streets over, carried on the morning air. "I found what the blank pages were."

Torvald set the kettle down. He turned and cupped his left ear slightly. He was paying close attention. "Tell me."

"Volume Four. The fourth-generation Bellwright — he was an Academy lecturer, a theorist, spent years working through the acoustic implications of the sub-harmonic. The argument is good. It's — it's the same argument I've been building, but complete. Rigorous. He got all the way to the conclusion."

"Which is."

"If the question requires consent, then any binding ratified without genuine consent has been technically void since the first morning Civic Pitch. Every contract. Every verdict. Every obligation the city runs on. Writing that down doesn't fix any of it. It just makes the whole structure fall at once."

Torvald was quiet. He picked up his tea, which had been sitting on the table already, and drank from it.

"So he left it blank," Cass said. "Tomas. The fourth-generation Bellwright. He got there and he left it blank. And the second generation got there before him and wrote eight words. And the third generation didn't write anything at all. And the Voss paper — I just re-read it, at the library. Voss got there too, from the acoustic measurements alone, without the journals. Stopped on page eleven."

"I know Senna Voss," Torvald said.

"I thought it might be her."

"She doesn't talk about it." He set the tea down. "She hasn't talked about it in eleven years."

"Because she made the same choice."

"Because she made the same choice." Torvald sat down across from Cass, the chair creaking under his weight. "What did you come here to tell me?"

"The bells contain something Aldric put there deliberately. Something that's been in the Civic Pitch for two hundred years. It's been suppressed — suppressed by every person who found it choosing the same thing. Silence. Because saying it out loud doesn't free anyone. It just breaks everything."

"You said this to your father?"

"My father's known for ten years. Since the night Perin signed."

Torvald's expression didn't change. He'd suspected as much, probably — he was a man who built arguments from evidence, and the evidence of Eddan Bellwright's shaking hands had been available to anyone paying attention. He didn't ask how Cass knew. He trusted the ear.

"All right," Torvald said. "What's it void?"

Cass looked at him.

"Perin's contract," Torvald said. "That's why you've been pulling this thread. Does it void Perin's contract?"

"It voids every contract," Cass said.

Torvald went still.

Not surprised, exactly. The stillness of a man absorbing a weight he'd been told to expect but hadn't quite believed until it landed. He sat with it for a moment. His stained hands rested on his knees. Outside, somewhere down the street, someone was hammering — a steady, ordinary sound that had nothing to do with any of this.

"That's not a solution," Torvald said. "That's a different problem."

"I know," Cass said.

"Your brother is still bound."

"I know."

"And the people who've been coerced into bad contracts in the outer districts for sixty years are still bound. And the Weavers' District families who signed near the Fade Line without a broker are still bound. And voiding everything at once doesn't free any of them — it just creates a new fight that the people with the most resources win. Same people who've been winning."

"Yes."

Torvald picked up his tea again.

"So what do you want to do with it?" Torvald asked.

Cass opened his mouth. Closed it.

"I don't know yet," he said, and it cost him something to say it, but it was the only true thing available.

Torvald nodded, as if this were an acceptable answer. Maybe it was.

The knock at the door came before either of them spoke again. Torvald crossed the room and opened it. A boy stood on the landing — twelve, maybe thirteen, one of the Compact's runners, breathing like he'd taken the stairs fast. He held out a folded paper. Torvald took it, glanced at the seal, and held it toward Cass.

"Addressed to the Bellwright family," Torvald said. "Runner was told to find you."

The seal was the House of Corda's — a tuning fork pressed into dark wax. Cass broke it.

The document was formal. It was exactly the kind of document you'd find in an Academy archive fifty years from now, properly composed, every clause in its correct position. He read it once fast and once slow.

Maret Corda, head of the House of Corda, was invoking the co-custodial clause of an agreement dated twelve years prior between the Corda family and the Bellwright family. Under the terms of that clause, the House of Corda would assume shared authority over the forty-seven civic bells within thirty days unless the Bellwright family could demonstrate, to the satisfaction of a licensed Court Singer, that the six repaired bells in current service met the original custodianship standard established at the city's founding.

Thirty days.

The runner was still standing in the doorway. Cass looked up at him.

"How was this delivered?" Cass asked. "To my house?"

"Sung, sir," the runner said. He was trying to sound professional and mostly managing it. "Formal messenger. Proper form. My master at the registry heard it go and sent me to find you."

Cass read the document a third time. The language was clean. The clauses held together. Every term was exactly what it claimed to be — he could feel the absence of an under-note as you feel the absence of pain when you're expecting it, a negative space that was almost more disorienting than the pain itself.

Maret believed every word of this. She wasn't lying. She was certain of her ground, certain enough to send a formal messenger to sing a notice in proper form to the Bellwright house, certain enough to start a thirty-day clock in front of the whole district.

The six repaired bells. He knew what his father knew about those bells. The old-alloy stock was running too low to replace them. The repairs would hold for three years, maybe, and his father had known that when he made them, and the custodianship standard required bells that held their tune without repair for —

He folded the document.

"Thank you," he said to the runner. The boy left.

Torvald was watching him.

"Thirty days," Cass said.

"I heard."

"She's invoking the co-custodial clause. She wants shared authority over the forty-seven bells unless we can prove the repaired ones meet standard." He set the document on the table between them. "The repaired ones don't meet standard. My father knows it. She probably knows it too."

"So in thirty days, the House of Corda has a hand on the bells."

"Yes."

Torvald looked at the document. He touched it with two fingers, not picking it up, just touching the edge of it. "And the bells set the Civic Pitch. And the Civic Pitch validates every contract ratified each day."

"Yes."

"So in thirty days, if the Bellwright family can't prove the repairs hold —"

"Maret Corda has a hand on the pitch," Cass said. "On every contract ratified in the city."

The hammering down the street had stopped. The room was quiet in the way rooms get when the thing you've been building toward has finally arrived and there's nothing left to do except sit with it.

Cass looked at the document. He looked at his own hands, which were steady —

Thirty days to prove the repairs held. Thirty days before Maret Corda put her hand on the bells.

He had the knowledge that every Bellwright before him had chosen to bury. He had thirty days. He had a choice that none of the blank pages had been willing to make.

He picked up the document and put it in his coat.

"I need to go home," he said.

---

He didn't go home. Not right away.

He walked south through the Weavers' District, past the dye-vats and the shuttered looms, and then he kept walking, down toward the Fade Line where the careful speech of the inner city thinned into ordinary noise. The afternoon was cold. His coat was thin. He walked until his legs ached and the Bell Tower was small behind him, just a shape above the rooftops, and then he stood at the Fade Line and listened to the sound of a city that didn't know what he knew.

A woman was arguing with a vendor about the price of candles. Two children ran past him, shouting. A dog barked from a doorway. Ordinary sounds, unmanaged, the kind of noise people made when they weren't afraid of being heard.

Every contract ratified today was void. Every one ratified yesterday. Every one the city had ever sung into the air between parties who believed they were being bound.

The woman bought her candles. The children disappeared around a corner. The dog lost interest.

He turned back toward the inner city. The walk home took forty minutes, and he used every one of them, not thinking — not making plans, not running through options, not doing the arithmetic of thirty days — just walking, carrying the weight of what he'd read in the locked room, letting his body do the work of moving through space while the knowledge settled into him the way a bell's tone settled into stone.

By the time he reached the Bell Quarter the evening bells were ringing from the outer districts. The workshop light was on. He could see his father's shadow through the glass.

He didn't go in. He went upstairs, lay on his bed in the dark, and stared at the ceiling until morning.
