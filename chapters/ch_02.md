# Chapter Two: Song-Broker Weather

The Academy's theory rooms smelled of chalk dust and floor resin — sharp on the first breath, gone by the second, absorbed into the general smell of sixty students who had been in the same building since dawn.

Proctor Fen was demonstrating binding intervals again. He did this every third session, regardless of where the syllabus said they were meant to be, because he believed — and said so, at least once a month — that a student who couldn't ratify a basic contract under pressure had learned nothing, regardless of their written assessments.

"Volunteer," he said, with the expression of a man who had been disappointed by volunteers before.

No one moved. Cass kept his eyes on the notation board, where Fen had chalked out a descending fifth with the slightly aggressive strokes of someone who had written the same interval eight thousand times and still found people getting it wrong.

"Volunteer," Fen said again. "Or I assign one."

A boy named Ossian stood up, faster than Cass expected from Ossian, who usually waited to see which way the room was leaning before he committed to anything.

"Thank you, Ossian." Fen produced his pitch-pipe — small brass, worn smooth on the barrel where his thumb rested. He sounded the reference note, a clean A, and waited for it to settle. The theory room was in the old building, tight-corridored stone, and the A filled it differently than it would have in the limestone arcades: more absorption, less carry. Cass let it settle in his chest anyway.

"Ratification first," Fen said. "Major third from the reference. You take the upper voice. I'll take the lower."

The major third: two notes that locked together like a handshake. When you heard it done right, there was a small structural click to it — not loud, not dramatic, just the acoustic fact of two pitches finding each other and agreeing.

Ossian sang the upper note. Fen sang the lower. The interval locked, clean and correct. Cass heard the click.

"Now the binding," Fen said. "Descending fifth from the ratification note."

A descending fifth was the door closing. The upper note fell to the lower, and the distance between them — seven half-steps, a specific ratio the air between two people could hold — sealed whatever the ratification had opened. Up was refusal. Down was done.

They sang it.

And then something went wrong.

It happened at the bottom of the interval, in Ossian's voice. The note arrived — but it arrived somewhere it wasn't supposed to be. Not dramatically wrong. Not wrong enough that a person without training would catch it. Just slightly, specifically, precisely flat.

Cass's jaw tightened. A small involuntary thing, like touching a bruise.

He knew that sensation. Not the under-note — nothing like the under-note — but the body's recognition of a note that had missed its target. The interval had collapsed into something that was not a binding and not quite a rejection but a third thing: a provisional agreement. Legal force until sundown. No longer.

Fen stopped singing. He looked at Ossian with unsurprised disappointment.

"Well," he said.

"I'm sorry," Ossian said. "The acoustics in here —"

Not the clean needle of a deliberate lie. Something muddier — the texture of a person who had repeated something to themselves until the repetition felt like truth. The under-note couldn't quite decide what to do with it. It flickered behind Cass's eye, there and not there, like a candle in a draft.

"The acoustics in here are identical to the acoustics in the Civic Court's secondary chamber, which is where you will be composing contracts for paying clients if you don't flat your ratification notes." Fen looked at the class. "What did Ossian just produce?"

"Provisional agreement," said three people at once.

"Expiring when?"

"Sundown," said two people.

"Which means," Fen said, "that Ossian is currently provisionally bound to carry my books until sundown. The question before the class is whether this binding is enforceable under Codex Volume One, Section Twelve, given that the provisional agreement was produced in a pedagogical context and not a commercial one, and given that I have not yet stated what terms the provisional agreement covers."

"The terms have to be stated before ratification," said a girl in the second row. "You can't retroactively assign terms to a provisional."

"Volume One, Section Four. Ratification requires stated terms."

"So Ossian's not bound."

"Ossian produced the interval. The interval has acoustic force regardless of whether the terms were stated."

"That's not how it —"

"It's how the physics work. The Codex doesn't repeal physics."

Cass was watching Ossian, who was standing at the front of the room calculating how much this was going to cost him. He felt a faint sympathy. The flat had been easy to make. The stone rooms had genuinely different acoustics, and a nervous student singing for the class could miss the bottom note without hearing it — the ear wanted the note to be right and sometimes convinced the brain it was.

The debate continued for several minutes. Fen watched it with the quiet satisfaction of a man who had planned this exact outcome. Eventually he raised one hand.

"The answer," he said, "is that it depends on which proctor is marking your case. Which is the most honest thing I will say to you all term. Carry my books, Ossian."

Ossian sat down.

From two rows ahead and one seat left, a voice said: "The terms were implicit in the pedagogical context. Demonstration of a binding produces an implicit offer of the demonstration's natural consequence. Ossian accepted the demonstration voluntarily. The terms were present. He's bound."

Fen turned. Cass turned with him.

The girl who had spoken was making a small notation on her paper, fingering an interval against the desktop — two fingers, quick and unconscious, working through a phrase while thinking about something else. Dark hair pulled back without ceremony. The upright posture of someone trained in breath control since before they could read.

Lenne Ashvale. He had been in the same theory class with her for two months and had exchanged perhaps thirty words with her, most of them about notation.

"Interesting interpretation," Fen said. "Cite the Codex."

"Volume Two, Section Nine. Implicit terms in demonstration contracts." She looked up from her paper. "Usually applied to commercial demonstrations. There's no case law that explicitly extends it to pedagogical ones. But there's no case law that explicitly excludes it either."

"So Ossian is bound or not bound depending on a gap in the Codex."

"Yes."

Fen smiled. Unusual. "Ossian," he said. "Carry my books."

Ossian stood up again, slowly, visibly lost in a legal argument that had left him two turns ago.

Cass put his hand up.

Fen looked at him. The class looked at him. He was aware of Lenne Ashvale looking at him.

"Volume Two, Section Nine requires the demonstration to have commercial intent," he said. "Implicit or otherwise. There's no commercial intent in a classroom exercise. The section doesn't apply."

Lenne turned around in her seat. "Intent doesn't have to be present at the time of ratification. It can be inferred from the context of the parties."

"He's a proctor demonstrating an interval. She's a student arguing he should make a student carry his books. Where's the commercial context?"

"The outcome has commercial implications. Ossian's grade reflects his performance. His grade affects his licensure prospects. His licensure is commercial."

"That's three steps removed from the ratification."

"The Codex doesn't specify how many steps."

"The Codex also doesn't say you can extend a commercial section to cover whatever you want by building a long enough chain."

Fen was watching this with the expression of a man who had planned for a different outcome and found this one acceptable.

"The section applies," Lenne said. "Ossian's bound."

"The section doesn't apply," Cass said. "Ossian's not bound. And either way, Proctor Fen can't actually make him carry the books, because a provisional agreement requires both parties to have legal standing in the transaction, and Fen is an Academy employee conducting a demonstration, which means he's acting in an institutional capacity, which means the binding runs to the Academy, not to Fen personally, and the Academy hasn't authorized book-carrying as a penalty."

The room was quiet for a moment.

"Volume Three, Section Two," Cass added. "Institutional capacity and personal liability."

Lenne turned back around to face the front. She picked up her pen. He couldn't see her face.

"Ossian," Fen said, "sit down." He put the pitch-pipe back in his coat. "Both arguments have merit. Both arguments have flaws. This is the condition you will practice in for your entire career. The Codex is not a complete document. It never has been." He looked at the notation board. "Read chapters four through six before next session. We'll be examining provisional agreements in commercial contexts. Actual commercial contexts."

---

In the corridor outside, Cass was pulling his bag onto his shoulder when Lenne fell into step beside him.

"Volume Three, Section Two," she said. "Nice."

"It's in the Codex."

"Everything's in the Codex. The question is whether Fen agreed with you."

"He didn't disagree."

"He also didn't agree. He said both arguments have merit."

"Which means neither of us lost."

"Which means neither of us won." She held the door to the arcade and he walked through without thinking about it. Morning light came in level through the limestone columns, bright and cold. "You heard the flat before Fen's expression changed."

He looked at her sideways.

"You went still," she said. "Right when Ossian hit the bottom note."

"The room's got poor absorption. The flat was audible."

"I heard flat too. I didn't hear how flat."

He considered not answering. There was no good reason not to answer. "Quarter-tone," he said.

She walked beside him for a few steps. Not looking at him — looking at the arcade columns, or at something past them. "I heard wrong. I didn't hear *how* wrong."

"Stone rooms. Less scatter."

"Is that all it is."

That wasn't a question. He didn't answer it.

"Volume Two, Section Nine really does apply," she said. "The chain of commercial implication is legitimate."

"The chain is too long. You can connect anything to commercial intent if you're allowed unlimited steps."

"You can connect anything to anything if you're allowed unlimited steps. That's not an argument against the chain, it's an argument for limiting step-count. Which the Codex should specify and doesn't."

"So your argument is that the Codex has a gap."

"My argument is that the Codex has a gap and the gap favors my interpretation until someone closes it." She adjusted the strap of her folder. "My mother keeps her voting records in a separate book — the one she doesn't show the Academy. Even she knows the gaps are there."

He stopped walking. She stopped a step later and turned back.

"That's not what an argument is," he said. "An argument is a reason the interpretation is correct. 'The Codex doesn't say I'm wrong' isn't a reason."

"It's a legal reason."

"It's a procedural reason. Those aren't the same thing."

She looked at him for a moment with the expression of someone recalibrating a measurement. Not offended. Interested in the same way she'd been interested in the demonstration — like she was taking a reading.

"Ossian was bound," she said.

"Ossian was not bound."

"We'll see what Fen's assessment says."

"We will."

She turned into the covered walkway toward the upper arcades. He went the other way, toward the gate and the market. He was aware of her watching him go for a second before she didn't.

He was also aware that he'd enjoyed that more than he'd expected to, which was an annoying thing to notice.

---

The market near the Fade Line ran along the lower edge of the Academy District and down two streets into the territory where the acoustic rules went soft. You could tell where the boundary was on a calm day by the volume: inner-district conversations carried in that muffled, careful way of a city that knew its words traveled, and then at a certain point the conversations got louder and less careful, and people argued at normal human volume, and a man three stalls down could shout a price without the whole street hearing it.

Today there was a headwind coming down from the bowl's rim, pushing the Fade Line inward by half a block. The market noise pressed at him from all sides — stall-keepers calling prices, the clatter of copper coins struck against testing surfaces, bread and tallow cut through with dye-stink from a cloth merchant's cart that hadn't yet sold enough to justify the trip home.

He needed flux. Grade-three tin flux for bell-metal repairs, the kind his father used for maintenance work that didn't require the forge at full heat — tightening a hairline crack, re-seating a clapper pin. The merchant who stocked it was on the third row, past the cloth and the grain and past the stall with the woman who had been tuning her bronze weights slightly sharp for six months.

Cass had noticed the weights three months ago. A sharp weight didn't look wrong; it looked like every other weight on the stall. But when you struck it against the testing block, it rang half a tone above where it should have been, and a contract ratified using it as a reference tone would be sitting on a crooked foundation. The woman's customers were all from the outer districts, where the Fade Line made acoustic boundaries ambiguous already. He had told himself this was not his business. He was mostly convinced.

The flux merchant was a short man named Brae who kept his stock in labeled jars and knew every grade by sight without checking the labels. Cass told him what he needed, Brae found it, they agreed on a price in three exchanges — no song-broker required, both of them speaking in the clipped shorthand of a transaction that didn't need legal force to be real. Cass paid in two copper quarters, struck them against the testing block out of habit, heard the clean A of both. He pocketed the flux.

He was turning to leave when the argument started two stalls down.

A cloth merchant and a woman in a weaver's apron, both past negotiating and not yet at resolution. The cloth merchant had the specific expression of someone who believed he was right and was finding that being right was not, by itself, sufficient. The woman was pointing at a length of cloth with the focused energy of someone making an argument she had already made twice.

Between them, a song-broker was working.

Cass stopped.

He had the flux. He had an assignment due tomorrow. He was already late. He watched anyway.

The broker was composing on the fly. What market brokers did — no resonance chamber, no hour of preparation, just two angry people and the ambient acoustics of a market day and whatever was in your head. She was humming to herself, working through the interval structure before she sang it aloud, moving through the key of F, building toward the ratification sequence.

Cass heard it forming.

The major third was correct — that handshake-click of two notes locking together. The ratification note was correct. The descending fifth began correctly, fell the right distance, and then —

Something clenched in his jaw. Back teeth, on the left side, like biting down on something that shouldn't be there.

He stood very still.

The fifth had almost landed. Almost. The resolution tone — the final note that said *done, settled, no going back* — was sitting a fraction sharp of center. Not enough to hear in the ordinary sense. Not enough that the cloth merchant would feel anything. Not enough that the woman in the apron would feel anything, or that the broker herself would catch it, composing in real time in a headwind with the Fade Line pressing inward and the note almost right.

But his jaw had caught it before his mind named it.

He knew what this was. He had read about it in Volume One, Section Fourteen: a lame chord. An interval that looked complete and wasn't. A binding that would hold one party without holding the other — which one depended on which voice sang which part of the ratification, and he couldn't determine that yet from here, but the structure was wrong, and in two minutes the broker would sing it into the air between these two people and one of them would walk away believing they had a binding agreement.

He had taken one step toward the stall before he stopped himself.

He didn't have credentials. He didn't have guild standing. He couldn't walk up to a licensed broker in the middle of a commission and say *your resolution tone is sharp* without being able to cite a volume and section, without being able to put his name to a formal objection, without being a person the market recognized as someone whose ear meant something legally.

Three years left at the Academy. Then a license examination. Then a probationary period. Then, if everything went right, he would be a person whose observations could be recorded and acted on.

He stood at the edge of the stall and watched the broker finish composing. Watched her pitch the ratification interval. Watched both parties sing their parts, heard the lame chord settle into the air between them with the faint structural wrongness of a bell that had been repaired instead of recast.

The cloth merchant shook the woman's hand. Both of them looked satisfied.

One of them was wrong about what had just happened. He didn't know which one yet.

He turned away and walked toward the market's edge.

---

There was an old man standing where the stalls thinned out and the road started down toward the Weavers' District. Large, going soft through the middle, with hands stained a permanent blue-green that Cass recognized as dye-work — the color you got from decades near indigo vats, the kind that didn't wash out. He had one hand cupped around his left ear, the posture of someone with partial deafness trying to hear something specific in a loud space.

He was watching the cloth merchant's stall.

He said, to no one in particular, at the comfortable volume of a man from the outer districts where talking to yourself in public was not considered remarkable: "Forty years of that. Academy trains them to compose fast, not right. The people who get hurt by a lame chord aren't the ones who can afford to appeal it."

He wasn't talking to Cass. He was talking to the market, or to himself, or to the general principle of the thing. He watched the cloth merchant stack his recovered bolt of fabric. Nothing in his face suggested surprise.

Cass kept walking.

He made it six steps before he stopped. He turned back.

"You heard it," he said.

The old man looked at him. He had the eyes of someone who had been underestimated long enough to find it useful.

"Heard what," the old man said.

"The lame chord. You were watching the stall before the ratification. You knew it was coming."

"I've been watching that broker work this market for three years." He lowered his hand from his ear. "Don't need to hear it sharp to know what she builds."

"She's been doing it for three years?"

"Not always. Not on purpose, maybe. But the outer-district clients don't come back to appeal, so she never finds out when she's wrong." He looked at Cass with a directness that had nothing polite in it. "You heard it."

"The resolution tone was sharp."

"How sharp?"

Cass thought about the sensation in his jaw. The precise location of the wrongness. "Eighth of a tone. Maybe less."

The old man was quiet for a moment. He struck his blue-green thumb against his forefinger, a small percussive gesture, like testing a surface. "Most trained singers wouldn't catch an eighth. Not in market conditions."

"I'm not a trained singer."

"I know what you are. Bellwright boy. I've seen you come through this market before." He extended one stained hand. "Torvald Hess."

Cass shook it. "Cass."

"I know." Hess looked back at the cloth merchant's stall. "The woman in the apron is going to find out in about two weeks that the binding she thinks she has isn't what she thinks it is. By then the cloth merchant will have sold the stock. She'll file an appeal, if she can afford the fee, and the appeal will take four months, and by the time it resolves she'll have lost the season." He said all of this without particular bitterness, the way someone describes weather. "That's how it goes."

"Someone should have stopped the broker before she sang it."

"Someone with credentials should have."

"I don't have credentials."

"I know." Hess looked at him again. "You're at the Academy."

"Three more years."

"Three more years." He said it the way you'd say *a long time*. "The woman in the apron doesn't have three more years. She needed someone today."

Cass had no answer for that. He was aware of how young he sounded without having said anything.

"Come find me when you have those credentials," Hess said. He turned back toward the Weavers' District road. "I'm in the Tanner's Quarter most days. Ask for Hess — people know where I am." He paused. "You've got a good ear, boy. Better than good. Don't let them sand it down."

He walked away without looking back.

Cass stood at the market's edge for a moment. The cloth merchant was laughing at something a neighboring stall-keeper had said. The woman in the weaver's apron was folding her copy of the ratification record, tucking it into her coat with the careful satisfaction of someone who believed they had protected themselves.

He turned away before he had to keep watching it.

---

His father was at the lathe when Cass got back. He could hear the rhythm of it before he opened the gate — the pauses where Eddan stopped to check the pitch-gauge, the small adjustments that meant the work was going well. The workshop smelled of metal-dust and linseed oil, the faint char of the morning's forge work still in the air, and the bells in progress along the walls were humming faintly in sympathy with the lathe's vibration.

Cass set the flux on the supply shelf, labeled side out. He heard his father's hands pause on the lathe and then continue. That was acknowledgment enough.

He went through the connecting door into the house, meaning to drop his bag and come back to help with the secondary bell work.

There was a letter on the kitchen table.

Face-down. The paper was thin but stiff, cream-gray — the Corda compound's stock. He had seen four letters in the last year, each of them face-down when he found them.

He turned this one over.

Perin's handwriting was neat and very small, the handwriting of someone who had learned to fit a lot into a small space.

*The weather here has been cold and damp. The east wing resonance chamber needs re-tuning — the humidity has been affecting the wall panels, and the reference tone is running approximately two cents flat of true. I've submitted a maintenance request. They'll get to it eventually.*

Cass read that twice. Two cents flat. His father would know the number immediately — Eddan tracked the Corda compound's resonance chambers the way he tracked all the secondary installations in the city, as a professional habit, the way a carpenter noted which doors were warping. Two cents flat was significant. It meant every contract ratified in that chamber for the past several weeks had been built on a reference tone that was slightly, specifically wrong.

He kept reading.

*The new commission work is interesting from a technical standpoint. The broker they've brought in from Veth uses iron-bell references rather than bronze, which produces a slightly different harmonic envelope around the ratification intervals. I've been studying the difference. The Veth approach has some structural advantages in outdoor contract settings where bronze would be affected by temperature variance. I don't expect the Academy to find this interesting.*

*I had fish soup for lunch three days running. The cook here makes it well. I hope father's hands are better in the cold season. I hope your theory work is going. I hope the Seventh of Ash Street is running clean — it tends to drift in autumn, as you know.*

*P.*

Cass set it back face-down.

He stood in the kitchen with one hand flat on the table, the letter under his palm. Through the workshop wall he could hear the lathe, the pause, the pitch-gauge check, the lathe again.

He thought about what Perin had written. The specific, technical care of it — the resonance chamber's drift, the Veth broker's harmonics, the exact quality of the fish soup. Three observations that had nothing to do with each other. Or that had everything to do with each other, if you were paying attention to the right thing.

He picked the letter up again.

*The reference tone is running approximately two cents flat of true.*

*The Veth approach has some structural advantages in outdoor contract settings.*

*I hope the Seventh of Ash Street is running clean.*

Three things Perin would know Cass could verify. Three things that had specific, measurable values. The east wing chamber's drift, the Veth broker's harmonic envelope, the Seventh's autumn variance. All of them checkable. All of them things Perin could not say directly — not in a letter that left the Corda compound, not in writing that anyone could read.

He set the letter down. Face-down, as he'd found it.

He went back through the connecting door into the workshop. His father's hands were settled on the lathe, the tremor gone, as it always was in here, and the secondary bell on the frame was almost done — a clean, patient shape, a bell that would do its work for another generation if the alloy held.

"Letter came," Cass said.

"I saw it." His father's eyes stayed on the work.

"He says the east wing resonance chamber is running flat."

"Two cents. He'll have it sorted."

"Has it been two cents before?"

A pause. The lathe continued. "It drifts in humid weather."

"He says he's submitted a maintenance request."

"Then they'll see to it."

Cass watched his father's hands on the lathe. Steady. Absolutely steady, the way they never were at the dinner table.

"He says the Seventh drifts in autumn," Cass said.

"It always has." His father picked up the pitch-gauge and struck a test note.

The bell rang a clean D, true to center, and the tone spread through the workshop and touched the other bells on their frames and they answered it in their different voices, each one a slightly different shade of the same frequency. For a few seconds the workshop was full of sound going in all directions at once.

Cass closed his eyes. Counted the resonances. Let them settle.

He thought about the lame chord in the market. The way his jaw had caught the wrongness before his mind named it. The woman in the weaver's apron folding her ratification record into her coat.

He thought about Perin writing in small, careful handwriting about fish soup and humidity and iron bells.

He thought about Lenne Ashvale saying *the Codex doesn't say I'm wrong* and meaning it as an argument, and about himself saying *that's not what an argument is* and being right, and about both of them being right, in their different ways, about a gap the Codex had left open for a hundred and sixty years.

"Hand me the flux," his father said.

Cass got it from the shelf. Their fingers touched on the jar, and neither of them said anything, and the workshop held its warmth around them both, and the bells on their frames went on humming, very faintly, long after the struck note had stopped.
