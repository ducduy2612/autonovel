# Chapter Six: What a Lame Chord Sounds Like

The registry hall smelled of chalk dust and old paper and the damp that seeped from stone walls that never fully dried. The registrar who met him had a copper guild pin on her collar and the focused stillness of someone who had answered the same questions for twenty years.

"Bellwright," she said, writing it down. "First name of the contracting party?"

"Perin. P-E-R-I-N."

"Contracting with?"

"The House of Corda."

She went to the index cabinet, pulled a drawer, ran her finger along a row of cards. Pulled one. Looked at it. Looked at him.

"How old are you?"

"Fourteen."

"And your relation?"

"Brother."

She set the card on the counter between them, face down. "I'm sorry," she said, and she did sound it, a little. "The record is sealed under privacy clause 7-C. Standard for service contracts involving minors at the time of signing."

Perin had been sixteen. A minor, technically. Just barely.

"Can I request a review?"

"You can. You'd need a licensed song-broker to file the petition. The fee is forty-three copper, and the review timeline is six to eight weeks from filing."

Forty-three copper. He had eleven, in the tin box under his bed.

"Thank you," he said.

She nodded and returned the card to the drawer. The small metallic click of it closing was the cleanest sound in the room.

---

He was sitting on the worn lip of the Academy's dry fountain when Lenne found him, twenty minutes later, notebook already open, writing something in the margin with her left hand.

"Registry," she said, without looking up. "Perin's contract?"

"Sealed. Privacy clause. Forty-three copper to file a petition, six to eight weeks."

"Seven-C." She closed the notebook. "My mother filed one of those last year. Took nine weeks."

That was not helpful. He didn't say so.

She looked at him sideways. "Your interval's wrong."

He almost laughed — it came up unexpectedly and he swallowed most of it. "What does that mean."

"It means you're running through options and none of them are working and you look like someone handed you a chord that won't resolve. I've seen you in theory class when the interval's wrong. You make that face."

"I don't make a face."

"You make a face."

Across the courtyard, a second-year student was practicing a ratification progression in the south arcade — a major third, held long and clean, two notes locking together the way two hands lock in a confirmed grip, the sound the Academy used to mean *yes, agreed, I am bound to this*. Cass's ear filed it away as correctly formed and moved on.

"The sealed record blocks the petition," he said. "I need a broker to file, which costs money I don't have. And even if I had it, six weeks before anything comes back. But something's happening with the maintenance clause in Perin's contract — I think the timeline matters. I don't know why. I just think it does."

Lenne was quiet in the way she had of checking her work before showing it.

"Torvald Hess," she said.

"Who?"

"Retired dye-merchant. He runs something in the Weavers' District." A pause. "My mother mentioned him once. She was annoyed about it, which is how I remembered. He keeps tally-songs of contract disputes in the outer districts. Twenty years' worth, maybe more. She said he was thorough. She said it the way she says things she doesn't want to be true."

"Is there any chance he'd have records on Corda?"

"Corda has resonance chambers in the Weavers' District. They've been running contracts over there for years." She picked up her notebook, not to write in it, just to have something in her hands. "If anyone was keeping track of those disputes —"

"He would have been."

"That's what I'm thinking."

He looked at the dry fountain basin. Someone had dropped a copper quarter into it years ago and never retrieved it — he could see it down in the stone, green with age, dull. He wondered if it still rang at A when struck.

"Anything he has isn't legally usable," he said.

"No. But you're not trying to file a court case today. You're trying to understand what you're dealing with. Those are different problems."

That was a cleaner way of putting it than he'd managed on his own. He sat with it for a moment, then stood.

"Do you know where in the Weavers' District?"

"Near the dye-vats on the western end." She paused. "That's all I have."

---

The Weavers' District hit him before he reached it — sulfur and indigo fermentation and something he'd never found a name for. Past the Fade Line the acoustic geometry loosened; a flat note was just a flat note. He asked twice for directions and got pointed to a building with a blue-stained doorframe, the color worked into the grain from decades of dye-stained hands.

The room had been a merchant's back office once. Shelves still held sample cards and empty pigment jars. But the center had been cleared for a long table, and on the table were strips of notched wood arranged in rows, weighted at the corners with smooth river-stones. Dozens, maybe more — each about the length of his forearm, the wood worn pale from handling. The notches along the top edge were cut at different depths and angles, in a system he didn't recognize. Small circles of wax pressed into the lower margin — witness seals, but not the Academy's copper guild seals. Something rougher. He picked one up and ran his thumb along the notches.

The man behind the table was large, going soft with age, with hands stained blue-green so permanently it looked like his natural color. He was cupping his left ear toward Cass in a way that was not a gesture, just how he held his head.

"Close the door," he said.

Cass closed it.

"Bellwright boy." He said it before Cass could speak. "I told you to come find me when you had credentials. You don't have credentials yet." He gestured at the chair across the table. "Sit anyway."

Cass sat. He set the strip down, not wanting to be caught holding it.

"I heard you were asking around," Torvald said.

"I only asked two people."

"Two people here is enough." He picked up one of the strips, turned it in his blue-green hands. "What do you need."

"Records on the House of Corda. Service contracts specifically." Cass paused. "I need to understand the structure of a specific contract. The original record is sealed at the Academy."

"Whose contract."

"My brother's."

Torvald set the strip down. He looked at Cass with the kind of attention that wasn't sympathy and wasn't calculation but something between the two — a man taking an accurate measurement.

"Perin Bellwright," he said. "Signed twelve years ago."

Cass went still. "You know it."

"I know of it. Nobody outside Corda and the Academy registry has the contract itself." He shifted in his chair. "But I have tally-songs on four other Corda service contracts from the same period. And I've been watching the maintenance filing on your family's custodianship." He paused. "It went in three days ago."

"I know. We got the notice."

"Then you know they're moving." He looked at Cass steadily. "Question is what you're planning to do about it."

"I'm trying to understand what I'm dealing with first."

"Good answer." Torvald folded his hands on the table. He looked at the strip he'd set down, then back at Cass. "You were at the Corda compound last autumn — don't look surprised, you were seen — and you stood outside the gate for a while. What did you hear?"

Cass looked at him. Torvald's expression didn't change.

"The bells inside were running flat," Cass said. "Wrong for the district. A quarter-tone, maybe less. High register — the pitch range you'd use for codicil ratification. Amendment confirmation."

"Which direction flat."

"Down. They were pulling down from the ratification note."

Torvald was quiet. He looked at Cass the way Cass's father looked at a bell he was trying to diagnose without striking it — gathering information before committing to an interpretation. "Tell me what else you've heard in any Corda context. Exact pitch. Forget the Academy words for it. Just the sound."

So Cass told him about the courtroom the previous spring — a defendant's oath in G-major, coached and correct on the surface, and the thing underneath that had put a needle behind his left eye for the rest of the afternoon.

He tried to describe it. "Like someone shakes your hand but only squeezes with three fingers. You register the handshake. You register something's wrong. It holds one side without holding the other."

"Yes," Torvald said. He said it quietly, the way you say something you've been trying to find the right words for. "Yes, that's exactly it." He reached across the table and picked up a strip — not one of the ones in front of Cass, one from a separate pile he'd been keeping to his left. He turned it so Cass could see the notch-marks along the edge. "This is what that looks like in a tally-song. The Academy calls it a lame chord. I've been calling it a trap interval for twenty years, but lame chord is better. A binding that grips one party without gripping the other." He set the strip down between them and put his finger on a specific notch. "See this mark? Ratification interval. Major third — your handshake, both parties squeezing. Binding confirmed." He moved his finger to a notch further along, angled differently, cut shallower. "This is the codicil added in year two. See how the direction changes? The Academy notation would call this an ascending resolution. It should go down. It went up." He looked at Cass. "One hand squeezing. The other hand thinking it shook."

Cass looked at the two notches. The difference between them was, to him, invisible — the same depth, the same approximate angle, nothing his eye could catch. But he ran his thumb along them the way he'd done with the first strip, feeling for the difference, and there it was. The second notch was cut fractionally shallower on its right side. A lean. A lean in the wrong direction.

"And the person who signed it," Cass said. "Do they know?"

"That's the question, isn't it." Torvald took the strip back. "See, if you're trained — and I mean properly trained, not the three-week course the Academy sells to traders — you might catch it. But most people aren't, are they? They're nervous, they're being rushed, the Court Singer sounds like he knows what he's about, and you trust the form because what else are you going to trust? It's like buying dyed cloth in bad light — the color looks right, the weave feels right, you take it home and the next morning you see it's not the shade you paid for. But you already cut it." He paused. "That's what a lame chord does. You walk away bound to something you didn't agree to, wearing a color you didn't choose, and you spend years trying to work out why it sits wrong."

He rubbed his stained thumb along the strip's edge. "And that's the thing that makes it complicated — she's not wrong that the outer districts needed someone who understood contracts. Before Corda, people out here got eaten alive by the brokers. Maret built something real. She just built it on a lie."

Cass pressed his tongue to the roof of his mouth. "Come back tomorrow," Torvald said. "I'll have the records pulled. The four Corda service contracts I mentioned, and what I have on the maintenance filings. You can look at them yourself." He paused. "Can you read folk notation?"

"No."

"I'll teach you enough to follow along. You read Academy notation?"

"Yes."

"Same intervals. Different marks." He went back to sorting the tally-songs. "Go home. Get some sleep."

Cass stood. He reached for the chair back, steadied himself. "Hess."

Torvald looked up.

"The maintenance filing. The access clause. Do your records show whether Corda has used it before? The acoustician access to the bells?"

Torvald looked at him for a moment with that same measuring attention. "That's exactly the right question," he said. "Come back tomorrow and I'll tell you what I found."

He walked Cass toward the district's edge.

The Fade Line wasn't marked. You knew it because you felt the air change — sound stopped being gathered by the limestone curve and became ordinary, undirected. Cass felt it as a loosening behind his ears.

A table was set up at the intersection where cobblestones gave way to packed earth. A song-broker — not Academy-licensed; cotton sash, not copper-threaded — sat behind it with contract parchment and a tuning fork. Across from him: a family. A man with his hands flat on the table. A woman holding the younger child's wrist the way you hold something you're afraid will be taken.

The broker struck the fork and began to sing.

Cass heard the form immediately — the ratification interval, the major third, the handshake. The pitch was correct. The vowels sat in the standard positions for service-contract ratification: *yes, agreed, I accept these terms.*

The man nodded along as though he understood the structure. He didn't. Cass could see it in his eyes — tracking the broker's mouth instead of his hands, following the performance rather than the content. The woman wasn't looking at the broker at all. She was looking at the children.

Then the under-note.

It came in on the codicil — the amendment clause — threaded into the overtone the way a wire threads through wool. The surface note was clean. Underneath, the interval resolved upward. Wrong direction. The ascending resolution Torvald had shown him on the tally-strip twenty minutes ago. One hand squeezing while the other thought it shook.

The asymmetric chord gripped the family — all of them, the man and the woman and the children's futures — while the other party remained free. A lame chord, composed in real time, at a table on the Fade Line, for people who didn't know what they were hearing.

Cass started forward. Torvald's hand closed on his arm.

"You don't have credentials," Torvald said quietly. "You interrupt a binding in progress without standing and they file against your whole family. Corda has another name on a contract before sundown."

Cass stood still. The broker sang on. The man reached for the pen because the form was correct and the sound was correct and what else was he going to trust. The woman's hand tightened on the child's wrist.

Torvald watched it happen with the expression of a man who had seen this many times and would see it again — not anger, not resignation, but the thing between them that had no name because no one who felt it had ever been in a position to stop it.

"That's what it looks like," he said. "Every day."

They watched the family sign. The broker's fork rang once more — bright, completed — and the family took their copy and stood. The man shook the broker's hand. He was smiling. The woman was not.

Cass turned away. The under-note sat in his jaw like a dull ache that would stay for hours.

"Go home," Torvald said again. "Get some sleep."

---

He came back the next afternoon. Lenne was already there.

She was sitting at the near end of the table with her notebook open, and she looked up when Cass came in with an expression that was not quite apologetic. "I thought another set of eyes wouldn't hurt," she said.

Torvald, at the far end, didn't look up from the strip he was reading. "She reads folk notation," he said. "Badly, but she reads it. Sit down."

Cass sat. He looked at Lenne. She looked back.

"Your mother know you're here?" he said.

"No." She said it without hesitation, which told him she'd thought about it and decided anyway. He let it go.

The table was covered. More strips than yesterday, weighted with ledger books. The room held sound dead and close.

Torvald had already taught Cass the basics of the notation — the deep notch for ratification, the lean of the codicils indicating direction. Cass could feel the difference now: lean left for a correct resolution, lean right for a lame chord. Lenne couldn't yet, which bothered her. She'd get there.

Torvald moved to the far end of the table and began laying strips into groups. "Corda service contracts. I have four from the decade surrounding your brother's signing. Not the contracts themselves — the tally-songs from disputes that arose under those contracts. Wage disputes, mostly. One boundary complaint." He tapped a group of three strips. "This group here is interesting. The contracting party filed a codicil amendment in the second year, tried to limit their service hours. The amendment was rejected by the Corda-retained Court Singer on the grounds of improper interval." He paused. "It wasn't improper. I had three witnesses and a licensed broker from the Potters' Quarter look at the tally-song at the time. The interval was clean. The Court Singer said it wasn't."

"What happened to the contracting party?" Lenne said.

"Still in service. Ninth year now." He moved to the next group. "This one resolved. The party paid out the penalty clause and left. Took them four years to save enough." He set that group aside. "And this one —"

He stopped.

Cass looked at him. Something had changed in his face — the patient precision gone. Something harder and faster, the expression of a man who has opened a drawer and found it empty when he knows he filled it.

"What is it," Cass said.

Torvald set down the strip he was holding. He moved along the table to the far end, where the shelves were, and ran his blue-green finger along a row of notches in the shelf-edge itself — a catalogue system, Cass realized, the same notch-marks as the strips, the shelf recording what should be where. Torvald ran his finger back along it. Stopped. Ran it forward again.

"I have four Corda service contracts from that decade," he said. "Or I should." He turned around. His voice was level and his face was not. "The fourth one is gone. The strip has been pulled."

The room went quiet in a way that had nothing to do with acoustics.

"Which one," Cass said.

"The one from twelve years ago." Torvald looked at him steadily. "The one that would cover your brother's signing year."

Nobody spoke. Lenne had stopped writing. Her pen was resting against the page and her hand wasn't moving.

"Could it have been misfiled," she said.

"No." He said it without looking at her, still looking at Cass. "I know this archive. I built it. Every strip has a catalogue mark and a shelf position and I check them twice a year." He moved back to the table and sat down heavily, the chair creaking under him. "That one was there six months ago. I checked it myself."

Torvald put both hands flat on the table. Then he stood again — fast, the chair scraping stone — and went back to the shelf and ran his finger along the catalogue notches a third time, his nail catching on the wood.

"Twenty years," he said. The patient measuring tone was gone. What replaced it was the voice of a craftsman looking at damage that should never have been possible. "Twenty years of records. Every dispute, every bad codicil, every contract in this district that didn't sound right. I cut every one of those strips myself." He turned around. His face was dark. "And someone walked into my workshop and put their hands in my shelves and pulled the *one strip* that would have shown what Corda did to your brother."

He picked up the nearest river-stone and set it down hard enough to shake the table. The remaining strips shifted in their rows.

"This is my house. These are my records. Whoever did this stood where you're standing and chose which piece of twenty years' work to destroy." His hand was trembling. He pressed it flat to still it. "I want you to understand what this is. This isn't a misfiled document. This is someone reaching into my life and telling me they can take whatever they want, whenever they want, and I'll never know when it happened."

Lenne's pen was motionless on the page.

"Someone came in here," Cass said.

"And they knew what to look for." Torvald said it quiet and exact. "You don't pull one strip from forty years of records by accident. You pull it because you know it's there and you know what it shows."

Lenne looked up from her notebook. "Who has access to this room?"

"People I trust." He paused. "People I trusted." He picked up one of the remaining strips and set it down again. "The Compact has twelve members. Three of them have been here in the last six months on business. I'm not going to tell you it wasn't one of them, because I don't know that yet."

"Or someone else," Cass said. "Someone who knew the Compact existed and knew what you kept here and had a reason to be watching."

Torvald looked at him. "Yes," he said. "Or that."

Cass felt the shape of his plan — the path from Torvald's records to the Civic Court to a petition that could challenge Perin's contract — collapsing inward. Without the fourth strip, he had three contracts showing matching malformations and a gap exactly where the proof should be. He could demonstrate a pattern. He could not show the pattern touched Perin. Everything he'd built toward for two days led to a door that was no longer there.

"This changes things," he said. "I can't go to the Civic Court with three strips and an empty shelf."

"No," Torvald said. The anger was still in his hands, in the set of his jaw, but he was watching Cass with that testing attention — waiting to see whether the boy would fold or turn.

"Whoever took the strip knows I'm looking, and they're ahead of me," Cass said. "If I keep following the paper trail, they'll keep clearing it." The thought was forming as he spoke, and he let it form. "I have to come at the sound itself. Not the record of what was sung — the actual interval. If the lame chord is in Perin's contract, it's still there. You can burn a strip. You can't unring a bell."

Torvald went still. The anger didn't leave — it was in every line of him — but something else moved behind it. Recognition, maybe.

"That's a different kind of path," he said.

"It's the only one I've got."

"The three contracts you still have," Cass said. "Is the pattern in all of them?"

Torvald laid the remaining strips out in order. His finger moved along the notch-marks, and Cass watched his face. In each contract, the same thing: ratification clean, year-two codicil leaning wrong. The lame chord, three times running.

"Same Court Singer on all three." Torvald turned one of the strips so Lenne could see the witness marks. "Corda-retained. Married to a Corda cousin."

Three contracts with the same malformation in the same position. And a fourth — Perin's signing year — gone.

"If the fourth one showed the same pattern," he said.

"Then it's not an accident," Torvald said. "And someone knows it's not an accident. And they'd rather you didn't know it either."

Lenne put her pen down. She'd been writing steadily since Torvald started laying out the strips, filling her notebook page in the small fast hand she used when she was trying to get everything down before she'd decided what mattered. Now she stopped.

"Okay," she said. "So what do we do."

"We don't do anything yet," Cass said. "We don't have the fourth strip."

"We have three contracts showing the same illegal codicil construction."

"They're not legally usable. Torvald said so yesterday."

"I said they weren't usable in a court case," Torvald said. He was watching them both. "That's not the same as useless."

"It's the same as not enough," Cass said. "If we go to a Court Singer with three folk-notation tally-songs and no fourth strip and no access to the sealed registry record —"

"I'm not saying we go to a Court Singer today," Lenne said. "I'm saying we know something now that we didn't know yesterday. That's not nothing."

"It's also not Perin."

She looked at him. "I know it's not Perin."

"Then what are you suggesting we do with it? Go to the Academy? File a complaint? With what? Forty-three copper we don't have and a six-to-eight-week timeline while Corda's maintenance filing goes through and they get access to the bells?"

"I'm suggesting we don't panic."

"I'm not panicking."

"You're getting loud," Torvald said mildly.

Cass closed his mouth. He was, in fact, getting loud.

"I'm sorry," he said. To Lenne, not to the room.

"You're catastrophizing," she said. "That's different from thinking."

He looked at her. She looked back, steady and precise and annoyingly correct.

"Fine," he said. "What are you thinking?"

She picked up her pen and turned to the next blank page in her notebook. "I'm thinking there's a pattern across three contracts. Same Court Singer, same malformation, same year-two timing. That's not an accident. That's a method." She wrote something, showed it to him — not words, intervals, the Academy notation she'd been trained in, set next to the folk notation marks she'd been copying from the strips. "And if it's a method, it's been used before these four contracts and it'll be used again. Which means there are other records. Not here — Torvald's already checked, and whoever pulled the fourth strip would have looked at the others too. But the disputes that arose under those contracts — some of them would have gone to the Civic Court. Public record. Not sealed."

Cass looked at the notation on her page. "The Civic Court records are indexed by contracting party."

"Yes."

"We don't have names for the contracting parties in the three strips. The tally-songs use a witness code, not a name."

"Two of them resolved," Torvald said. "I can give you names for those. The third — the one still in service — I'll need to think about whether that's mine to share."

"I understand," Lenne said, and she said it with the particular formality she used when she was taking something seriously and wanted you to know it.

"The fourth strip," he said. "The one that's missing. When you checked it six months ago — do you remember what it showed?"

Torvald was quiet for a moment. Outside, the work song continued, unhurried.

"I remember," he said, "that I thought I should look at it more carefully. I remember thinking I'd come back to it." He set the strip down. "I didn't come back to it."

The wrongness of it settled in his chest and stayed. Someone was ahead of him, cleaning the shelves before he arrived.

"Hess," he said.

Torvald looked up.

"Is there anywhere else? Any other archive that might have covered the same contracts? Anyone in the Compact who was keeping their own records at the time?"

"One person," Torvald said. He said it slowly, like he was checking the weight of it before he put it down. "She moved to the Flood Mouth eight years ago. I haven't spoken to her since." He looked at the empty space on the shelf where the fourth strip should have been. "I'll write to her." He paused. "There's also a Court Singer — independent, not Corda-retained. Name's Sorn. He's heard things too, but he won't move without evidence."

"How long will that take?"

"Two weeks if she's prompt. Three if she's not."

Three weeks. The maintenance filing was already in. Three weeks was a long time to wait while Corda moved toward the bells.

"How long to learn to read these properly?" Cass said.

"Enough to follow along? An hour." Torvald looked at him. "Enough to argue it before a court? Years."

"I don't need a court today."

Lenne moved her chair around to Cass's side of the table without being asked, notebook open.

"Hess," Lenne said. She was looking at her notebook, not at either of them. "The lame chord in these contracts — does it hold the contracting party or the Corda party?"

Torvald looked at her. Something shifted in his expression, quick and considered. "That," he said, "is a very good question."

"Because if it holds the contracting party —"

"Then the Corda party isn't bound at all," Cass said. He heard his own voice come out flat and certain, the way it came out when he'd heard something he couldn't unhear. "The contract looks bilateral. It reads bilateral. The Court Singer confirms it as bilateral. But one hand is squeezing and the other hand is free." He set the strip down. "Perin is bound. Corda isn't."

The room was quiet.

"That's —" Lenne stopped.

"Yes," Torvald said.

"That's the whole thing," she said. "That's why it doesn't expire. A real contract has release terms that bind both parties. But if Corda isn't actually bound —"

"There's nothing to release," Cass said. "Because there's nothing holding them. The release would only apply to Perin, and the penalty clause for unilateral release —" He stopped. He already knew the answer. He'd known it, he realized, since his father had said *your brother is well, he writes*, in that specific flat voice. He'd just needed the vocabulary to name it. "The penalty clause for unilateral release absorbs the breaching party. Which is the only party who's actually bound."

Lenne was looking at him.

"So he can't leave," she said.

"He can't leave without triggering a penalty that finishes him. And Corda can walk away any time they like because the lame chord never gripped them." He exhaled. "Twelve years."

"Cass —"

"Twelve years and the whole time the contract's been —" He stopped himself. His jaw was tight. "Sorry. I know. We need to think."

"You're allowed to be angry," she said.

"I know I'm allowed." He looked at the strips on the table. At the absent space on the shelf. "Being angry doesn't get Perin out."

"No," Torvald said. "It doesn't." He looked at Cass steadily. "But understanding what you're dealing with might. And now you understand it."

Outside, the work song went on — minor key and untroubled. Cass looked at the three tally-songs laid out across the table: notched wood and wax seals, because the Academy's records cost forty-three copper and six to eight weeks.

"Teach me the rest of it," he said.

Torvald picked up a strip. Lenne's pen moved. And Cass bent over the table and began to learn a different way of reading what the intervals said.
